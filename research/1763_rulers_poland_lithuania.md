# Poland-Lithuania at 1763: Territorial Extent & Political Structure

## Bookmark Context
Start date: **11 February 1763** (moved from 1759). Augustus III (Wettin) is alive but will die **5 October 1763**—the 1764 election of Stanisław August Poniatowski has not yet occurred. The Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth is fully sovereign and intact; the first territorial partition will not occur until 1772.

---

## Territorial Extent: Full Commonwealth (Pre-Partition)

### Kingdom of Poland (Crown Lands)
The Crown comprised two major provinces plus a semi-autonomous region:

- **Greater Poland Province** (*Wielkopolska*)
- **Lesser Poland Province** (*Małopolska*), which administratively included the Right-bank Ukrainian voivodeships (Kiev and Bracław)
- **Royal Prussia Province** (semi-autonomous), including the Baltic trading cities of Gdańsk, Toruń, and Elbląg

### Grand Duchy of Lithuania
A separate federated state within the Commonwealth with its own administration, army, treasury, courts, and seal. Organized into multiple voivodeships (palatinates) including:
- Vilnius, Trakai (core lands)
- Samogitian eldership  
- Polotsk, Smolensk, Vitebsk, Minsk
- The Duchy of Livonia (*Inflanty*)

By 1763, the Grand Duchy occupied approximately 250,000 km² (declining from its 15th-century peak of 930,000 km²).

### Vassal & Dependent Territories
- **Duchy of Courland and Semigallia**: A fief of the Polish Crown; ruled in 1763–1769 by Ernst Johann von Biron (reappointed by Catherine II of Russia in 1763 after earlier exile). Courland served as a buffer but increasingly fell under Russian influence.
- **Prince-Bishopric of Warmia**: Church territory integrated into the Polish state (1466–1772)
- Towns in Spisz County (1412–1795): Border holdings with Hungary
- Duchy of Siewierz (1443–1795): Church territory

### Overall Territorial Span at 1763
The Commonwealth stretched across present-day Poland, Lithuania, Belarus, most of Ukraine, and portions of Russia—roughly 830,000 km² at this period, making it one of Europe's largest states. This vast territory would contract dramatically after the three Partitions (1772, 1793, 1795), which ultimately erased the Commonwealth from the map.

## Political Structure: Elective Monarchy & Szlachta Power

### The Monarchy
- **Elective, not hereditary**: While reigning kings' children were preferred candidates, the throne remained elective and required formal election by the szlachta (nobility). Thirteen royal elections occurred between 1573 and 1764.
- **Augustus III (Wettin)**: King of Poland and Grand Duke of Lithuania from 1733; also Elector of Saxony (as Frederick Augustus II). His reign spanned the Seven Years' War, during which the Commonwealth maintained compromised neutrality (allowing Russian transit to attack Prussia; suffering Prussian economic retaliation via currency counterfeiting).
- **Royal limits**: The king was not an autocrat. The 1505 *Act of Nihil novi* forbade the monarch from passing new laws without the consent of the nobility's representatives. The 1430 *Privilege of Jedlnia* guaranteed protections against arbitrary arrest—a proto-habeas-corpus right predating English models.

### The Szlachta (Hereditary Nobility)
An unusually large and powerful noble estate comprising 8–15% of the population (compared to ~1% in France and Italy):

- **Political rights**: 
  - Elected the monarch
  - Served in the *Sejm*, a bicameral parliament, with representatives chosen at local *sejmiks* (provincial diets)
  - Held land allodially (not feudally)—owing no feudal service to lord or king, standing as equals before the crown

- **Legal status**: 
  - Few legal distinctions between wealthy magnates and poorer nobles
  - "Golden Liberty" (*Złota Wolność*): the accumulated privileges protecting szlachta rights, creating a noble commonwealth rather than a centralized monarchy

- **Internal diversity**: 
  - Magnate families (like the Czartoryskis, Potockis, Radziwiłłs) wielded disproportionate power through vast landed wealth
  - Smaller gentry often allied with or opposed magnate factions
  - By 1763, the monarchy was weakened by magnate competition and Russian interference (Russia had steadily elevated its influence over Polish affairs)

### Administrative Federalism
The Commonwealth operated as a federation of two equal states (Poland and Lithuania) plus subordinate provinces:

- Each voivodship had its own *sejmik* (provincial diet), which exercised serious political power and elected deputies to the national *Sejm*
- The Grand Duchy of Lithuania retained its own laws, treasury, courts, ministries, army, and official seal, remaining a "separate state" within the federation despite the 1569 Union of Lublin
- Capital: Warsaw (since 1596), located in the Crown lands

### Succession Crisis (1763–1764)
Augustus III's death in October 1763 would trigger the 1764 royal election. Russia and the Czartoryski faction would back Stanisław August Poniatowski (who reigned 1764–1795), but at the Feb 1763 bookmark, this election has not yet occurred. The throne remains vacant in legal terms until the national *Sejm* assembles to elect the next monarch.

## Contrast with 1815 Partitioned Map

### 1815 (Post-Partition)
After the three Partitions:
- **Russian Empire** controlled the bulk of former Lithuanian lands and eastern Crown territories (Kingdom of Poland, a Russian puppet state from 1815–1830)
- **Prussia** held Royal Prussia and western Polish lands (→ Grand Duchy of Warsaw 1807–1815, then back to Prussia)
- **Austrian Empire** held Galicia and other southern Crown territories

### 1763 (Full Commonwealth)
- Single, federative, sovereign state stretching from the Baltic to the Black Sea region
- No external partition; internal governance shared between Crown and Lithuanian institutions
- Dynastic union with Saxony (through Augustus III) and vassal Courland both still intact

## Game Implementation Notes

- **Ruler**: Augustus III (Wettin); alive at start; historical death 5 Oct 1763
- **Government**: Elective monarchy with szlachta-dominated Sejm and federated Grand Duchy
- **Territorial footprint**: Pre-Partition Commonwealth (all provinces and Lithuanian lands intact)
- **Vassals**: Courland as a fief; Warmia as an ecclesiastical territory
- **Political weakness**: Magnate factionalism + Seven Years' War neutrality costs + nascent Russian dominance (will culminate in 1772 partition) suggest vulnerability to great-power pressure
