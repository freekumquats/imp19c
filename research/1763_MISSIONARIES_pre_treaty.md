# Christian Missionary Presence in China Before the Unequal Treaties (c.1580s–1860)

**Purpose**: To correct the game mechanic that gates Christian missions to post-1858/60 only. Reality: missions were present for ~280 years before the treaties, but at LOW social tension and NEAR-ZERO political tension (no foreign state backing, no extraterritoriality). The treaty system transformed missions from a tolerated/underground cultural phenomenon into an acute political crisis.

**Focus date**: 1763 (High Qing, Qianlong 乾隆, 28th regnal year)

---

## 1. THE JESUIT COURT MISSION (c.1601–1773)

### Foundation and Golden Age (1601–1705)

Matteo Ricci 利瑪竇 reached Beijing in 1601, inaugurating the sustained Jesuit presence at the Ming court. The Jesuit strategy — "accommodation" 適應政策 — embraced Confucian learning, classical Chinese literacy, and court service in technical capacities rather than mass proselytizing (Spence, *The Memory Palace of Matteo Ricci*, 1984; Brockey, *Journey to the East*, 2007). Ricci and his successors (Johann Adam Schall von Bell 湯若望, Ferdinand Verbiest 南懷仁) gained imperial favor by reforming the calendar, improving astronomical prediction (critical for ritual legitimacy), and staffing the Imperial Board of Astronomy (欽天監). 

The **1692 Edict of Toleration** 容教令, issued by the Kangxi Emperor 康熙帝, formally legalized Christian worship throughout the empire and permitted mission activity (Mungello, *The Great Encounter*, 2005, pp. 57–60). This marked the high-water mark of Jesuit influence: by the early 18th century, estimated Catholic converts numbered 200,000–300,000 (Standaert, ed., *Handbook of Christianity in China vol. 1*, 2001, p. 380), and Jesuits enjoyed privileged access to the court, serving as mathematicians, cartographers, painters (Giuseppe Castiglione 郎世寧 arrived 1715), and clockmakers.

### The Rites Controversy and Proscription (1705–1724)

The **Chinese Rites Controversy** 禮儀之爭 shattered this equilibrium (Mungello, *The Chinese Rites Controversy*, 1994). The Jesuits had permitted converts to continue ancestral rites and Confucian ceremonies, arguing these were civil rather than religious acts. Rival orders (Dominicans, Franciscans) denounced this as idolatry. Pope Clement XI sent Charles-Thomas Maillard de Tournon as legate in 1705; Tournon's 1707 decree condemned the Jesuit position. The Kangxi Emperor, furious at papal interference in Chinese ritual interpretation, began restricting missionaries. Papal legate Carlo Ambrogio Mezzabarba's 1720 visit failed to repair the breach.

The **1724 Yongzheng proscription** 雍正禁教, issued by the Yongzheng Emperor 雍正帝, officially banned Christianity in the provinces and ordered missionaries expelled (Standaert, vol. 1, pp. 382–383). Crucially, **Jesuit court astronomers and artists were exempted** and retained at Beijing, distinguishing the imperial science mission from provincial evangelism. Provincial missionaries were forced underground or into neighboring territories (Macao, Manila), though many remained covertly.

### Qianlong Era and Jesuit Suppression (1736–1773 and beyond)

The Qianlong Emperor 乾隆帝 (r. 1735–1796) maintained this bifurcated policy. **In 1763** — the focus date — **Jesuits still served at the Qing court**, notably Castiglione (郎世寧, d. 1766), Michel Benoist 蔣友仁 (hydraulic engineer, designer of the European Palaces 西洋樓 at Yuanmingyuan 圓明園), and others in the Astronomical Bureau. Christianity remained **officially proscribed in the provinces**, but enforcement was inconsistent: periodic persecutions punctuated long stretches of de facto toleration, especially in areas with established Catholic communities (Fujian, Sichuan, Jiangnan).

The **1773 papal suppression of the Society of Jesus** by Pope Clement XIV dealt a severe organizational blow (Brockey, pp. 411–420). The Jesuit mission infrastructure collapsed globally. In China, the Vincentians (Lazarists) 遣使會 gradually assumed the Beijing court-science posts in the 1780s–1790s, but the intellectual and institutional capital of the Jesuit era was irreplaceable. By the late 18th century, the court mission had dwindled to a handful of clerics maintaining the calendar and repairing clocks; the golden age was over.

### Mechanic Implication (1763 + Pre-Treaty)

**MECHANIC IMPLICATION (1763 + pre-treaty)**: Christian missions were INSTITUTIONALLY PRESENT at the Qing court in 1763, but in a **purely technical/scientific capacity** with near-zero evangelistic or political tension. Provincially, missions existed underground or semi-tolerated with **low-level social friction** (periodic local persecutions, literati disdain for heterodoxy) but **minimal political/diplomatic stakes** — the Qing could (and did) imprison or execute missionaries without foreign state retaliation. Model this as: missions present, **social tension = LOW (2/10)**, **political tension = NEAR-ZERO (0.5/10)**, no foreign protectorate, no extraterritoriality. The court-science layer is a distinct mechanic (palace buildings, prestige, minor tech bonuses) that should be ACTIVE in 1763 and uncontroversial.

## 2. THE PROVINCIAL / UNDERGROUND MISSIONS (17th–18th c.)

### Geographic Spread and Organizational Diversity

Beyond the Beijing court, Catholic missions operated across the provinces from the early 17th century onward. The **Jesuits** concentrated in Jiangnan 江南 (Jiangsu, Zhejiang, Anhui), Fujian, and Sichuan; the **Dominicans** 多明我會 in Fujian (especially Fuzhou 福州 and Funing 福寧); the **Franciscans** 方濟各會 in Shandong, Shanxi, and Shaanxi; and the **Missions Étrangères de Paris (MEP)** 巴黎外方傳教會 in Sichuan, Yunnan, and later Guangdong (Standaert, vol. 1, pp. 305–340; Tiedemann, ed., *Handbook of Christianity in China vol. 2*, 2010, pp. 5–30). 

These missions established **native Christian communities** 本地教友團體 that persisted across generations. By the early 18th century, estimated Catholic converts numbered **200,000–300,000** (Standaert, vol. 1, p. 380; Mungello, *Great Encounter*, p. 58). After the 1724 proscription, these communities often **sustained themselves** through **native catechists** 傳道員 and lay leaders who conducted services, baptisms, and instruction during long intervals when foreign priests could not visit or had gone underground (顧衛民, 《基督教與近代中國社會》, 2006, pp. 45–52).

### Pattern of Persecution and Toleration

Provincial enforcement of the 1724 ban was **inconsistent and episodic**. Local officials often tolerated Christian communities unless provoked by gentry complaints, heterodoxy scares (邪教 panic), or factional disputes (張力/劉鑒唐, 《中國教案史》, 1987, vol. 1, pp. 80–95). Major persecution waves occurred in **1746–1748 (Fujian)**, when several missionaries were executed or died in prison (including five martyrs beatified in 1900; Tiedemann, vol. 2, pp. 35–38), and sporadically in Sichuan and Jiangxi. Between crackdowns, missions resumed quietly, often with tacit local tolerance. 

The **隱蔽傳教** (covert evangelism) pattern: missionaries disguised as merchants or tutors, meeting converts in private homes, burying Bibles and liturgical texts during raids. This low-intensity, high-resilience mode contrasts sharply with the later treaty-port era's open, state-protected missions.

### Russian Orthodox Mission

A distinct strand: the **Russian Orthodox Ecclesiastical Mission** 俄羅斯東正教傳道團 in Beijing, formalized under the 1727 Treaty of Kyakhta (following the 1689 Treaty of Nerchinsk framework). This mission served Russian nationals (descendants of Cossack prisoners from the Albazin conflicts) and conducted limited outreach to Mongols and Chinese. It remained **tiny** (rarely more than a dozen clergy and students) and **state-to-state diplomatic** in character rather than evangelical (Mungello, pp. 74–75). In 1763 it was an institutional curiosity, politically inert.

### Mechanic Implication (1763 + Pre-Treaty)

**MECHANIC IMPLICATION (1763 + pre-treaty)**: Provincial Christian communities of 200,000–300,000 believers existed across Jiangnan, Fujian, Sichuan, Shandong, etc., sustained by underground/semi-tolerated missions and native catechists. **Social tension = LOW to MODERATE (3/10)** — periodic local 教案 incidents, gentry hostility to heterodoxy, occasional martyrdoms, but no systematic imperial campaign. **Political tension = NEAR-ZERO (0.5/10)** — persecuted missionaries had no foreign state backing; executions/expulsions carried no international consequence (contrast post-1860 indemnities and gunboats). Model this as: missions present in provinces via "hidden mission station" mechanic (small pop modifier, periodic risk of local crackdown event, NO diplomatic incident). The Russian Orthodox mission is a separate, trivial flavor object (Beijing only, no gameplay impact).

## 3. TENSION LEVELS: Social vs Political, Pre-Treaty vs Post-Treaty (THE CRUX)

### Pre-Treaty Era (c.1724–1860): Low-Intensity, Locally Contained

Anti-Christian sentiment existed throughout the 18th and early 19th centuries, but it was **qualitatively and quantitatively different** from the post-treaty crisis.

**Social Tension (LOW, ~2–4/10):**
- **Cultural/ideological friction**: Literati 士大夫 and gentry denounced Christianity as **heterodoxy** 邪教, grouping it with White Lotus 白蓮教, Eight Trigrams 八卦教, and other suspect sects. The critique: Christianity undermined filial piety (rejection of ancestral rites), Confucian social order, and imperial ritual legitimacy (Mungello, *Chinese Rites Controversy*; Paul Cohen, *China and Christianity*, 1963, pp. 3–25).
- **Local 教案 incidents**: Periodic anti-Christian riots, denunciations, arrests. Examples: the **1746–1748 Fujian persecution** (multiple missionaries executed; Tiedemann, vol. 2, pp. 35–38); sporadic Sichuan and Jiangxi crackdowns. These were **episodic and localized**, often triggered by gentry complaints or heterodoxy scares, not systematic imperial policy.
- **Pattern**: Long stretches of **de facto toleration** punctuated by short, sharp crackdowns. Christian communities survived underground or with tacit local acceptance. No mass mobilization or sustained anti-Christian movement (contrast the Boxer milieu).

**Political Tension (NEAR-ZERO, ~0.5/10):**
- **No foreign state backing**: Missionaries were private individuals (or operated under religious orders). If arrested, tortured, or executed, there was **no diplomatic recourse** and **no gunboat retaliation**. The 1748 Fujian martyrdoms provoked no international incident. The Qing state could enforce the 1724 proscription (or ignore it) at will, with zero external constraint.
- **No extraterritoriality**: Missionaries subject to Qing law. No treaty ports, no consular protection, no indemnities for 教案.
- **No land/property rights**: Missions operated covertly, in rented rooms or private homes. No permanent church buildings, no mission compounds as post-treaty enclaves.

**Key point**: Pre-treaty Christianity was a **cultural irritant**, not a **political crisis**. The state could suppress it at low cost, and did so intermittently, but also tolerated it when convenient. **Total diplomatic/political stakes ≈ zero**.

### Post-Treaty Era (1860–1900): High-Intensity, Diplomatically Explosive

The **1858 Treaty of Tianjin** 天津條約 (ratified 1860) and **1860 Convention of Beijing** 北京條約 transformed missions into a **core political problem**:

**Treaty Provisions:**
1. **Inland travel rights** 內地傳教權: Missionaries could travel and proselytize anywhere in the empire, not just treaty ports (Article 8, French Treaty of Tianjin; Tiedemann, vol. 2, pp. 99–102).
2. **French Protectorate** 法國保教權: France assumed formal protection of all Catholic missions (and later informally of Protestant missions via British/American pressure). Any 教案 became a **diplomatic incident** involving foreign consuls and potential gunboat diplomacy.
3. **Extraterritoriality** 治外法權: Missionaries (and often Chinese converts under mission protection) exempt from Qing law, subject to consular courts. This created a **jurisdictional nightmare** and fueled gentry resentment.
4. **Property rights**: Missions could purchase land, build churches and schools, establish permanent compounds. The **1865 Berthemy Convention** added a clause allowing missions to reclaim property confiscated during earlier persecutions, leading to massive land disputes (張力/劉鑒唐, vol. 2, pp. 120–135).

**Social Tension (HIGH, ~7–9/10):**
- Missions expanded **aggressively**: by 1900, ~750,000 Catholics and ~100,000 Protestants (Tiedemann, vol. 2, p. 150). Mission compounds became **enclaves** with schools, hospitals, orphanages — and legal immunity.
- **"Rice Christians"** 吃教 phenomenon: Converts sought mission protection in legal disputes, tax evasion, landlord conflicts. Missions became **parallel power structures**, undermining local gentry authority (Cohen, pp. 26–55).
- **Anti-Christian riots** 教案 escalated: **Tianjin Massacre 天津教案 (1870)**: rumors of orphanage kidnappings (children dying in cholera epidemic) led to mob killing French consul, missionaries, and Chinese converts; France extracted indemnities and executions (張力/劉鑒唐, vol. 2, pp. 180–210). Hundreds of major 教案 in the 1860s–1890s.
- **Boxer Uprising 義和團 (1899–1901)**: anti-Christian, anti-foreign mass movement, slogan "扶清滅洋" (support Qing, exterminate foreigners); killed ~230 missionaries and ~30,000 Chinese Christians (Tiedemann, vol. 2, pp. 450–480; Paul Cohen, *History in Three Keys*, 1997).

**Political Tension (ACUTE, ~9/10):**
- Every 教案 triggered **diplomatic crisis**: foreign consuls demanded indemnities, punishment of officials, garrison rights. The Qing state lost sovereignty over domestic religious affairs.
- **Military enforcement**: Gunboats backed mission demands. The Tianjin Massacre indemnity included execution of 20 Chinese, degradation of officials, and a mission to France with apologies (Tiedemann, vol. 2, pp. 205–210).
- **Sovereignty erosion**: Missions became symbols of foreign imperialism. The 1900 Boxer Protocol indemnity (450 million taels, largest in Chinese history) directly linked to anti-Christian violence (Cohen, *History in Three Keys*, pp. 281–290).

### Quantified Contrast (for Mechanic Calibration)

| Dimension | Pre-Treaty (1724–1860) | Post-Treaty (1860–1900) |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------------------|
| **Mission scale** | ~200,000–300,000 converts, covert/semi-tolerated | ~850,000 converts, open mission compounds |
| **Social tension** | **2–4/10**: episodic local 教案, literati disdain, no mass movement | **7–9/10**: aggressive expansion, "rice Christians," endemic 教案, Boxer mobilization |
| **Political tension** | **0.5/10**: zero foreign backing, zero diplomatic consequence | **9/10**: every 教案 = diplomatic crisis, indemnities, gunboats, sovereignty loss |
| **State response** | Episodic suppression OR toleration, at will, low cost | Forced toleration under treaty, high cost to suppress (foreign retaliation) |
| **Mechanic gate** | Missions PRESENT, low-intensity, no gameplay crisis | Missions crisis-generator, major diplomatic/domestic stability penalty |

### Mechanic Implication (Pre-Treaty vs Post-Treaty)

**MECHANIC IMPLICATION (1763 + pre-treaty)**: Missions must be PRESENT but operate in a **low-intensity mode** — small pop modifiers (conversion trickle), periodic **local crackdown event** (random province, minor unrest, player can choose suppress/tolerate, NO diplomatic incident, NO foreign relation penalty). **Social tension = LOW (2–4/10)**, **political tension = NEAR-ZERO (0.5/10)**.

**Post-1860 transition**: Flip to **high-intensity mode** — mission compounds (buildings), aggressive conversion (larger pop modifiers), frequent **教案 diplomatic incidents** (player must pay indemnity OR face foreign relation penalty + potential war), **anti-Christian unrest modifier** rises over time, culminating in **Boxer crisis event chain** (1899–1901, massive stability hit, potential foreign intervention). **Social tension = HIGH (7–9/10)**, **political tension = ACUTE (9/10)**.

**Key toggle date: 1860** (Treaty of Beijing ratification). Pre-1860 = present but quiet. Post-1860 = present and explosive. The current mechanic that gates missions to post-1858/60 ONLY is **historically wrong** by ~280 years; correcting it requires the two-mode framework.

### Mechanic Implication: Gating and Ramping

**MECHANIC IMPLICATION (timeline gates):**

1. **1763 (or 1815 start)**: Missions PRESENT, **social 2/10**, **political 0.5/10**. Mechanic = hidden mission stations (small convert pop trickle), rare "local anti-Christian incident" event (player chooses suppress/tolerate, no diplomatic consequence).

2. **1844 gate**: Treaty of Whampoa. **Social ramps to 4/10**, **political ramps to 2/10**. Mechanic = mission stations visible at treaty ports, consular backing (教案 at ports generates minor foreign relation penalty).

3. **1860 gate** (Treaty of Beijing): **THE KEY INFLECTION**. **Social ramps to 7/10**, **political jumps to 8/10**. Mechanic = mission compounds (buildings) proliferate inland, frequent "教案 diplomatic incident" event chain (player must pay indemnity OR face relation penalty + war-threat), new modifier "anti-Christian unrest" (scales with mission expansion), Qing sovereignty penalty.

4. **1870 event** (Tianjin Massacre): **Social spike 9/10**, **political spike 9/10**. Scripted crisis event (if player is Qing), massive indemnity + stability hit.

5. **1899–1901 gate** (Boxer crisis window): **Social ramps to 9–10/10**, **political 9–10/10**. Scripted event chain: Boxer movement emerges (if anti-Christian unrest > threshold), player chooses back Boxers (temporary unrest relief, then foreign invasion) OR suppress Boxers (domestic instability, no invasion). Outcome determines indemnity scale and Qing survival.

**Key dates for code**: 
- **1860.10.24** (Convention of Beijing ratification) = flip from low-intensity to high-intensity mission mode.
- **1870.6.21** (Tianjin Massacre) = scripted spike event.
- **1900.6.20** (Boxer siege of legations begins) = Boxer crisis chain start.

The current mechanic that gates missions to post-1858 is **280 years too late**. The corrected mechanic activates missions at game start (1815 or 1763) in low-intensity mode, then ramps through three stages (1844 treaty-port, 1860 inland, 1899–1901 Boxer) to the crisis the user's existing code already models.

## Summary for Mechanic Re-Gating

**Historical correction**: Christian missions were present in China for ~280 years BEFORE the 1858/60 treaties, but in a fundamentally different mode:

- **Pre-1860**: Missions present (Jesuit court scientists, underground provincial evangelists, 200,000–300,000 converts), **social tension LOW (2–4/10)**, **political tension NEAR-ZERO (0.5/10)** — no foreign backing, no extraterritoriality, no diplomatic incidents.

- **Post-1860**: Missions expand aggressively under treaty protection (inland rights, French protectorate, extraterritoriality), **social tension HIGH (7–9/10)**, **political tension ACUTE (8–10/10)** — every 教案 becomes a diplomatic crisis backed by gunboats, culminating in the Boxer catastrophe (1899–1901).

**Mechanic fix**: Activate missions at game start (1815 or 1763) in **low-intensity mode** (hidden stations, small convert trickle, rare local incidents with NO diplomatic penalty). Ramp through **three gates**:
1. **1844** (treaty-port toleration): social 4/10, political 2/10.
2. **1860** (inland rights + protectorate): social 7/10, political 8/10, frequent 教案 crises.
3. **1899–1901** (Boxer crisis): social 9–10/10, political 9–10/10, existential Qing crisis.

This corrects the ahistorical post-1858-only gate while preserving the existing high-intensity crisis mechanics for the treaty era.
