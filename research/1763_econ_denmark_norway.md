# 1763 Denmark-Norway Economy: Timber, Fish, Sound Dues, and Trade

**Ruler:** King Frederick V of Denmark (reigned 1746–1766)

## Overview

Denmark–Norway in 1763 was a two-part economic union with **complementary sectoral specialization**:
- **Denmark**: Agricultural economy (subsistence farming, noble landholdings); domestic food production
- **Norway**: Industrialized, export-driven economy (timber, mining, shipping, fishing); supplied Denmark with raw materials and semifinished goods

The union relied on internal trade: Denmark supplied agricultural goods and farm products; Norway supplied timber, metals, and shipping services. Norwegian industry enjoyed a legal monopoly in Denmark. However, up to two-thirds of Norway's audited national income was transferred to Copenhagen, making Norway a colonial-style revenue producer for the Danish Crown.

## Norwegian Primary Sectors

### 1. Timber and Lumber (Primary Export)

**Scale and importance:**
- Timber was one of Norway's **central exports** and had become a major industry by the 17th century
- Exports went "especially to England" and to foreign navies requiring naval timber
- By the mid-18th century (including 1763), a small number of merchants controlled the entire lumber industry under Danish royal monopoly oversight
- A royal decree in 1688 had restricted sawmill operations to prevent deforestation, limiting industrial capacity

**Geographic centers:**
- Concentrated in southern and eastern Norway's river valleys and coastal regions (Glomma, Lægen, and other major river systems)
- Shipping ports: Oslo (Christiania), Frederikstad, Moss, Sandefjord on the eastern coast; Bergen on the western coast

**Mechanism:**
- Timber was floated down rivers to coastal towns, then exported via merchant vessels
- Danish merchants dominated the licensing and export trade, taking a share of profits

### 2. Fishing and Preserved Fish Products

**Stockfish (dried unsalted cod):**
- **"Norway's longest sustained export commodity"** (13th century through modern era)
- Unsalted fish (especially cod) dried by cold air and wind on wooden racks ("hjell" in Norwegian)
- ~80% water removed through fermentation with cold-adapted bacteria, giving storage life of several years
- Production occurred February–May during cod season, with maturation indoors for additional months

**Geography and ports:**
- Produced along entire Norwegian coast; **northern Norway was ideal** due to cool climate and cod abundance
- Major fishing/drying centers: Lofoten Islands (north), Bergen (west), Stavanger (southwest), southern coastal towns
- Stockfish was shipped to Mediterranean Catholic countries (notably Italy as *stoccafisso*), Baltic markets, and England

**Salted cod (klippfisk) - emerging trade:**
- From the 18th century, salted/dried cod began being commercially produced, requiring fishermen to purchase salt from Danish merchants
- This increased Danish merchant control over the fishery

**Economic impact:**
- Fishing provided essential income along the Norwegian coast; shipping infrastructure focused on exporting fish products
- Danish and Norwegian merchants competed for export licenses and profit shares

### 3. Mining and Metals

**Major mines and production:**
- **Kongsberg Silver Mines**: Major silver production; required royal licenses and contributed significant revenue
- **Røros Copper Mines**: Significant copper production center in central Norway
- These mines operated under Danish royal monopoly charters

**Trade:**
- Metals exported primarily to Denmark and to foreign markets via Danish merchant networks

### 4. Shipping and Maritime Trade

**Norwegian neutral-power advantage (1690–1763):**
- During Europe's frequent 18th-century wars, Norway–Denmark maintained relatively neutral status, allowing Norwegian merchant vessels to capture neutral shipping market share
- Norwegian ships supplied foreign navies with timber and other goods
- Many Norwegians worked as sailors on foreign (especially Dutch) vessels, generating income remittances

**Recovery after Great Northern War (1709–1721):**
- Danish entry into the Great Northern War (1709) disrupted shipping; Norwegian maritime advantage declined
- Shipping recovered toward end of 18th century as European peace enabled trade expansion

## Danish Economy and the Sound Dues (Øresundstolden)

### Sound Dues Revenue

**Historical importance:**
- The Sound Dues **constituted up to two-thirds of Denmark's state income in the 16th and 17th centuries**
- By the 18th century (including 1763), still a significant revenue stream, though agricultural reforms and mercantile policies were diversifying the economy

**Mechanics:**
- All foreign ships crossing the Øresund strait (between Denmark and Sweden) had to halt at **Helsingør** (Elsinore) and pay the Danish Crown
- In 1567, the toll shifted from flat-rate to **1–2% tax on cargo value**, tripling revenue
- To prevent captains from underreporting cargo value, the Crown reserved the right to purchase any cargo at the declared value
- Enforcement: Danish cannons at Helsingør and Swedish cannons at Helsingborg (Helsingborg) discouraged non-compliance

**Traffic volume:**
- Historical ship counts show growth: 795 ships (1479) → 2,731 (1560) → 4,262 (1581)
- By the 18th century, traffic was substantial and included all Baltic trade, Mediterranean grain shipments, and Northern European commerce

**Trade affected by the toll:**
- All commerce into and out of the Baltic Sea (Russian furs, grain, naval stores, timber from the Baltic region, colonial goods from the Atlantic)
- This made the dues a point of friction with trading nations, especially Sweden (whose exemption status shifted with various treaties: Kalmar Union, Treaty of Brömsebro 1645, Treaty of Frederiksborg 1720)

### Toll collection infrastructure

**Ports and locations:**
- **Helsingør** (Denmark): Primary toll collection point on the eastern sound
- **Helsingborg** (Sweden): Secondary enforcement point on the western sound
- **Great Belt** and **Little Belt**: Secondary toll points to prevent ship diversion

## Danish Mercantilist Policy (18th Century Context)

- Mercantilism was the leading economic doctrine in Denmark during the 17th–18th centuries
- This led to:
  - Monopolies granted to favored merchants and trading companies (e.g., Asiatisk Kompagni for Asian trade)
  - Infrastructure development (ports, roads, canals)
  - Territorial acquisition (colonies in Tranquebar and the West Indies for slave-trade and sugar production)
  - Legal monopolies for Norwegian industry within Denmark

**Agricultural base:**
- 80–85% of the Danish population lived in small villages on a subsistence level
- Large noble landowners dominated agricultural production
- Late-18th-century agricultural reforms ("Agrarian Revolution") introduced crop rotation and land consolidation

## Trade Ports and Regional Centers (1763)

### Denmark
- **Copenhagen**: Capital, royal seat, primary administrative and merchant hub; customs and Sound Dues headquarters
- **Helsingør** (Elsinore): Sound Dues toll collection point; significant merchant activity
- **Aarhus**: Regional port on Jutland
- **Aalborg**: Northern Jutland port

### Norway
- **Oslo** (Christiania): Eastern coast, primary capital and merchant center; timber export hub
- **Frederikstad**: Eastern coast, timber and shipping port
- **Moss**: Eastern coast, smaller timber port
- **Bergen**: Western coast, primary port for western Norway; fish (stockfish and salted cod) exports; primary hub for North Sea and Atlantic trade
- **Stavanger**: Southwestern coast, fishing and preserved fish exports
- **Trondheim**: Central western coast, regional administrative and trade hub
- **Lofoten Islands region**: Arctic north, major cod fishing grounds and stockfish production

## Summary: 1763 Economic Profile

**Denmark–Norway in 1763 was characterized by:**

1. **Sectoral specialization**: Norway as the industrial/export engine (timber, metals, fish, shipping) dependent on Denmark for food; Denmark as the agricultural base supplying the union and collecting rents from Norwegian exports via monopoly control

2. **Norwegian exports** (in order of importance):
   - Timber (to England, foreign navies, and southern Europe)
   - Stockfish and salted cod (to Mediterranean, Baltic, and Atlantic markets)
   - Metal goods (silver and copper via royal monopoly)
   - Shipping services and neutral-flag merchant capacity during wartime

3. **Danish revenues**:
   - Sound Dues (still substantial; 1–2% of Baltic trade value)
   - Agricultural surplus and exports
   - Rents from Norwegian industry monopolies and taxed exports
   - Colonial trade (sugar, spices, slave trade)

4. **Key ports**:
   - **Copenhagen** (Denmark): Administrative/customs hub
   - **Helsingør** (Denmark): Sound Dues enforcement
   - **Oslo, Frederikstad** (Norway): Timber exports
   - **Bergen** (Norway): Fish, Western trade hub
   - **Lofoten Islands** (Norway): Cod production

This economic structure persisted with only modest change through the 1760s–1780s until the Napoleonic Wars disrupted Danish-Norwegian trade patterns.
