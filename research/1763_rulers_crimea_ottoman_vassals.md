# Crimean Khanate and Ottoman Balkan Vassals (1763)

**Research Date**: 2026-07-08  
**Start Date Context**: 1763.2.11  
**Ottoman Sultan**: Mustafa III (r. 1757–1774)

---

## Crimean Khanate as Ottoman Vassal

### Reigning Khan (1763)

**Qırım Giray** (first reign: 1758–1764)
- In power during February 1763
- Appointed by Ottoman Sultan Mustafa III from the Giray dynasty
- Second reign would follow later (1768–1769)

### Nature of Ottoman Suzerainty over Crimea (c. 1763)

**Status**: *De jure* Ottoman vassal (1475/1478–1774), but with exceptional autonomy

**Scholarly Consensus**:
- **Russian historiography**: Emphasizes vassalage throughout the Khanate's existence
- **Turkish/Crimean Tatar sources**: Stress practical independence
- **Kolodziejczyk (leading scholar)**: "Privileged vassal status" — a nuanced middle ground between vassalage and independence
- Some scholars (e.g., French historian D'Abbeville): Characterized as "confederation" rather than vassalage

### Khan Appointment and Removal Process (by 1763)

**Evolution of Ottoman Control**:

1. **Pre-1524**: Khans from Giray dynasty elected by Crimean nobility (beys/mirzas), with Ottoman approval/recognition required

2. **1524 crisis**: During Gazi Giray's reign, Ottoman control began to increase

3. **Post-1584**: After deposition of Khan Mehmed II Geray (1584), Ottoman Sultan gained direct appointment/removal power ("обычно осуществлялось по воле Порты")

**By 1763**: 
- Ottoman Sultan had **full appointment and removal authority**
- Sultan selected khans exclusively from the Giray dynasty
- Internal Crimean politics (powerful bey families) still influenced which Giray the Sultan would choose
- Khans required formal investiture (berat) from the Sultan

### Military Obligations

**Primary Duty**: Provide light cavalry for Ottoman campaigns

**Characteristics**:
- Crimean Tatar cavalry considered **"indispensable" (vazgeçilemez)** for Ottoman military operations
- Deployed in European campaigns against Russia, Poland-Lithuania, Hungary, Austria, and Persia
- Famous for reconnaissance, raiding, and harassment tactics
- Large contingents mobilized: tens of thousands of cavalry for major campaigns

**Types of Service**:
1. **Mandatory campaign participation**: Major Ottoman military operations
2. **Independent slave raids**: Conducted into Eastern Europe (Ukraine, Poland, Russia) that served Ottoman strategic interests
3. **Border defense**: Against Russian expansion

**Historical Examples**:
- 1571: Crimean Khan Devlet I Giray burned Moscow under Ottoman direction
- Regular participation in 18th-century Ottoman-Russian wars

### Tribute Requirements

**Unusual Arrangement** (contrary to typical vassalage):
- **NO regular tribute paid to Ottomans** (Turkish sources emphasize: "vergi ödememesi")
- **Reverse financial flow**: Ottomans paid subsidies and gifts to the Khanate
- Payments to Crimea often came after successful joint military campaigns

**Economic Relationship**:
- Based on **military service exchange** rather than fiscal extraction
- Ottomans valued Crimean military contribution more than tribute revenue
- Trade privileges and diplomatic gifts replaced formal tribute obligations

### Level of Autonomy

**High Internal Autonomy**:
1. **Internal governance**: Full control over domestic administration, justice, taxation of subjects
2. **Religious affairs**: Independent; Khan held title of "Caliph of the North"
3. **Economic policy**: Largely autonomous; controlled Black Sea slave trade
4. **Military policy**: Mixed — independent raids allowed, major campaigns coordinated with Ottomans

**Constraints on Autonomy**:
1. **Foreign policy**: Limited — required Ottoman coordination for major diplomatic initiatives
2. **Peace/war with Russia**: Could not make treaties independently without Ottoman consent
3. **Ottoman military presence**: Ottoman garrisons in some coastal fortresses (e.g., Caffa/Kefe)
4. **Ottoman diplomatic oversight**: Ottoman representative (Kapi Ağası) stationed at Khan's court
5. **Succession**: Ottoman Sultan had final approval and removal power (by 1763)

## Danubian Principalities as Ottoman Vassals

### Moldavia

#### Reigning Hospodar (1763)

**Grigore Callimachi** (first reign: 1761–1764; second reign: 1767–1769)
- From the Callimachi family
- Appointed by Ottoman Sultan
- In power during February 1763

#### Ottoman Suzerainty Structure (Phanariote Period)

**Phanariote System** (inaugurated after 1711):
- Following Prince Dimitrie Cantemir's alliance with Peter the Great (defeated at Stănilești, 1710–1711), Sultan Ahmed III abandoned recognition of local princely elections
- New system based "solely on Ottoman approval"
- Hospodars appointed from **Phanariote Greek families** of Constantinople

**Appointment Process**:
- Candidates typically served as **chief dragoman of the Porte** (skilled in Ottoman statecraft and contemporary politics)
- Positions **purchased through bribery** — rulers obtained office "in exchange for a generous bribe"
- Hospodars often arrived not speaking Romanian, accompanied by retinues of family, favorites, and creditors
- Over the Phanariote epoch, **31 princes from 11 families** ruled the two principalities
- When choices narrowed, rulers were shuffled between Moldavia and Wallachia

**Tribute Requirements**:
- Moldavia paid tribute directly to the Porte
- Tribute levels were "directly proportional with Ottoman requests"
- After 1774 Treaty of Küçük Kaynarca, tribute levels declined significantly (to ~3,100 gold coins by the 1820s)
- Hospodars' debts were primarily owed to creditors (who financed their bribes) rather than the Sultan

**Level of Autonomy**:
- Moldavia remained "relatively rich and—more importantly—**autonomous**"
- Retained internal self-government: own princes, courts, boyar councils
- Full control over domestic administration and justice
- Principalities were vassal states, not directly administered Ottoman provinces
- Ottoman central institutions aimed to "maintain their rule over the principalities and not exploit them irrationally"

**Constraints**:
- Princely appointments entirely dependent on Ottoman Sultan's decision
- Could not conduct independent foreign policy
- Required to participate in Ottoman military campaigns when requested
- Russian intervention rights established after 1774 Treaty

### Wallachia

#### Reigning Hospodar (1763)

**Constantin Mavrocordat** (one of several reigns: 1761–1763)
- From the Mavrocordato family
- Most famous Phanariote hospodar
- Previously abolished serfdom in Wallachia (1746)
- His reign ending in 1763, so he was ruler during February 1763

#### Ottoman Suzerainty Structure (Phanariote Period)

**Phanariote System** (established 1715):
- After deposing Prince Ștefan Cantacuzino (1716), Ottomans abandoned elective system
- Princes "appointed from the Phanariotes of Constantinople"
- Coincided with decline of Boyar Divan's influence relative to Sultan's decisions

**Appointment Process**:
- Same bribery-based system as Moldavia
- **Wallachia commanded higher bribes** than Moldavia (being wealthier)
- Chief dragomans of the Porte were primary candidates
- Positions financed by creditors, creating debt obligations

**Tribute Requirements**:
- Wallachia paid tribute to the Porte throughout Ottoman suzerainty
- Tribute increased over time under various princes
- After 1774 Treaty, tribute dropped from ~50,000 to ~20,000 gold coins by the 1820s
- Political control combined with "Ottoman economical hegemony"

**Level of Autonomy**:
- Same high degree of internal autonomy as Moldavia
- Retained self-government, domestic administration, justice system
- Boyars gained tax privileges (though this caused fiscal pressure)
- Autonomous in religious affairs (Orthodox Christian)

**Constraints**:
- Ottoman suzerainty "remained virtually unchallenged" after early 16th century
- Princely succession entirely controlled by Sultan
- No independent foreign policy
- Military obligations to Ottoman campaigns

### Comparative Notes: Wallachia vs. Moldavia

**Similarities**:
- Both under Phanariote Greek rule (1715/1711–1821)
- Both appointed by Ottoman Sultan through bribery system
- Both retained high internal autonomy
- Both paid tribute to Porte
- Both prohibited from independent foreign policy

**Differences**:
- **Wallachia**: Wealthier, commanded higher bribes for appointments
- **Moldavia**: Lower tribute levels, closer to Russian sphere of influence
- Hospodars were sometimes **shuffled between the two principalities**

## Ottoman Suzerainty Comparison: Crimea vs. Danubian Principalities

| Aspect | Crimean Khanate | Moldavia/Wallachia |
|--------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Appointment** | Sultan selected from Giray dynasty | Sultan appointed Phanariote Greeks via bribery |
| **Tribute** | None (reverse flow: Ottomans paid Crimea) | Regular tribute to Porte |
| **Military Obligation** | Massive cavalry contingents (primary obligation) | Participation when requested |
| **Internal Autonomy** | Very high (full self-governance) | Very high (full self-governance) |
| **Foreign Policy** | Constrained (Russia only with Ottoman approval) | Entirely controlled by Ottomans |
| **Religious Status** | Muslim (Khan = "Caliph of the North") | Orthodox Christian (Patriarch as ethnarch) |
| **Economic Relationship** | Military service exchange | Tributary/fiscal extraction |
| **Ruler Ethnicity** | Crimean Tatar (Giray dynasty) | Greek (Phanariote families) |
| **Language** | Turkish/Crimean Tatar | Hospodars often didn't speak Romanian |

## Research Notes

**Date Adjustment**: Original research commissioned for 1759 start date; adjusted to **1763.2.11** without substantive changes to vassal relationships:
- Crimean Khanate remained Ottoman vassal throughout (Russian annexation: 1783)
- Phanariote system in Moldavia/Wallachia unchanged 1759–1763
- Ottoman Sultan: Mustafa III (r. 1757–1774) in both periods

**Key Finding**: The Crimean Khanate represents an **unusual vassal model** — no tribute paid, military service exchange, reverse financial flows — contrasting sharply with the **fiscal-tributary model** of Moldavia/Wallachia under Phanariote rule.

**Game Design Implications**:
- Crimea: Model as military-service vassal with high autonomy, Ottoman subsidies, mandatory cavalry obligations
- Moldavia/Wallachia: Model as tributary states with bribery-based appointments, tribute obligations, Greek Phanariote rulers
- Both: High internal autonomy, constrained foreign policy, Ottoman appointment/removal power
