## I. Siberian Fur Trade (1763 Status)

### Overview
By 1763, the Russian fur trade was 150+ years established, having peaked in the 17th century and consolidated into a state-managed system in the 18th. The trade was no longer expansion-driven but revenue-generating and trade-route-stabilizing.

### Fur Sources & Regions
- **Sable** ("soft gold," "Golden Fleece"): premium fur, primary trade target
- **Primary source regions**: 
  - Central Siberia (Lena River basin, Yenisei River basin)
  - Western Siberia (Ob River basin)
  - Far East (Amur region, approaching Kamchatka)

### Collection System: Yasak (Tribute)
- Indigenous peoples (Siberians, Tungus, Buryats, etc.) paid tribute in furs
- Males aged 18–50 subject to yasak collection
- Winter outposts (*zimovie*) and forts (*ostrogs*) at river confluences for collection
- By 1763, harsh coercion had given way to regularized tribute lists and customs administration
- **Key administrators**: "Bukharans" (Central Asian merchants) recruited for customs and logistics

### Trade Routes (River-Based)
- **Primary arteries**:
  - **Lena River**: Moscow → Arkangelsk → Arkhangelsk trade fairs, then sea export
  - **Yenisei River**: connecting interior fur zones to state trading posts
  - **Ob River** (flowing through Tobolsk): westward route to Russian heartland
  - **Amur River**: southern frontier, contact with China
  
- **Key trading posts/cities**:
  - **Tobolsk** (Ob River): fur-trade hub, expanded wealthy, customs center
  - **Yeniseysk** (Yenisei River): central Siberian collection point
  - **Mangazeya** (Taz River): arctic fur post (declining by 1763)
  - **Yakutsk** (Lena River): far-eastern collection hub

### Export Markets (1763)
- **China**: dominant buyer; 7+ million pelts annually in peak years (early 18th c.), down by 1763 due to overhunting of Siberian game
- **Western Europe** (via Arkhangelsk/Baltic): secondary, higher-value market
- **Bukhara/Central Asia**: silk and textile exchange (perpendicular trade)

### Overhunting Crisis Impact
- Early 18th century sharp decline in Siberian fur-bearing animals
- By 1763: trade maintained by **intensification of collection** (harder extraction from indigenous peoples) and **geographic expansion** (eastward, toward Kamchatka and Far East)
- Sable hunting ban discussed but not yet implemented (enacted 1913)

**Province Mapping (Imperator: Rome geography proxy):**
- Tobolsk region: **Yugra** / Tobolsk area (core trade hub)
- Lena basin: **Yakutia** (far-east collection)
- Yenisei basin: **Minusinsk** area, **Irkutsk** direction (interior collection)
- Ob/Western Siberia: **Tyumen**, **Tara** region (west-flowing)

## II. Demidov Ironworks Urals Empire (1763 Status)

### Founder & Lineage
- **Nikita Demidov** (1656–1725): founder, ennobled by Peter I, granted Urals foundry 1702
  - Built 5 metallurgical factories 1716–1725
  - Supplied Great Northern War munitions (cannons, pistols, swords)
  - Quality: "first Russian iron to rival English- and Swedish-produced iron"

- **Akinfiy Nikitich Demidov** (1678–1745): expanded empire, built 9 steel foundries/munitions factories 1717–1735; owned 25+ by death
  - Operations in Urals and Western Siberia
  - By mid-1700s: **52% of all Russian metal production**
  - Died 1745; heirs maintained empire through 1763

### Major Locations (1763)

**Nevyansk (Core Hub)**
- Founded 1700 by Nikita Demidov
- Location: Neyva River (Ob basin tributary), 97 km north of Yekaterinburg, eastern slope Middle Urals
- Land grant: 3,900,000 acres
- Infrastructure: Iron foundry, smeltery, goldmine (foothills)
- Cultural landmark: Leaning Tower of Nevyansk (built 1725–1740)
- Population: Old Believers (religious minority), skilled craftspeople
- **Province mapping**: Upper Ob / **Nevyansk district** (or Yekaterinburg vicinity, depending on map granularity)

**Yekaterinburg (State Works)**
- Founded 1723 by Vasily Tatishchev & Georg Wilhelm de Gennin
- Location: Iset River, eastern slope Urals
- Facilities: Large blast furnace iron plant (initially state-built, partially Demidov involvement)
- Status 1763: **largest iron factory in world at time of construction** (1723); dominates Urals production
- Secondary facilities:
  - Verkh-Isetsky plant (1726) on Iset River, 2 versts upstream
  - Uktus state ironwork (1702, Andrew Vinius)
  - Shuvakish ironworks (1704)
- Later role: gold minting (1820–1845: 45% world production; 1876: 80% Russian imperial coins)
- **Province mapping**: **Yekaterinburg** district, Iset River region

**Perm (Copper)**
- Founded ~1723–1726 as copper smelter (Yegoshikha site)
- Location: Mulyanka River, established by Georg Wilhelm de Gennin
- Role: copper extraction & smelting, secondary to iron focus
- **Province mapping**: **Perm** district, west-central Urals

**Western Siberia Mines (Demidov Operations)**
- Kolyban: Siberia's first iron mine (Nikita Demidov)
- Unnamed gold, silver, gemstone extraction: foothills of Urals across Siberian side
- **Province mapping**: Scattered across western Siberia / Ob basin tributaries

**Gold Discoveries (1745+)**
- Berezovskoe (discovered 1745, mining 1747): significant gold deposits
- Gumyoshevsky mine (1702): copper/malachite, "Copper Mountain" legend
- Magnetite deposits: Magnitogorsk area (not yet developed 1763)
- **Province mapping**: scattered across Urals foothills

### Production Scale (1763)
- Demidov heirs (post-1745) maintained ~25 factories
- Estimated output: 30–40% of Russian metal, down from Akinfiy's 52% (due to distributed state-factory growth)
- Primary products: 
  - **Cannons** and military ordnance
  - **Bar iron** for export to Western Europe
  - **Copper** (secondary)
  - **Gold** and **silver** (emerging)

### Strategic Context
- Peter I's **Berg Privilege (1719)**: allowed anyone to prospect and build factories; exempted manufacturers from taxes, troop quartering, and local authority interference
- **Collegium of Mining**: established 1721, state oversight body
- By 1763: Demidov family among richest private entities in Russia (second only to Tsar)

## III. Naval Stores Production (1763)

### Overview
Russia's maritime expansion (Baltic Sea post–Great Northern War 1721, Arctic expeditions) created demand for naval stores: tar, pitch, timber, hemp, linseed oil.

### Sources & Regions

**Tar & Pitch (Turpentine Products)**
- Primary source: **northern forests** (spruce, pine pitch distillation)
- Key regions:
  - **Karelia** (northwest of Urals, Finnish border): tar production centers
  - **Arkhangelsk region**: tar export hub for White Sea/Arctic shipping
  - **Lake Onega basin**: established tar distillery infrastructure
- Production method: forest pitch distillation in kiln structures (*smolokupki*)
- State monopoly over tar production post–Peter I reforms

**Timber**
- Primary source: **vast northern/central Russian forests**
- Key regions:
  - **Vologda region**: timber rafting routes to Arkhangelsk
  - **Northern Dvina River basin**: floating logs to shipyards
  - **Arkhangelsk**: major timber processing and export hub
- Strategic use: shipbuilding (Baltic Fleet, Arctic exploration vessels)

**Hemp & Flax**
- Production: **central Russian agricultural zone** (around Moscow, Ryazan, Tula)
- By 1763: established fiber production for rope, sailcloth
- Export outlet: Baltic ports for Western European textile/naval use

**Linseed Oil**
- Production: **central Russian agricultural zones**, oilseed crushing mills
- Use: waterproofing ship components, lamp oil (naval lighting)

### Strategic Economic Status (1763)
- Naval stores were **secondary to iron/fur** in volume but **primary to naval power**
- Post–Great Northern War (1721), Russia controlled Baltic naval bases (St. Petersburg, Kronstadt)
- Arctic exploration (1760s expeditions being planned, executed 1768–1769 Bering Strait expeditions) drove demand for vessels and tar
- By 1763: established state monopolies and private contractor networks for supply

**Province Mapping for Naval Stores:**
- **Arkhangelsk region**: tar, timber, hemp export; trade_good: `timber`, `tar`, `hemp`; building: `tar_kiln`, `timber_mill`, `shipyard`
- **Lake Onega / Karelia**: tar production; trade_good: `tar`; building: `tar_kiln`
- **Vologda / Northern Dvina**: timber rafting, fiber production; trade_good: `timber`, `flax`, `hemp`; building: `timber_mill`, `fiber_mill`
- **Moscow / Ryazan**: hemp, flax, linseed oil; trade_good: `hemp`, `flax`, `linseed_oil`; building: `fiber_mill`, `oil_mill`

## VI. Conclusion: 1763 Snapshot

By 1763, Russian economy was firmly built on three pillars:

1. **Siberian Fur Trade** (~1600s–present): state-managed yasak tributaries, river-route logistics, export to China and Western Europe. By 1763, overhunted but revenue-stable via extraction intensification and geographic expansion.

2. **Demidov Iron Empire** (1700–1763): private-state partnership producing ~30–40% of Russian metals. Core hubs at Nevyansk, Yekaterinburg, Perm. Exports to Western Europe; strategically critical for military (cannons, ordnance).

3. **Naval Stores** (1700–1763): tar, timber, hemp, linseed oil from northern forests and agricultural zones. Arkhangelsk hub; supporting Baltic Fleet and Arctic exploration. Secondary to furs/iron but strategically vital for naval power.

This tripartite economy underpinned Russia's position as a rising European power post–Great Northern War, enabling the military and commercial expansion that would characterize the reign of Catherine II (1762–1796, beginning just after our 1763 start date).
