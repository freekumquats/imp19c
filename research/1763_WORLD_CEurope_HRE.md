## I. HRE STRUCTURE 1763

### Constitutional Framework

The **Heiliges Römisches Reich deutscher Nation** (Holy Roman Empire of the German Nation) remains a functioning if archaic constitutional structure in 1763. Emperor **Francis I Stephen of Lorraine** (r. 1745-1765), husband of Maria Theresa, holds the imperial crown, but real Habsburg power resides with **Maria Theresa** as ruler of the hereditary lands (Archduchess of Austria, Queen of Hungary and Bohemia).

The HRE operates under the **Westphalian Settlement** (1648) as modified by subsequent constitutional practice. Key organs:

- **Reichstag** (Imperial Diet) at Regensburg — permanent assembly since 1663, divided into three colleges:
  - *Kurfürstenrat* (Electoral College): 8 Electors (later 9)
  - *Reichsfürstenrat* (Princely College): ~100 secular and ecclesiastical princes
  - *Reichsstädtekollegium* (Imperial Cities College): ~50 free imperial cities

- **Reichskammergericht** (Imperial Chamber Court) at Wetzlar
- **Reichshofrat** (Aulic Council) at Vienna — Habsburg-controlled competing jurisdiction

## III. SEVEN YEARS' WAR OUTCOME (Just Concluded)

The **Treaty of Hubertusburg** (15 February 1763) ended the German theater of the Seven Years' War (1756-1763). Our snapshot date of **16 February 1763** is the first day of peace.

### Terms & Territorial Status

**Status quo ante bellum** in Germany:

- **Prussia retains Silesia** — the core objective secured by Frederick II; Prussian sovereignty over Silesia confirmed. This is the **critical outcome**: Prussia has successfully defended its 1740s conquest and is now recognized as a European great power.
  
- **Saxony restored** — Electoral Saxony returned to Augustus III after Prussian occupation (1756-1763). Saxony devastated by war: Leipzig occupied, Dresden bombarded, treasury emptied. Economic recovery will take decades.

- **Glatz (Kłodzko) county** — remains Prussian (taken 1742).

No territorial changes beyond reaffirming the post-War of Austrian Succession settlement (Treaty of Dresden 1745, Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle 1748).

### Geopolitical Consequences for Central Europe (1763)

1. **Prussia's Great Power Status** — Frederick II's military reputation secured; Prussia now peer to Austria/France/Russia/Britain in diplomatic weight, despite smaller population/resources than Austria. The Austro-Prussian rivalry (***Deutscher Dualismus***) will define German politics until 1866.

2. **Austrian Strategic Posture** — Maria Theresa failed to recover Silesia; focuses on consolidation and reform of hereditary lands. The co-regency with **Joseph II** (from 1765) shifts Habsburg policy toward enlightened absolutism and administrative centralization.

3. **Saxony's Decline** — once wealthy, now ruined. Augustus III dies 5 October 1763; succession crisis in Poland (Elector Frederick Christian rules Saxony only 74 days before dying). Economic/military weight diminished.

4. **Imperial System Unshaken** — the HRE itself was not the war's object. Smaller states continued under imperial protection. No territorial mediatization yet (that comes 1803).

5. **Peace** — exhaustion all around. Next major European war is War of Bavarian Succession (1778-1779, bloodless). Central Europe enters a generation of relative stability.

## IV. THE 1763-1815 TRANSFORMATION: Map Delta

**THIS IS THE SINGLE LARGEST EUROPEAN MAP CHANGE** between the two dates. The Holy Roman Empire's unique fragmentation — ecclesiastical electorates, prince-bishoprics, imperial abbeys, free cities, imperial knights — **all vanishes between 1803-1806**.

### A. The Napoleonic Dissolution (1803-1806)

**1803: Reichsdeputationshauptschluss** (Imperial Recess / Final Recess)

Context: French Revolutionary Wars (1792-1802) saw France annex the left bank of the Rhine (Treaty of Lunéville 1801). HRE princes with territories west of the Rhine lost lands; compensation ("secularization" + "mediatization") destroyed the imperial structure.

**Secularization** (*Säkularisation*) — all ecclesiastical territories dissolved:
- The **three ecclesiastical Electorates** (Mainz, Trier, Cologne) abolished as states. (Mainz's Electoral vote transferred to Regensburg briefly; electoral college reformed.)
- ALL prince-bishoprics secularized — Salzburg, Würzburg, Bamberg, Münster, Paderborn, Hildesheim, Freising, Passau, Augsburg, etc. — absorbed by secular neighbors.
- All prince-abbacies dissolved.

**Mediatization** (*Mediatisierung*) — 45 of 51 imperial free cities lost independence, absorbed by larger states. Only **Frankfurt**, **Hamburg**, **Bremen**, **Lübeck**, **Augsburg**, **Nürnberg** survived to 1806 (most lost status 1806; Hamburg/Bremen/Lübeck/Frankfurt lasted as sovereign cities into 19th century).

**Beneficiaries**:
- **Baden** — gained Bishoprics of Konstanz, Basel, Strasbourg territories; raised to Electorate 1803
- **Württemberg** — gained bishoprics, abbeys, free cities; raised to Electorate 1803
- **Bavaria** — gained Bishoprics of Würzburg, Bamberg, Freising, Augsburg, parts of Salzburg, Swabian territories; raised to Electorate (moot since HRE dissolved 1806)
- **Hesse-Darmstadt** — gained territories
- **Nassau** — consolidations
- **Prussia** — gained Hildesheim, Paderborn, parts of Münster, Erfurt

**Result**: The HRE shrank from ~300 states to ~100 by 1803. Imperial institutions barely functioned.

**1806: Dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire**

- **Confederation of the Rhine** (*Rheinbund*) formed 12 July 1806 under French protection: Bavaria, Württemberg, Baden, Hesse-Darmstadt, Nassau, Berg, and others left the HRE.
- **Francis II** abdicated as Holy Roman Emperor **6 August 1806** (retaining title Emperor of Austria, adopted 1804).
- HRE ceased to exist.

**1815: German Confederation**

The **Congress of Vienna** (1814-1815) created the **German Confederation** (*Deutscher Bund*), a loose association of 39 sovereign states (vs. 300+ in 1763):
- **Austria** presided (until 1866).
- **Four Free Cities** remained: Frankfurt, Hamburg, Bremen, Lübeck.
- NO ecclesiastical states.
- Most small principalities mediatized (absorbed).
- New Kingdom of **Hanover** (elevated from Electorate, personal union with Britain ended 1837).
- Prussia gained Rhineland, Westphalia.

## V. ECONOMY c.1763 — Regional Detail with Locations

The Central European economy in 1763 is **pre-industrial**, dominated by agriculture, craft production, and mercantilist trade policies. No factories; putting-out system (Verlagssystem) dominates textile production. The Industrial Revolution has begun in Britain (1760s) but has not yet reached Central Europe (German industrialization begins c.1830-1850).

### A. Saxony — Mining, Porcelain, Textiles

**Location: Electoral Saxony (capital Dresden, commercial hub Leipzig)**

**Mining**:
- **Erzgebirge** (Ore Mountains, border Saxony/Bohemia): Europe's premier silver-mining region since medieval period. By 1763 output declining but still significant. Freiberg — center of mining operations + mining academy (Bergakademie Freiberg, founded 1765 just after our date; Saxon mining expertise long predates this). Silver, tin, cobalt.
- **Schneeberg, Annaberg, Marienberg** — historic mining towns in Erzgebirge.

**Porcelain**:
- **Meissen** (near Dresden): **first European hard-paste porcelain** manufacture (founded 1710 by Augustus II; secret formula discovered by Johann Friedrich Böttger). In 1763, Meissen porcelain is Europe's most prestigious ceramic; competes with Chinese imports. Royal Saxon Porcelain Manufactory at Meissen.

**Textiles**:
- Linen production, wool processing. Leipzig — major trade fair city (one of Europe's largest trade fairs, **Leipziger Messe**); Saxon textiles traded across Europe.

**Commerce**:
- Leipzig — banking, publishing (Leipzig book fair dates to 1632).

**War devastation**: Saxony suffered severe damage during SYW; Prussian occupation 1756-1763 drained treasury. Recovery slow.

**Sources**: 
- Ingrao, Charles W. *The Habsburg Monarchy, 1618-1815*. Cambridge UP, 1994.
- Ogilvie, Sheilagh. *A Bitter Living: Women, Markets, and Social Capital in Early Modern Germany*. Oxford UP, 2003.

### B. Bohemia — Glass, Textiles, Agriculture

**Location: Kingdom of Bohemia (capital Prague); Habsburg possession**

**Glass**:
- **Bohemian glass** (crystal, chandeliers): world-renowned; produced in northern Bohemia (Jablonec nad Nisou / Gablonz, Železný Brod, Nový Bor). Bohemian glassmaking guilds export across Europe. Bohemian crystal competes with Venetian glass.

**Textiles**:
- Linen (flax cultivation in rural Bohemia), wool cloth. Proto-industrial putting-out system: merchant capitalists organize rural spinners/weavers.

**Agriculture**:
- Grain (wheat, rye, barley). Bohemian estates worked by serf labor (robota obligations; serfdom not abolished in Habsburg lands until 1781-1785 Josephine reforms, 1848 final abolition).

**Other**:
- Brewing (Bohemian beer tradition ancient; Plzeň/Pilsen later famous).
- Prague — royal residence, university (Charles University, founded 1348), cultural center.

**Sources**:
- Sayer, Derek. *The Coasts of Bohemia: A Czech History*. Princeton UP, 1998.
- Hochedlinger, Michael. *Austria's Wars of Emergence, 1683-1797*. Routledge, 2003.

### C. Silesia — Linen (Prussian after 1742)

**Location: Silesia (capital Breslau / Wrocław); Prussian possession since 1742**

**Textiles**:
- **Silesian linen** — Europe's leading linen-producing region. Rural proto-industry: peasant households spin/weave flax; Breslau merchants export. Silesian linen shipped via Hamburg to Atlantic markets.
- Linen bleaching, dyeing.

**Mining**:
- Coal (Upper Silesia, near Katowice; extraction modest in 1763, grows 19th c.).
- Lead, zinc.

**Agriculture**:
- Grain estates (Prussian Junker estates worked by serfs; Prussian serfdom not abolished until 1807-1848 reforms).

Frederick II's administration: encourages settlement, mercantilist policies, promotes linen industry.

**Sources**:
- Clark, Christopher. *Iron Kingdom: The Rise and Downfall of Prussia, 1600-1947*. Belknap, 2006.
- Hagen, William W. *Ordinary Prussians: Brandenburg Junkers and Villagers, 1500-1840*. Cambridge UP, 2002.

### D. Habsburg Hereditary Lands — Austria, Hungary, Styria

**Austria (Archduchy of Austria, capital Vienna)**:

**Vienna**:
- Imperial capital (until 1806 HRE; Habsburg court).
- Administrative, cultural center.
- No major manufactures; court consumption drives luxury crafts.

**Agriculture**:
- Danube valley grain, wine (Niederösterreich, Burgenland).

**Hungary (Kingdom of Hungary, capital Pressburg / Bratislava for Diet, Buda-Pest dual city)**:

**Agriculture** dominates:
- **Great Hungarian Plain** (Alföld): grain (wheat), cattle ranching. Hungary as Habsburg breadbasket.
- **Wine**: Tokaj (Tokaji) wine — premium sweet wine exported across Europe. Eger, other regions.
- Livestock: Hungarian cattle driven to Vienna, German markets (long-distance cattle drives; major export).

**Mining**:
- Copper, gold, silver in Carpathian regions (Slovakia, Transylvania).
- Bánát region (southern Hungary/Vojvodina): Habsburg resettlement (after Ottoman reconquest 1699-1718); German, Serb, Romanian settlers.

**Styria (Steiermark)**:
- Iron mining and working (Erzberg, Eisenerz). Styrian iron exported; arms production.

**Sources**:
- Ingrao, *Habsburg Monarchy*.
- Kann, Robert A. *A History of the Habsburg Empire, 1526-1918*. U of California Press, 1974.
- Fichtner, Paula Sutter. *The Habsburg Monarchy, 1490-1848*. Palgrave, 2003.

### E. Prussia — Grain, Wool, Early Manufactures

**Brandenburg-Prussia (capitals Berlin, Königsberg)**:

**Agriculture**:
- **Grain** (rye, wheat) — Junker estates in Brandenburg, Pomerania, East Prussia export grain via Baltic (Danzig, Königsberg, Stettin) to Western Europe. Prussia as "grain exporter."
- **Wool** — sheep farming (especially Brandenburg); wool exported to Silesian/Saxon textile regions.

**Berlin**:
- Royal capital (Hohenzollern seat).
- **Silk manufacture** (royal manufactory; Huguenot refugees brought silk-weaving skills late 17th c.).
- **Porcelain** (KPM, Königliche Porzellan-Manufaktur Berlin, founded 1763 — literally this year by Frederick II, building on earlier private venture).
- Military production (arms, uniforms).

**Trade**:
- Baltic ports: Königsberg (East Prussia), Stettin (Pomerania), Danzig (independent free city but Prussian sphere, annexed 1793).

**Mercantilist state direction**: Frederick II promotes state-sponsored manufactures, settlement of depopulated areas (post-SYW), religious toleration attracts skilled migrants (Huguenots, Jews).

**Sources**:
- Clark, *Iron Kingdom*.
- Dwyer, Philip G., ed. *The Rise of Prussia, 1700-1830*. Routledge, 2000.

### F. Bavaria — Agriculture, Beer, Crafts

**Location: Electorate of Bavaria (capital Munich)**

**Agriculture**:
- Grain, livestock. Upper Bavaria (Alpine foothills), Lower Bavaria (Danube valley).

**Brewing**:
- Bavarian beer — regional tradition; Munich breweries (monastic brewing ancient; secular breweries growing). Reinheitsgebot (1516 purity law) still in force.

**Crafts**:
- Munich — court city (Wittelsbach residence); luxury crafts, goldsmiths, religious art (Baroque churches).
- Salt mining (Reichenhall, Berchtesgaden).

**Sources**:
- Blanning, T.C.W. *Reform and Revolution in Mainz, 1743-1803*. Cambridge UP, 1974 (covers broader South German context).

### G. Switzerland (Swiss Confederation) — Watchmaking, Textiles, Mercenaries

**Location: Old Swiss Confederacy (13 cantons + associates); independent from HRE since 1648 (de facto 1499)**

**Watchmaking**:
- **Geneva** — Europe's premier watchmaking center by 1763. Huguenot refugees (post-1685 Revocation of Edict of Nantes) brought skills; Genevan watches exported globally. Neuchâtel also center.

**Textiles**:
- **Silk ribbons** (Basel).
- Cotton spinning/weaving (Zürich, St. Gallen region develops Swiss cotton industry 18th c., booms 19th c.).

**Mercenaries**:
- Swiss military service contracts (capitulations) with France, Netherlands, other powers. Swiss Guard at Vatican; French Swiss regiments. Mercenary service = major Swiss export, brings remittances.

**Agriculture**:
- Alpine dairy (cheese exports: Emmentaler, Gruyère); livestock.

**Banking**:
- Geneva, Basel — banking centers (though Swiss banking dominance is later 19th-20th c. development).

**Sources**:
- Head, Randolph C. *Early Modern Democracy in the Grisons: Social Order and Political Language in a Swiss Mountain Canton, 1470-1620*. Cambridge UP, 1995.
- Körner, Martin. *Luzerner Staatsfinanzen 1415-1798*. Luzern, 1981 (German scholarship on Swiss economy).

### H. Rhineland — Wine, Trade

**Location: Electoral Cologne, Electoral Trier, Electoral Mainz, Palatinate, minor territories**

**Wine**:
- **Rhine wine** (Rheingau, Rheinhessen, Mosel): prestigious wines traded across Europe. Vineyards along Rhine and Mosel valleys (Rüdesheim, Bacharach, Bernkastel, Trier region). Riesling, other varieties.

**Trade**:
- Rhine river trade (Frankfurt, Mainz, Cologne major nodes). Imperial free cities (Frankfurt, Cologne) commercial hubs.
- Frankfurt — trade fairs, banking (Jewish banking houses: Rothschild family origin Frankfurt ghetto, though prominence comes 19th c.).

**Cologne**:
- Cologne water (eau de Cologne, Kölnisch Wasser) — perfume; Johann Maria Farina established 1709.

**Sources**:
- Blanning, *Reform and Revolution in Mainz*.
- Robisheaux, Thomas. *Rural Society and the Search for Order in Early Modern Germany*. Cambridge UP, 1989.

### I. Hanover — Agriculture, Trade

**Electorate of Hanover (personal union with Great Britain since 1714)**

**Agriculture**:
- North German plain: grain, livestock.

**Trade**:
- No major manufactures; agricultural exports, regional trade.

**Sources**:
- Simms, Brendan. *Three Victories and a Defeat: The Rise and Fall of the First British Empire, 1714-1783*. Penguin, 2007 (covers Hanoverian connection).

## VI. SUMMARY OF 1763 vs. 1815 DIFFERENCES

### Map Complexity
- **1763**: ~300 HRE states (electorates, prince-bishoprics, free cities, minor principalities, imperial knights)
- **1815**: 39 German Confederation states (no ecclesiastical states, 4 free cities only)

### Political Structure
- **1763**: Holy Roman Empire functioning (weak but intact); Emperor Francis I
- **1815**: HRE abolished 1806; German Confederation (1815-1866) replaces it; Austrian presidency

### Ecclesiastical States
- **1763**: 3 ecclesiastical Electorates + ~50 prince-bishoprics + prince-abbacies
- **1815**: ZERO (all secularized 1803)

### Free Cities
- **1763**: ~51 imperial free cities
- **1815**: 4 free cities in German Confederation (Frankfurt, Hamburg, Bremen, Lübeck)

### Great Power Balance
- **1763**: Austria/Prussia rivalry emerging; Prussia confirmed great power
- **1815**: Austria presides German Confederation; Prussia ascendant (gains Rhineland, Westphalia); rivalry continues to 1866

### Territorial Consolidation
- **1763**: Fragmentation extreme; many territories under 100 km²
- **1815**: Medium states (Bavaria, Württemberg, Baden, Hesse) greatly enlarged; Prussia dominates north; Austria dominates south/southeast

### Economy
- **1763**: Pre-industrial; craft guilds, putting-out system, agriculture dominant
- **1815**: Early industrialization beginning (textiles in Saxony, Rhineland; still pre-railroad)
