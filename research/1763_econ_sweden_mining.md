# Swedish Mining & Extraction Economy c.1763

## Overview

By 1763, Sweden had established itself as one of Europe's leading mining and metal-export powers, built on centuries of extraction from the Bergslagen region and the Falun copper mine. Swedish bar iron dominated European trade; copper, iron, tar, and timber constituted 75% of exports. This document consolidates production data, geographic specifics, and academic sourcing for 1760s-era Swedish mining operations.

---

## Falun Copper Mine (Kopparberg)

### Location & Administrative Context
- **Province**: Dalarna (Dalecarlia), central Sweden
- **Township**: Falun municipality, Kopparberg hundred
- **Coordinates**: Approximately 61°N, 15.6°E (modern reference)
- **UNESCO Designation**: World Heritage Site (inscribed 2001) as "Mining Area of the Great Copper Mountain in Falun"

### Historical Production & Significance
- **Peak production era**: 17th century (1600s), still commercially active through 18th century
- **1750s–1780s status**: Secondary producer relative to 17th-century peak, but remained a prestige operation and employment hub
- **Labor force**: Hundreds of miners and support workers (exact 1763 count not precisely documented in accessible sources)
- **Export commodity**: Refined copper bars for European markets; copper also supplied domestic arms, bells, and domestic metalware production

### Operational Details
- **Mining technique**: Deep underground shafts with drainage systems (sophisticated for era)
- **Output scale**: Bergskollegium (Swedish Bureau of Mines) regulated production and export
- **Integration**: Part of the larger Kopparbergslagen industrial district (copper and iron combined)

**Sources**:
- UNESCO World Heritage documentation (https://whc.unesco.org/en/list/1027/)
- University of Oslo research project "Copper in the Early Modern Period" (https://www.hf.uio.no/iakh/english/research/projects/copper-in-the-early-modern-period.-a-comparative-s/index.html)
- Jernkontoret Atlas of Sweden's Mining and Metallurgical Districts (34-district coverage; Kopparbergslagen detailed in district reports)

## Bergslagen Iron Mining Region

### Geographic Scope
- **Primary location**: Central Sweden, spanning modern Örebro, Värmland, and Gävleborg counties
- **Core provinces (1763 administrative)**: Västmanland, Dalarna, Örebro, Värmland, Gävleborg
- **Approximate area**: ~6,000–8,000 km² of mining and metallurgical activity

### Major Iron Districts & Mines

| District | Province | Key Centers | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------------|-------|
| **Värmland** | Värmland | Filipstad, Sunne, Fryksdalen | Major bar-iron forges; critical export hub |
| **Västmanland** | Västmanland | Arboga, Västerås hinterland | Bloomery and forge operations |
| **Dalarna** | Dalarna | Falun, Hedemora, Säter | Mixed copper/iron (Kopparbergslagen); bar iron forges |
| **Gävleborg** | Gävleborg | Gävle, Ljusdal | Coastal trade exit; bar-iron shipment point |
| **Örebro** | Örebro | Örebro, Lindesberg | Forges and re-export warehouses |

### Production Network
- **Bloomeries** (småbruk): Small, dispersed iron-smelting operations using local bog ore and charcoal
- **Forges** (bruk): Larger finishing operations converting pig iron to bar iron, concentrated in Värmland and Västmanland
- **Export pipeline**: Bar iron collected at provincial centers (Gävle, Västerås, Värmland ports) → Swedish west-coast ports (Gothenburg) or Baltic ports (Stockholm, Västervik) → European markets (England, Netherlands, Hamburg)

### Production Volumes (1750s–1790s Era)
- **1740s baseline**: ~40,000 tonnes/year bar-iron exports (Jernkontoret data)
- **1790s peak**: ~52,000 tonnes/year bar-iron exports
- **1763 estimate**: ~42,000–46,000 tonnes/year (interpolated from surrounding decades; trajectory rising post-1740s)
- **English imports from Sweden**: ~20,000 tonnes/year typical (1730s data; likely similar or higher by 1763)
- **Share of Swedish exports**: Iron constituted **~75% of total Swedish commodity exports** by late 18th century

**Sources**:
- Jernkontoret, "The History of Swedish Iron and Steel Industry" (https://www.jernkontoret.se/en/the-steel-industry/the-history-of-swedish-steel-industry/)
- Jernkontoret Atlas of Sweden's Mining and Metallurgical Districts (https://www.jernkontoret.se/en/research--education/research-in-the-history-of-metallurgy/atlas-of-swedens-mining-and-metallurgical-district/)
  - 30-year interdisciplinary project (1993–2024) in partnership with Swedish National Heritage Board

  - 2024 English summary: "Medieval mining districts in Sweden – The Essential Outlands"
- Taylor & Francis, "Swedish Iron and the English Iron Industry about 1700" (peer-reviewed academic journal; 1982)

## Tar Production (Pix Suecica)

### Geographic Distribution
- **Primary source regions**: Swedish Norrland (boreal forests) — Västernorrland, Jämtland, Norrbotten provinces
- **Primary production center**: Ume River valley (Västernorrland), Torne River region (Norrbotten/Finnish border)
- **Secondary regions**: Upper Dalarna, parts of Gävleborg forest belt

### Production Process
- **Material**: Pine (Scots pine, *Pinus sylvestris*) tar extraction via charring in pit kilns
- **Scale**: Dispersed cottage-industry operations (tarverk) in forested zones; seasonal (spring/early summer harvest)
- **End product**: Thick, sticky tar (pix) exported in barrels

### Export & Market Significance
- **Primary buyers**: English shipyards (naval stores), Dutch shipbuilders, other West European maritime powers
- **Export volume**: Data less specific than iron/copper, but tar constituted 5–10% of Swedish exports by value in the 1760s
- **Trade routes**: Barrels via Stockholm or Västervik ports → North Sea/Atlantic shipping

**Sources**:
- Helsinki University academic publication, "The Swedish Tar Company" (via institutional repository)
- Baltic maritime trade records (Wikipedia's "Baltic Maritime Trade (c. 1400–1800)"; cited cross-reference)

## Timber & Lumber Exports

### Primary Regions
- **Norrland forests** (boreal belt): Västernorrland, Jämtland, Norrbotten — primary long-timber source
- **Upper Svealand**: Gävleborg, Dalarna, parts of Västmanland — supplementary supply
- **Log-export routes**: River drives (Göta Älv, Klarälv, Göte Älv) from interior forests to west-coast ports

### Products & End Markets
- **Timber types**: Long-timber logs for shipbuilding; deals (cut boards) for general construction
- **Buyers**: English shipyards, Dutch traders, West European construction markets
- **Volume**: Complementary to tar and iron, constituting 3–8% of export value

## Mining Regulation & Governance

### Bergskollegium (Swedish Bureau of Mines)
- **Founded**: 1637
- **Authority**: Crown monopoly over all mining operations; licensing of private forges
- **Functions by 1763**:
  - Mining permits and regulations
  - Export licensing and tax collection
  - Technical standardization (bar-iron quality, weight)
  - Travelogue documentation (study missions to understand mining practices)

### Tour Documentation & Knowledge Circulation
- **Uppsala University research project** (2022–2024, Swedish Research Council-funded): "Mapping the Geographies of Early Modern Mining Knowledge"
  - Analyzed Bergskollegium-sponsored travelogues (1691–1826) via Swedish National Archives
  - Findings: Knowledge exchange among mining regions; iterative technology adoption
  - Researchers: Jacob Orrje, Måns Jansson (Uppsala), Olof Karsvall (Stockholm University/National Archives)

**Sources**:
- Uppsala University research project description (https://www.uu.se/en/department/history-of-science-and-ideas/research/research-projects-and-programmes/mapping-the-geographies-of-early-modern-mining-knowledge.html)
- Swedish National Archives (Riksarkivet), Jernkontoret collections: https://sok.riksarkivet.se/?postid=ArkisRef+SE%2FRA%2F420114

## Integration into 1763 Swedish Economy Model

### Trade Goods Classification
- **Iron** (bar iron): Manufactured export; primary value commodity (~40,000+ tonnes/year)
- **Copper**: Secondary manufactured export; prestige commodity from Falun and smaller operations
- **Tar** (pix): Commodity input to shipbuilding; export value for Swedish trading companies
- **Timber/lumber**: Commodity export; ancillary to shipbuilding supply chains

### Province-Level Economic Focus

| Province | Primary Product | Secondary | Export Hub |
|----------|-----------------|-----------|------------|
| **Västmanland** | Iron (bar, forges) | Timber | Västerås/Stockholm |
| **Örebro** | Iron (forges, warehousing) | — | Örebro/Västerås |
| **Dalarna** | Iron (bar) + Copper (Falun) | Timber | Gävle, Stockholm |
| **Värmland** | Iron (bar, major forges) | Timber | Gothenburg, Stockholm |
| **Gävleborg** | Iron (finishing, export) | Timber, tar | **Gävle** (primary export hub) |
| **Västernorrland** | Timber, tar | — | Stockholm, Västervik |
| **Norrbotten** | Tar, timber | — | Stockholm (via Baltic) |

### Port Infrastructure (1763)
- **Gothenburg** (west coast): Primary export hub for Värmland iron and timber (Atlantic/North Sea routes)
- **Gävle** (east coast, Gävleborg): Major iron and timber export point to Baltic
- **Stockholm** (central Baltic): Administrative hub; secondary export point for copper, tar, inland-origin goods
- **Västervik** (southeast, Östergötland): Secondary export point for tar and timber

## Conclusion

By 1763, Sweden's mining economy was mature, regulated, and globally significant. The Bergslagen region functioned as an integrated iron-extraction and refinement network spanning five provinces, exporting 40,000+ tonnes of bar iron annually. The Falun copper mine, though past its 17th-century peak, remained a prestige operation under Bergskollegium oversight. Tar and timber supplemented iron and copper as complementary export commodities. Geographic concentration in Värmland (forges), Västmanland (smelting), Gävleborg (export), and Norrland (tar/timber) provided robust provincial economic foundations for this period.
