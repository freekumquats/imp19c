## I. THE 1763 TREATY OF PARIS SETTLEMENT

The Treaty of Paris (10 February 1763) fundamentally reshaped colonial North America. The settlement that defines this start date:

### Territorial Transfers

**FRANCE → BRITAIN:**
- **New France (Canada)**: entire St. Lawrence basin, Great Lakes region, Acadia — the core of French North America ceded to Britain.
- **Pays d'en Haut**: upper Great Lakes and interior fur-trading posts.
- **All territory EAST of the Mississippi River** (except the city of New Orleans and its immediate environs): the eastern half of Louisiana, the Illinois Country, the Ohio Country.
- France retains only **fishing rights** off Newfoundland (at the islands of **Saint-Pierre and Miquelon**, returned to France as fishing bases).

**FRANCE → SPAIN:**
- **Louisiana** (territory WEST of the Mississippi River + the city of New Orleans): secretly ceded to Spain via the Treaty of Fontainebleau (3 Nov 1762), confirmed in the Paris settlement. Spain now controls the vast trans-Mississippi interior.

**SPAIN → BRITAIN:**
- **Florida** (both East and West Florida): ceded to Britain in exchange for the return of Havana (occupied by Britain 1762) and Manila (occupied 1762).

**France retains in the Americas:**
- **Saint-Domingue** (western Hispaniola, modern Haiti): the wealthiest colony in the hemisphere.
- **Guadeloupe** and **Martinique** (Lesser Antilles): sugar islands returned to France.
- **French Guiana** (Guyane française, cap. Cayenne): South American mainland holding.
- Saint-Pierre and Miquelon: fishing-station islands.

### Result: The 1763 Colonial Partition

| **Power**       | **Holdings in the Americas**                                                                 |
|-----------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **SPAIN**       | All of mainland Spanish America (see § II); Florida (NEW); Louisiana west of Mississippi (NEW) |
| **PORTUGAL**    | State of Brazil (see § III)                                                                  |
| **BRITAIN**     | Thirteen Colonies; Canada/New France (NEW); Florida (NEW, from Spain); West Indies sugar islands |
| **FRANCE**      | Saint-Domingue, Guadeloupe, Martinique, French Guiana; fishing rights (St-Pierre/Miquelon)  |
| **NETHERLANDS** | Suriname (Dutch Guiana); Curaçao and other Caribbean islands                                 |
| **DENMARK**     | Danish West Indies (St Thomas, St John, St Croix)                                            |

## II. SPANISH AMERICA (1763)

Spain's American empire in 1763 is organized into **four viceroyalties** and several **captaincies-general**. The Bourbon Reforms are underway but incomplete; the Viceroyalty of Río de la Plata does NOT yet exist (created 1776).

### A. Viceroyalties

#### 1. Viceroyalty of New Spain (Virreinato de Nueva España)
- **Capital**: Mexico City (Ciudad de México)
- **Established**: 1535
- **Territory (1763)**: 
  - Core Mexico (Nueva España proper)
  - **Captaincy-General of Guatemala** (subordinate, see below)
  - Northern frontier: Nueva Vizcaya, Nuevo León, Nuevo Santander, Texas, Nuevo México, Alta California (missions beginning 1769, but claimed 1763)
  - Caribbean: **Cuba**, **Santo Domingo** (eastern Hispaniola), **Puerto Rico**
  - The **Philippines** (Pacific, administered from Mexico City via the Manila galleon)
- **NEW (1763)**: Louisiana west of the Mississippi + New Orleans (from France, but administration messy — officially transferred to Spain 1769).
- **Viceroy (1763)**: Joaquín de Montserrat, Marqués de Cruilles (1760–1766)

#### 2. Viceroyalty of Peru (Virreinato del Perú)
- **Capital**: Lima
- **Established**: 1542
- **Territory (1763)**: 
  - Core Peru (Audiencias of Lima, Cuzco)
  - **Upper Peru** (modern Bolivia): Audiencia of Charcas, includes **Potosí** silver mines
  - **Chile** (subordinate Captaincy-General, see below)
  - **Río de la Plata region** (modern Argentina, Paraguay, Uruguay): still under Peru in 1763. Includes Audiencia of Buenos Aires (established 1661, suppressed 1671, re-established 1783 — in 1763 Buenos Aires is governed from Lima). The **Banda Oriental** (modern Uruguay) and the disputed Colônia do Sacramento region.
  - **Audiencia of Quito** (modern Ecuador): subordinate to Lima.
- **Viceroy (1763)**: Manuel de Amat y Junyent (1761–1776)
- **NOTE**: The **Viceroyalty of Río de la Plata** (cap. Buenos Aires) is NOT created until 1776. In 1763, all of modern Argentina, Paraguay, Uruguay is under the Viceroy of Peru in Lima.

#### 3. Viceroyalty of New Granada (Virreinato de Nueva Granada)
- **Capital**: Santafé de Bogotá
- **Established**: 1717 (suppressed 1723, **re-established 1739** — so it exists in 1763)
- **Territory (1763)**:
  - Core Nueva Granada (modern Colombia): Audiencia of Santafé de Bogotá
  - **Captaincy-General of Venezuela** (subordinate, see below)
  - **Audiencia of Quito** (modern Ecuador): initially under New Granada 1739–1751, then transferred back to Peru 1751–1802 — **in 1763, Quito is under Lima/Peru**.
  - Panama (Audiencia of Panama, part of Nueva Granada)
- **Viceroy (1763)**: Pedro Messía de la Cerda (1761–1772)

#### 4. Viceroyalty of Río de la Plata — **DOES NOT EXIST IN 1763**
- Created **1776** (capital Buenos Aires).
- In 1763, this territory (modern Argentina, Bolivia, Paraguay, Uruguay, southern Brazil frontier) is administered from **Lima** as part of the Viceroyalty of Peru.

### B. Captaincies-General (semi-autonomous, subordinate to viceroys)

#### 1. Captaincy-General of Guatemala
- **Capital**: Santiago de Guatemala (Antigua Guatemala until the 1773 earthquake; then Nueva Guatemala de la Asunción)
- **Superior authority**: Viceroyalty of New Spain (Mexico City)
- **Territory**: Central America from Chiapas (Mexico) to Costa Rica — modern Guatemala, El Salvador, Honduras, Nicaragua, Costa Rica, Chiapas.
- **Captain-General (1763)**: Alonso Fernández de Heredia (1761–1763)

#### 2. Captaincy-General of Chile
- **Capital**: Santiago
- **Superior authority**: Viceroyalty of Peru (Lima)
- **Territory**: Chile from Copiapó to Chiloé; ongoing **Mapuche resistance** in Araucanía (south of the Bío-Bío River) — the Spanish-Mapuche frontier is active, punctuated by *parlamentos* (treaty negotiations).
- **Captain-General (1763)**: Antonio de Guill y Gonzaga (1762–1768)

#### 3. Captaincy-General of Venezuela
- **Capital**: Caracas
- **Superior authority**: Viceroyalty of New Granada (Bogotá)
- **Established as Captaincy-General**: 1777 (before that, governed by a provincial structure under Bogotá — in 1763, **Venezuela is loosely organized under New Granada**, but the unified Captaincy-General does not exist yet).
- **Territory**: modern Venezuela (Caracas, Maracaibo, Cumaná, Guayana provinces).

#### 4. Captaincy-General of Cuba
- **Capital**: Havana
- **Superior authority**: Viceroyalty of New Spain
- **Territory**: Cuba, **Florida** (NEW in 1763, ceded by Spain to Britain — so in Feb 1763, Spain has JUST LOST Florida), Santo Domingo, Puerto Rico (Caribbean islands).
- **Captain-General (1763)**: Juan de Prado Portocarrero (at the time of Havana's fall to Britain 1762; succeeded by Ambrosio Funes de Villalpando, Conde de Ricla, 1763–1765, who oversees the British handover and the loss of Florida).

## III. PORTUGUESE AMERICA (1763)

### State of Brazil (Estado do Brasil)

- **Capital**: **Rio de Janeiro** (moved from Salvador/Bahia in **1763** — this is the EXACT YEAR of the capital transfer, coinciding with our start date).
- **Structure**: Brazil is a single unified colonial state (Estado do Brasil) under a governor-general (later styled Viceroy from 1720s onward). In 1763, the **Marquês de Pombal** (Sebastião José de Carvalho e Melo, Prime Minister of Portugal 1750–1777) is implementing administrative reforms (Pombaline reforms).
- **Territory**: 
  - **Northern coast**: Pará and Maranhão (sometimes administered as the separate **State of Maranhão and Grão-Pará** until 1774 — in 1763, there are TWO administrative states: Estado do Brasil + Estado do Maranhão e Grão-Pará, capitals Salvador/Rio and Belém).
  - **Northeast**: Pernambuco (cap. Recife/Olinda), Bahia (cap. Salvador) — **sugar** heartland.
  - **Center**: Minas Gerais (interior, **gold** mining region), Rio de Janeiro, São Paulo.
  - **South**: Santa Catarina, Rio Grande do Sul — **disputed frontier** with Spain over the Banda Oriental and **Colônia do Sacramento** (Portuguese outpost on the Río de la Plata estuary, opposite Buenos Aires, repeatedly contested).
- **Governor-General / Viceroy (1763)**: **Gomes Freire de Andrade** (died 1763); succeeded by **Antônio Álvares da Cunha, Conde da Cunha** (Viceroy 1763–1767), who presides over the capital's move to Rio.
- **Key issues (1763)**:
  - **Gold production** from Minas Gerais is DECLINING (peaked 1750s, now past peak).
  - **Sugar** remains dominant in Bahia and Pernambuco.
  - **Southern frontier tensions** with Spain: Colônia do Sacramento and the Banda Oriental (modern Uruguay) are flashpoints. The Treaty of Madrid (1750) and Treaty of El Pardo (1761) attempted to settle boundaries but failed; the **Treaty of Paris 1763** does NOT resolve Luso-Spanish borders — these remain contested.
  - **Indigenous policy**: Pombaline reforms (1755–1757) abolished indigenous slavery and Jesuit temporal power over missions (Jesuits expelled from Brazil 1759).

| **1815 Independent Tag/Nation** | **1763 Portuguese Authority**                                                                  |

| **Brazil** (BRZ)                | Estado do Brasil (cap. Rio de Janeiro, moved from Salvador in 1763) + Estado do Maranhão      |
| **Uruguay** (URU)               | **Disputed**: Portugal holds Colônia do Sacramento (under Estado do Brasil); Spain claims Banda Oriental (under Viceroyalty of Peru) |

## IV. BRITISH AMERICA (1763)

Britain emerges from the Seven Years' War as the dominant colonial power in North America.

### A. The Thirteen Colonies (British North America)

In **1763**, there is **NO United States**. The **Thirteen Colonies** are British:

1. **New England**: Massachusetts (includes Maine), New Hampshire, Rhode Island, Connecticut
2. **Middle**: New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Delaware
3. **South**: Maryland, Virginia, North Carolina, South Carolina, Georgia

- **Royal Proclamation of 1763** (7 Oct 1763, six months after the Treaty of Paris): Britain prohibits colonial settlement west of the Appalachian Mountains (the "Proclamation Line") to avoid conflict with Indigenous nations. This becomes a major grievance leading to the American Revolution.

### B. New Acquisitions (1763)

#### 1. Canada / New France (NEW, from France)
- **Quebec**: the core of New France, St. Lawrence valley (Montreal, Quebec City, Trois-Rivières).
- **Acadia / Nova Scotia**: already British since 1713 (Treaty of Utrecht), but now reinforced with the conquest of Quebec.
- **Pays d'en Haut**: Great Lakes interior and fur-trading posts (Fort Detroit, Michilimackinac, etc.).
- **Illinois Country**: trans-Appalachian interior (Fort de Chartres, Vincennes, Kaskaskia) east of the Mississippi — now British.
- **Governance**: The **Province of Quebec** is formally organized by the Quebec Act (1774), but in 1763 it is under military occupation transitioning to civil administration.
- **Indigenous relations**: **Pontiac's War** erupts in **May 1763** (three months after the Treaty of Paris) — a multi-tribal uprising (Ottawa, Ojibwe, Potawatomi, Seneca, Delaware, Shawnee) led by the Ottawa chief **Pontiac** against the British takeover of the Great Lakes forts. The war lasts into 1766 and is a direct response to British imperial overreach.

#### 2. Florida (NEW, from Spain)
- **East Florida** (cap. St. Augustine) and **West Florida** (cap. Pensacola): ceded by Spain to Britain in exchange for Havana and Manila.
- Sparsely populated; British establish new provinces.

### C. British West Indies (Caribbean)

- **Jamaica** (cap. Kingston): major sugar colony, large enslaved African population.
- **Barbados**: sugar island.
- **Leeward Islands** (Antigua, St Kitts, Nevis, Montserrat): sugar plantations.
- **Dominica, St Vincent, Grenada, Tobago**: **NEW** (1763, from France or neutral status) — ceded to Britain or confirmed as British in the Treaty of Paris.
- **Bahamas**: British since 1718.

### D. British Honduras (Belize)

- **Logwood and mahogany** extraction by British settlers (buccaneers-turned-loggers) in the Yucatán coast, nominally Spanish territory but with British presence tolerated under treaties. Formal colony status comes later.

### 1815 → 1763 MAPPING (British America)

| **1815 Tag/Nation**             | **1763 British Authority**                                                                     |
|---------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **United States** (USA)         | **The Thirteen Colonies** (British) — independence declared **1776**, NOT 1763                 |
| **Canada** (CAN)                | Province of Quebec (NEW, from France) + Nova Scotia + Newfoundland (British)                   |
| **Jamaica** (JAM)               | British colony (West Indies)                                                                   |
| **Bahamas** (BAH)               | British colony                                                                                 |
| **Various Caribbean islands**   | British Leeward Islands, Barbados, etc.                                                        |
| **Florida** (FLO)               | British East Florida + West Florida (NEW, from Spain in 1763)                                  |

## V. FRENCH AMERICA (1763) — REMNANTS

France loses its mainland North American empire but retains valuable Caribbean holdings:

### A. Saint-Domingue (Western Hispaniola)
- **Capital**: Cap-Français (Cap-Haïtien)
- The **wealthiest colony in the Americas** in 1763: produces ~40% of the world's sugar and more than half of its coffee. Enslaved African population ~500,000 (by 1789; smaller in 1763 but growing rapidly).
- Plantation economy: sugar, coffee, indigo.

### B. Guadeloupe and Martinique (Lesser Antilles)
- **Guadeloupe** (cap. Basse-Terre) and **Martinique** (cap. Fort-de-France/Fort Royal): sugar islands, returned to France in the Treaty of Paris (Britain had occupied Guadeloupe 1759–1763 and Martinique 1762–1763).
- France chose to keep these sugar islands rather than Canada (economic > territorial calculation).

### C. French Guiana (Guyane française)
- **Capital**: Cayenne
- South American mainland holding (north coast, between Dutch Suriname and Portuguese Brazil).
- Underdeveloped in 1763; plantation economy growing.

### D. Saint-Pierre and Miquelon
- Tiny islands off Newfoundland, returned to France in 1763 as **fishing bases** (cod fishery).

### 1815 → 1763 MAPPING (French America)

| **1815 Tag/Nation**             | **1763 French Authority**                                                                      |
|---------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Haiti** (HAI)                 | French colony of **Saint-Domingue** (western Hispaniola) — independence 1804, NOT 1763        |
| **Guadeloupe** (GUA)            | French colony (Lesser Antilles)                                                                |
| **Martinique** (MAR)            | French colony (Lesser Antilles)                                                                |
| **French Guiana** (FGU)         | French colony (Guyane française, South America)                                                |

### A. Dutch America
- **Suriname** (Dutch Guiana, cap. Paramaribo): plantation colony (sugar, coffee, cacao), large enslaved population.
- **Curaçao**, **Aruba**, **Bonaire** (ABC islands): Caribbean bases, trade entrepôts.

## VII. INDIGENOUS POLITIES (1763)

While European empires claimed the Americas on paper, vast territories remained under effective Indigenous control or contested in 1763.

### A. North America (Interior)

#### 1. **Comanche Empire** (Great Plains)
- **Territory**: Southern Plains (modern Texas, Oklahoma, New Mexico, Kansas panhandles).
- **Power peak**: 1750s–1840s. The Comanche (Numunuu) dominate the Plains via horse-mounted warfare, raiding Spanish New Mexico and Texas settlements, controlling bison hunting grounds and trade routes.
- By 1763, the Comanche are THE dominant power on the southern Plains, extracting tribute from Spanish settlers and Indigenous rivals alike.
- **Key rivals**: Apache (displaced southward), Spanish New Mexico.

#### 2. **Sioux / Lakota-Dakota-Nakota** (Northern Plains)
- **Territory**: Upper Missouri basin, northern Great Plains (modern Dakotas, Montana, Minnesota, Wyoming).
- In 1763, the Sioux are expanding westward (pushing aside the Cheyenne, Crow, Kiowa), acquiring horses and firearms via French traders (now transitioning to British after 1763).
- **Key trade goods**: Bison hides, furs.

#### 3. **Iroquois Confederacy** (Six Nations: Haudenosaunee)
- **Territory**: New York, Pennsylvania, Ohio Country — though much of the Ohio was ceded to Britain in treaties, the Iroquois maintain influence.
- **Structure**: Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca, Tuscarora (joined 1722).
- By 1763, the Iroquois are weakened (losses in the Seven Years' War, split allegiances between Britain and France), but still a diplomatic power. Some Seneca join **Pontiac's War** (1763).

#### 4. **Pontiac's Confederacy** (1763–1766)
- **Leader**: **Pontiac** (Obwandiyag), Ottawa war chief.
- **Participants**: Multi-tribal alliance — Ottawa, Ojibwe (Chippewa), Potawatomi, Wyandot (Huron), Seneca, Delaware (Lenape), Shawnee, Miami, Kickapoo, Mascouten, Wea, Piankashaw, Mingo.
- **Cause**: Resistance to British occupation of former French forts (Detroit, Niagara, Pitt, etc.) and land encroachment. The British, unlike the French, do NOT engage in the Indigenous diplomatic protocol of gift-giving and are seen as arrogant and land-hungry.
- **Timeline**: War begins **May 1763** (siege of Fort Detroit, 9 May 1763) — just THREE MONTHS after the Treaty of Paris. Multiple forts fall; frontier settlements raided. British response includes Colonel Henry Bouquet's relief of Fort Pitt (Battle of Bushy Run, Aug 1763) and BIOLOGICAL WARFARE (smallpox-infected blankets distributed at Fort Pitt by British officers, June 1763 — one of the first documented uses of biological weapons in the Americas).
- **Outcome**: Indigenous forces do not capture Detroit or Pitt; war winds down 1764–1766 with British treaties (but no formal surrender). The Royal Proclamation of 1763 (Proclamation Line) is partly a response to this conflict.

#### 5. **Cherokee** (Southeast)
- **Territory**: Southern Appalachians (modern Tennessee, North Carolina, South Carolina, Georgia, Alabama).
- Just fought the **Anglo-Cherokee War** (1758–1761) against British colonists; by 1763, an uneasy peace. Cherokee maintain autonomy but are pressured by British expansion.

#### 6. **Creek Confederacy** (Southeast)
- **Territory**: Georgia, Alabama, Florida panhandle.
- Divided into Upper Creek (Coosa, Tallapoosa rivers) and Lower Creek (Chattahoochee). In 1763, navigating relations with Britain (new power in Florida) and Spain.

#### 7. **Osage Nation** (Trans-Mississippi)
- **Territory**: Missouri, Arkansas, Kansas, Oklahoma — the **NEW Louisiana territory** ceded by France to Spain (1762) includes Osage lands.
- Dominant power in the lower Missouri valley, controlling trade routes. By 1763, Spanish authority is nominal; Osage are the effective power.

### B. Central and South America (Interior)

#### 1. **Mapuche** (Araucanía, Chile)
- **Territory**: South of the Bío-Bío River (southern Chile).
- **Status**: Autonomous. The Spanish have NOT conquered the Mapuche; the **War of Arauco** (1536–1818, intermittent) continues. In 1763, there is a frontier demarcated by *parlamentos* (treaties), but the Mapuche (Araucanía) are independent.
- Spanish Chile ends at the Bío-Bío; beyond is Mapuche country.

#### 2. **Gran Chaco Peoples** (Argentina interior)
- **Territory**: The Chaco (modern northern Argentina, Paraguay, Bolivia, southern Brazil) — arid lowlands between the Andes and the Paraná-Paraguay rivers.
- **Groups**: Guaycuru (Abipón, Mocoví, Toba, Pilagá), Wichí, Chorote, etc.
- **Status**: Unconquered. Spanish and Portuguese colonial authority is limited to the periphery (missions, forts). The Chaco interior is Indigenous-controlled, with groups raiding colonial settlements and resisting missions. Jesuit missions (until the Jesuit expulsion 1759–1767) had some presence but limited success.

#### 3. **Amazonian Peoples** (Brazil / Portuguese interior)
- The Amazon basin is sparsely colonized by Portugal; the interior is controlled by hundreds of Indigenous nations (Tupí, Jê, Pano, Arawak, Carib language families). Portuguese presence is limited to the lower Amazon (Belém, Manaus founded 1669), mission stations, and the *bandeirantes* (slave-raiding expeditions, though less active by 1763 after Pombaline anti-slavery reforms).

## VIII. ECONOMIC GEOGRAPHY (c. 1763) — by REGION with LOCATIONS

The 1763 economy is **pre-industrial**, agrarian, and extractive, organized around colonial trade goods.

### A. SILVER (Spanish America)

#### 1. **Mexico (New Spain)**
- **Zacatecas**: major silver mining district (mines opened 1540s, peak production 1700s–1800s).
- **Guanajuato**: by 1763, rising to prominence (will become the world's largest silver producer by 1800s). Valenciana mine (opened 1760s).
- **San Luis Potosí**: silver mining.
- **Taxco**: silver (older district, declining).
- **Trade route**: Mexico City → Veracruz (Gulf coast) → Spain (via Havana). Also Manila galleon (Acapulco ↔ Manila) for Asian trade.

#### 2. **Peru**
- **Potosí** (Upper Peru / modern Bolivia, Audiencia de Charcas): the LEGENDARY silver mountain (*Cerro Rico*). Discovered 1545; by 1763, production is PAST its peak (17th-century peak), but still immense. Colonial forced labor (*mita*) system extracts ore.
- **Huancavelica** (Peru): mercury mine, essential for silver refining (amalgamation process). Mercury production also declining by 1763.
- **Trade route**: Potosí → Lima → Panama (overland) → Portobelo → Spain. OR: Potosí → Buenos Aires (contraband route, growing in importance by 1763, reason for the later creation of the Viceroyalty of Río de la Plata 1776).

#### **Significance**: Silver is the BACKBONE of Spanish-American wealth. The Crown's *quinto real* (royal fifth, later reduced to tenth) on silver finances the Spanish empire.

### B. GOLD (Portuguese Brazil)

#### 1. **Minas Gerais** (Brazil)
- **Territory**: Interior highlands of southeast Brazil (cap. Vila Rica, modern Ouro Preto).
- **Discovery**: 1690s (gold rush); peak production **1750s**.
- **Status in 1763**: PAST PEAK, declining. Gold exhaustion is becoming apparent; the Crown imposes the *derrama* (forced tax quota), causing resentment (will lead to the *Inconfidência Mineira* rebellion 1789).
- **Labor**: Enslaved Africans (large-scale importation 1700s–1750s); free prospectors (*garimpeiros*).
- **Trade route**: Minas Gerais → Rio de Janeiro (the capital move to Rio in 1763 is directly related to the gold economy and the need to control the export route) → Lisbon.

#### 2. **Goiás and Mato Grosso** (Brazil interior)
- Gold discovered 1720s–1730s; by 1763, declining. Remote frontier.

#### **Significance**: Brazilian gold (1690s–1760s) floods into Portugal and thence into Britain (Portugal's ally and economic dependent via the Methuen Treaty 1703). The gold finances Portugal and indirectly Britain's industrial revolution.

### C. SUGAR (Caribbean + Brazil)

#### 1. **Saint-Domingue** (French, western Hispaniola)
- THE WEALTHIEST COLONY IN THE AMERICAS in 1763. Produces ~40% of the Atlantic world's sugar + majority of coffee.
- **Plantations**: Northern Plain (Cap-Français region), Artibonite valley, South Province.
- **Labor**: Enslaved Africans (~500,000 by 1789; smaller but growing rapidly in 1763). Brutal mortality rates; constant imports from West Africa.
- **Products**: Sugar (raw and refined), coffee, indigo, cacao, cotton.

#### 2. **Jamaica** (British)
- Second-wealthiest sugar colony after Saint-Domingue.
- **Plantations**: Coastal plains (Kingston, Montego Bay regions).
- **Labor**: Enslaved Africans (~200,000 by 1770s).

#### 3. **Barbados** (British)
- Sugar island, intensive plantation agriculture. By 1763, soil exhaustion is reducing productivity (peak was 1680s–1710s); still significant.

#### 4. **Martinique, Guadeloupe** (French)
- Sugar islands, returned to France in Treaty of Paris 1763. France valued these more than Canada.

#### 5. **Cuba** (Spanish)
- By 1763, Cuba is DEVELOPING as a sugar power (the sugar boom comes after the British occupation of Havana 1762–1763, when the British open Cuban ports to slave trade and modernize mills). In 1763, Cuba produces sugar but is NOT yet the giant it becomes by 1800s.
- **Havana**: major port, entrepôt for Spanish Caribbean and gateway to New Spain.

#### 6. **Brazil** (Portuguese)
- **Bahia** (cap. Salvador): THE heartland of Brazilian sugar (since 1500s). By 1763, facing Caribbean competition; still significant.
- **Pernambuco** (cap. Recife/Olinda): sugar region, second to Bahia.
- **Products**: Sugar (muscovado, white), molasses, *cachaça* (rum).
- **Labor**: Enslaved Africans (Salvador and Recife are major slave-trade ports).

#### **Significance**: Sugar is THE most valuable trade commodity of the 18th century. It drives the Atlantic slave trade (the Middle Passage: West Africa → Americas). By 1763, the plantation complex (sugar + slavery) is mature and enormously profitable, though ecologically and humanly devastating.

### D. TOBACCO

#### 1. **Chesapeake** (British colonies: Virginia, Maryland)
- **Regions**: Tidewater Virginia (York, James, Rappahannock rivers), Maryland (Potomac, Patuxent).
- THE dominant tobacco-producing region globally.
- **Labor**: Transition from indentured servitude (17th century) to **enslaved Africans** (by 1763, ~40% of Virginia's population is enslaved).
- **Trade**: Tobacco → Britain (re-exported to Europe). Basis of Chesapeake wealth; plantation economy (large estates).

#### 2. **Cuba** (Spanish)
- Tobacco (*tabaco*) cultivation in **Vuelta Abajo** region (Pinar del Río). By 1763, Cuban tobacco is prized (especially for cigars). Royal monopoly (*estanco*) controls trade.

#### 3. **Brazil** (Portuguese)
- **Bahia**: tobacco for West African slave trade (Brazilian tobacco exchanged for slaves in Angola, Benin). Lower quality than Virginia/Cuban; used as trade good, not luxury.

### E. COFFEE

#### 1. **Saint-Domingue** (French)
- By 1763, coffee is a GROWING crop (peak comes later, 1780s–1790s). Mountains of southern and western Saint-Domingue.

#### 2. **Martinique** (French)
- Coffee introduced ~1720s; by 1763, spreading.

#### 3. **Brazil** (Portuguese)
- Coffee is NOT yet significant in Brazil in 1763 (the coffee boom comes in the 19th century). Present in small amounts (Rio, Minas Gerais).

### F. INDIGO (dyestuff)

#### 1. **South Carolina** (British)
- **Lowcountry** (coastal plains, Sea Islands): indigo cultivation (introduced 1740s by **Eliza Lucas Pinckney**). By 1763, indigo is a major export (Britain subsidizes it with bounties to reduce dependence on French indigo).
- **Labor**: Enslaved Africans (South Carolina has a Black majority by 1708, sustained to 1763 and beyond).

#### 2. **Saint-Domingue, Guatemala** (French, Spanish)
- Indigo grown as secondary crop; Saint-Domingue indigo is high-quality.

### G. RICE

#### 1. **South Carolina and Georgia** (British)
- **Lowcountry** (tidal rice cultivation in swamps): Charleston region, Savannah River.
- **Labor**: Enslaved Africans (West African expertise in rice cultivation is exploited; the "Rice Coast" of West Africa — modern Sierra Leone, Senegal, Gambia — is targeted for slave raiding).
- By 1763, South Carolina is the wealthiest mainland British colony per capita (due to rice + indigo).

### H. FURS (North America)

#### 1. **Canada / Great Lakes** (British, formerly French)
- **Hudson Bay** (Hudson's Bay Company, British since 1670): fur trade (beaver, marten, fox) via York Factory, Fort Churchill. Indigenous trappers (Cree, Assiniboine) supply furs.
- **St Lawrence / Great Lakes** (former French fur trade, now transitioning to British): Montreal-based fur traders (many still French *coureurs de bois* and *voyageurs*) operate via the Great Lakes and Pays d'en Haut (Fort Detroit, Michilimackinac, Grand Portage). Indigenous suppliers: Ojibwe, Ottawa, Potawatomi, Huron.
- **Trade goods exchanged**: European manufactured goods (guns, iron tools, textiles, alcohol) for furs.

#### 2. **Louisiana** (Spanish, 1763–)
- **Missouri River** fur trade (Osage, Pawnee trappers). Spanish authority is nominal; French traders dominate.

#### 3. **Russian America** (Alaska, Aleutian Islands, northern Pacific coast)
- Russian fur trade (sea otter, fur seal) expanding in the North Pacific. By 1763, Russian posts in Alaska; the **Russian-American Company** is not chartered until 1799, but the trade is active. Exploits Aleut and Alutiiq labor.

#### **Significance**: The fur trade is THE economic driver of northern North America. Beaver pelts for European hat-making (the felt hat industry) fuel exploration and colonization. By 1763, over-trapping is depleting beaver populations in accessible regions, pushing the trade westward.

### I. NAVAL STORES (timber, tar, pitch, turpentine, masts)

#### 1. **New England** (British colonies: Massachusetts, New Hampshire, Maine)
- **White pine** (*Pinus strobus*) for ship masts (tallest, straightest trees in the world; reserved for the Royal Navy by the **Broad Arrow Policy** — trees marked with a broad arrow are Crown property).
- **Regions**: Maine forests, New Hampshire (Piscataqua River), Massachusetts.
- **Products**: Masts, spars, timber, tar, pitch, turpentine (from pine resin).
- **Significance**: Essential for the British navy (the "Wooden Walls" of Britain). American naval stores reduce British dependence on Baltic suppliers (Sweden, Russia).

#### 2. **North Carolina** (British)
- **Longleaf pine** forests: tar, pitch, turpentine. By 1763, North Carolina is THE leading producer of naval stores in British America.

### J. FISHING

#### 1. **Newfoundland** (British) and **Grand Banks**
- **Cod fishery**: The North Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) is THE staple protein of Catholic Europe and the Caribbean (salted cod). The Grand Banks (offshore Newfoundland) is the richest fishery.
- **British fishery**: Based in Newfoundland ports (St John's) and New England (Gloucester, Massachusetts).
- **French fishery**: France retains fishing rights off Newfoundland (at **Saint-Pierre and Miquelon**) in the Treaty of Paris 1763. French ships from Brittany (Saint-Malo, Granville) fish the Banks seasonally.
- **Trade**: Salted cod → Caribbean (slave-plantation food), Mediterranean (Catholic Europe).

#### 2. **Whaling** (North America)
- **New England** (Nantucket, New Bedford, Massachusetts): whaling for **sperm whale oil** (for lamps) and **baleen** (for corsets, etc.).
- By 1763, Nantucket whalers are the Atlantic leaders; the Pacific whaling boom comes later (1800s).

### K. CACAO (chocolate)

#### 1. **Venezuela** (Spanish)
- **Caracas region**: cacao plantations (for European chocolate consumption). By 1763, Venezuelan cacao is premium-quality; controlled by the **Guipuzcoana Company** (Caracas Company, Spanish royal monopoly).

#### 2. **Ecuador** (Spanish, Audiencia of Quito)
- Guayaquil region: cacao for export.

### L. COTTON

#### 1. **Brazil, Caribbean**
- Cotton is PRESENT in 1763 but NOT yet the dominant crop it becomes after the 1790s (Eli Whitney's cotton gin, 1793, and the Industrial Revolution textile demand). In 1763, cotton is a secondary crop (for local cloth and limited export).

#### 2. **Future significance**: Post-1790s, the US South (Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana) and Brazil become cotton empires. In 1763, this is not yet the case.

### M. LIVESTOCK (hides, tallow)

#### 1. **Río de la Plata** (Spanish South America)
- **Pampas** (modern Argentina): wild and semi-wild cattle herds (descendants of Spanish imports). By 1763, **hide exports** (to Europe, for leather) are growing.
- **Buenos Aires**: smuggling port (contraband trade with Britain, Portugal). Officially under the Viceroyalty of Peru (Lima), but Buenos Aires is developing economically due to Atlantic trade.
- **Products**: Hides, tallow, salted beef (*tasajo*).

#### 2. **Venezuela** (*Llanos*)
- Cattle ranching in the interior plains; hides for export.

### N. PLANTATION vs. SUBSISTENCE

#### **Plantation economies** (monoculture, enslaved labor, export-oriented):
- **Caribbean sugar islands** (Saint-Domingue, Jamaica, Barbados, Martinique, Guadeloupe, Cuba).
- **Brazilian Northeast** (Bahia, Pernambuco sugar).
- **South Carolina Lowcountry** (rice, indigo).
- **Chesapeake** (tobacco).

#### **Subsistence + market economies** (mixed farming, family labor or enslaved labor, local + export):
- **Thirteen Colonies** (outside Chesapeake/Lowcountry): New England (fishing, timber, small farms), Middle Colonies (grain — Pennsylvania/New York are "breadbaskets" supplying Caribbean plantations with flour, salted meat).

#### **Extractive economies** (mining, forced labor):
- **Spanish silver regions** (Zacatecas, Potosí).
- **Brazilian gold fields** (Minas Gerais).

## IX. SLAVERY AND THE ATLANTIC SLAVE TRADE (1763)

By 1763, the **Atlantic slave trade** is at or near its peak (the 1780s–1790s see the absolute height, but the 1760s are within the peak plateau).

### Scale (1763)

- **Estimated cumulative Africans forcibly transported to the Americas by 1763**: ~6–7 million (since 1500s).
- **Enslaved population in the Americas (c. 1763)**: ~2.5–3 million (concentrated in Brazil, Caribbean, southern British colonies).
- **Annual slave imports (1760s average)**: ~70,000–80,000 Africans per year across all European empires.

### Regional distribution (enslaved populations, c. 1763)

| **Region**                          | **Enslaved Population (approx.)**       |
|-------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------|
| **Brazil** (Portuguese)             | ~800,000 (largest in the Americas)      |
| **Caribbean** (all empires)         | ~1,000,000+ (Saint-Domingue, Jamaica, Barbados, Cuba, French/British Leeward Islands, Dutch Suriname) |
| **British Thirteen Colonies**       | ~400,000 (mostly Chesapeake, Lowcountry; ~20% of colonial population) |
| **Spanish America** (total)         | ~500,000 (Cuba, Venezuela, Colombia, Peru, Mexico — less plantation-intensive than Caribbean/Brazil) |

### Trade routes (1763)

- **West Africa** ("Slave Coast", "Gold Coast", "Windward Coast", Angola, Senegambia) → **Middle Passage** → Caribbean/Brazil/North America.
- **Slave-trading posts**: Elmina (Dutch/British), Cape Coast Castle (British), Ouidah (Dahomey, Portuguese/French/British traders), Luanda (Portuguese Angola).
- **Slave-trading ships**: British, Portuguese, French, Dutch, Spanish (by *asiento* contracts), Danish.

### Legal status (1763)

- Slavery is **LEGAL** in all European colonies in the Americas. The abolitionist movement is in its infancy (Quakers begin anti-slavery activism 1750s; the Somerset case in Britain is 1772; major abolition movements emerge 1780s–1800s).

## X. SYNTHESIS — THE 1763 COLONIAL MAP vs. 1815

### What changes between 1763 and 1815?

| **1763 (this start date)**                                      | **1815 (current mod start date)**                                   |
|-----------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Spanish America**: four viceroyalties, ALL colonies           | **Independent**: Mexico, Gran Colombia, Venezuela, Chile, Argentina, etc. (Bolivar's wars 1810s–1820s; Mexican independence 1821) |
| **Portuguese Brazil**: single colonial state                    | **Independent** (Brazil independence 1822, but de facto autonomous under João VI 1808–1821) |
| **British Thirteen Colonies**: colonies                         | **United States** (independent 1776–1783)                           |
| **Canada**: NEW British acquisition (from France)               | **British Canada** (remained British; confederation 1867)           |
| **Florida**: British (NEW, from Spain)                          | **Spanish Florida** (returned to Spain 1783) → **US Florida** (purchased 1819–1821) |
| **Louisiana**: Spanish (NEW, from France)                       | **US Louisiana** (purchased from France 1803 — France reclaimed it from Spain 1800) |
| **Haiti**: French Saint-Domingue, enslaved population           | **Independent Haiti** (independence 1804, Haitian Revolution 1791–1804) |
| **Indigenous polities**: Comanche, Sioux, Mapuche, Chaco, etc., control vast territories | **Reduced/displaced**: US/Argentina/Chile expansion pushes Indigenous nations westward/southward (ongoing wars, removals) |

### Key takeaway for the mod

To convert the **1815 map** to a **1763 map**, you must:

1. **Restore Spanish colonial authority** over all of Spanish America (12+ independent tags → 3–4 viceroyalties/captaincies).
2. **Restore Portuguese colonial authority** over Brazil (1 independent tag → 1 colonial state).
3. **Replace the United States with the Thirteen Colonies** (British subject).
4. **Add British Canada** (NEW in 1763, from France).
5. **British Florida** (not Spanish Florida of 1783–1819).
6. **Spanish Louisiana** (west of Mississippi, NEW in 1763 from France).
7. **Restore French Saint-Domingue** (not independent Haiti).
8. **Indigenous territorial control**: expand Comanche, Sioux, Mapuche, etc., territorial reach (less European encroachment in 1763 than 1815).

## XII. CONCLUDING NOTES FOR THE MOD

### The 1763 start date captures:

1. **The moment of maximum European colonial territoriality on paper** (Britain wins Canada and east-of-Mississippi; Spain gets west-of-Mississippi Louisiana; Spain loses Florida).
2. **The PRE-independence Americas**: NO United States, NO independent Spanish America, NO independent Brazil, NO independent Haiti — all are colonies. This is the LAST moment of unchallenged European colonial authority (before the American Revolution 1776, the Haitian Revolution 1791, and the Spanish-American wars of independence 1810s–1820s).
3. **Indigenous resistance at a high point**: Pontiac's War (1763) shows that Indigenous nations are NOT passive — the interior is contested.
4. **The plantation-slavery complex at near-peak**: The Atlantic economy is defined by sugar, silver, and slavery. Saint-Domingue is the richest colony; the slave trade is in full swing.
5. **Pre-industrial economy**: No steam, no factories, no railroads. Wealth comes from land (plantations, mines) and trade (Atlantic triangle trade, Manila galleon, fur trade).

### For the mod's mechanics:

- **Colonial subject mechanics**: All of Spanish America (12+ future nations) and Brazil should be colonial subjects of Spain/Portugal, not independent. Use the subject-interaction system (as in Qing mechanics) to model viceregal authority, *audiencias*, captaincies-general.
- **Indigenous nations**: The Comanche, Sioux, Mapuche, Pontiac's Confederacy, Osage, Creek, Cherokee should be PLAYABLE or at least represented as AUTONOMOUS polities (not just tribal pop modifiers). They control vast territories and engage in diplomacy, trade, and war with European empires.
- **Trade goods and buildings**: The economy list (silver, gold, sugar, tobacco, furs, naval stores, fish, etc.) should map to province-level trade goods and buildings (mines, plantations, sawmills, fisheries).
- **Event chains**: The 1763 start naturally seeds event chains — Pontiac's War (ongoing), the 13 Colonies' grievances (leading to 1776), the Louisiana handover to Spain (formalized 1769), the decline of Brazilian gold, the rise of Saint-Domingue coffee, etc.

### Map conversion priority (1815 → 1763):

1. **North America**: 
   - USA → British Thirteen Colonies (13 colonial provinces).
   - Canada → British Quebec (NEW, from France).
   - Florida → British (East/West Florida, from Spain).
   - Louisiana (west of Mississippi) → Spanish (NEW, from France).
   - Louisiana (east of Mississippi, north of New Orleans) → British (from France).
   - New Orleans → Spanish (from France).

2. **Spanish America**:
   - Mexico → Viceroyalty of New Spain (colonial subject of Spain).
   - Central America → Captaincy-General of Guatemala (under New Spain).
   - Gran Colombia/Colombia/Venezuela/Panama → Viceroyalty of New Granada.
   - Ecuador → Audiencia of Quito (under Viceroyalty of Peru).
   - Peru → Viceroyalty of Peru.
   - Bolivia → Upper Peru / Audiencia de Charcas (under Peru).
   - Chile → Captaincy-General of Chile (under Peru).
   - Argentina/Paraguay/Uruguay → Río de la Plata region (under Peru — no viceroyalty yet).
   - Cuba/Puerto Rico/Dominican Republic → Captaincy-General of Cuba (under New Spain).

3. **Brazil**:
   - Brazil → Estado do Brasil (colonial subject of Portugal, cap. Rio de Janeiro 1763).

4. **Caribbean**:
   - Haiti → French Saint-Domingue (colonial subject of France).
   - Other French islands (Guadeloupe, Martinique) remain French.
   - British islands (Jamaica, Barbados, etc.) remain British.

5. **Indigenous nations**:
   - Expand Comanche territory (southern Plains).
   - Expand Sioux territory (northern Plains).
   - Mapuche autonomous region (south of Bío-Bío, Chile).
   - Pontiac's Confederacy (Great Lakes, 1763–1766 — event-driven).
   - Osage Nation (Missouri valley).
