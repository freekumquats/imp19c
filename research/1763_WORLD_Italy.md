# Italian Peninsula: 16 February 1763
## Political Geography and Rulers

**Target Date**: 16 February 1763  
**Context**: Pre-unification, pre-Napoleonic Italy. Treaty of Hubertusburg (15 Feb 1763) ending Seven Years' War signed one day prior.

---

## 1763 ITALIAN STATES: RULERS AND EXTENT

### Kingdom of Sardinia (House of Savoy)
**Ruler**: King **Charles Emmanuel III** (Carlo Emanuele III)  
**Reign**: 1730–1773  
**Capital**: Turin  
**Extent**: 
- Duchy of Savoy (trans-Alpine territories)
- Principality of Piedmont
- County of Nice
- Island of Sardinia
- **Western Lombardy to the Ticino River** (acquired Treaty of Worms 1743, confirmed Aix-la-Chapelle 1748)
- Duchy of Aosta, Montferrat

**Status**: Independent kingdom, traditional French sphere but increasingly autonomous.

**Source**: Storrs, Christopher. *War, Diplomacy and the Rise of Savoy, 1690–1720*. Cambridge, 2000; Symcox, Geoffrey. *Victor Amadeus II: Absolutism in the Savoyard State 1675–1730*. London, 1983.

### Duchy of Milan (Lombardy)
**Ruler**: **Maria Theresa**, Holy Roman Empress and Archduchess of Austria  
**Reign**: 1740–1780 (Austrian Habsburg possession from 1706)  
**Governor**: Count Karl Joseph von Firmian (1759–1782)  
**Capital**: Milan  
**Extent**:
- Lombardy east of the Ticino (the river forms border with Sardinian Piedmont)
- Cremona, Mantua (from 1708), Pavia, Lodi, Como
- Does NOT include Venetian Lombardy (Brescia, Bergamo = Venetian terra firma)

**Status**: Austrian crown land, administered from Vienna; part of Habsburg Monarchy.

**Source**: Carpanetto, Dino, and Giuseppe Ricuperati. *L'Italia del Settecento: Crisi, trasformazioni, lumi*. Laterza, 1986; Hanlon, Gregory. *Early Modern Italy, 1550–1800*. Macmillan, 2000.

### Republic of Venice (La Serenissima Repubblica di Venezia)
**Ruler**: **Doge Marco Foscarini** (r. 31 May 1762 – 31 March 1763)  
→ Succeeded by **Alvise IV Mocenigo** (r. 31 March 1763 – 31 December 1778)  
**NB**: On 16 Feb 1763, Foscarini is doge; Mocenigo elected 6 weeks later.

**Government**: Oligarchic republic (Great Council, Council of Ten, Senate)

**Extent**:
- **Venetian Lagoon** and city of Venice
- **Terra firma** (mainland territories): Veneto — Padua, Vicenza, Verona, Brescia, Bergamo, Treviso, Udine, Belluno, Friuli west of Isonzo
- **Istria** (peninsula, inc. Pula/Pola, Rovinj/Rovigno)
- **Dalmatia** (coastal strip): Zadar/Zara, Šibenik/Sebenico, Split/Spalato, Dubrovnik enclave NOT included (independent Ragusa)
- **Ionian Islands** (Stato da Màr): Corfu (Kerkyra), Cephalonia, Zante (Zakynthos), Lefkada, Ithaca, Kythira, Cerigo
- **Crete** lost 1669; Cyprus lost 1571 (historical note)

**Status**: **INDEPENDENT sovereign republic** in 1763.  
**1815 fate**: **EXTINGUISHED**. Treaty of Campo Formio (17 Oct 1797) ceded Venice to Austria; became part of Austrian **Kingdom of Lombardy-Venetia** (1815–1866). Last doge Ludovico Manin abdicated 12 May 1797.

**TAG GAP**: The mod lacks a Venice tag because Venice ceased to exist as a polity by 1815.

**Economy 1763**:
- **Murano glass** (Venetian Lagoon islands) — monopoly weakening but still prestigious
- Declining maritime trade (Ottoman competition, Atlantic shift)
- Arsenal (shipbuilding, naval stores) in decline from 17th-century peak
- Terra firma agriculture: grain (Polesine), wine (Valpolicella, Soave)
- Wool and silk textiles (declining)

**Sources**:
- Lane, Frederic C. *Venice: A Maritime Republic*. Johns Hopkins, 1973.
- Gullino, Giuseppe. "La politica veneziana nei confronti dell'Austria nel XVIII secolo," *Studi Veneziani*, n.s. 10 (1985): 153–206.
- Georgelin, Jean. *Venise au siècle des lumières*. Paris: Mouton, 1978.
- Da Mosto, Andrea. *I Dogi di Venezia*. Milan: Martello, 1960. (Foscarini 1762–63, Mocenigo 1763–78)

### Republic of Genoa (Serenissima Repubblica di Genova)
**Ruler**: **Doge Agostino Lomellini** (r. 1760–1762)  
→ **Doge Rodolfo Giuseppe Brignole Sale** (r. 1762–1764) — in office 16 Feb 1763  
**Government**: Oligarchic republic (biennial doge, Great Council dominated by *alberghi* noble clans)

**Extent**:
- **Liguria**: Genoa city, Savona, La Spezia, Sarzana
- **Corsica** (nominal sovereignty only — island in revolt under **Pasquale Paoli** from 1755; Genoese control limited to coastal fortresses)
- **Cession to France 1768**: Treaty of Versailles (15 May 1768) cedes Corsica to France; Paoli defeated 1769

**Status**: **INDEPENDENT republic** in 1763, but in terminal decline; France militarily intervenes 1768.  
**1815 fate**: **ANNEXED**. Napoleon annexes Genoa to French Empire (June 1805, "Ligurian Republic" puppet 1797–1805). Congress of Vienna (1814–15) awards Genoa and Liguria to **Kingdom of Sardinia** (Treaty of Paris 30 May 1814, confirmed Vienna).

**TAG GAP**: The mod lacks a Genoa tag because Genoa ceased to exist as an independent state by 1815.

**Economy 1763**:
- **Banking and finance** (historically powerful; declining vs. Amsterdam/London)
- **Silk textiles** (imported raw silk from Levant/Persia, woven in Genoa and hinterland)
- Maritime trade (Mediterranean, Levant)
- Coral fishing (Ligurian coast)
- Alum, salt
- **Corsican exports** (while under nominal control): chestnuts, wine, timber (limited revenue to Genoa)

**Sources**:
- Costantini, Claudio. *La Repubblica di Genova nell'età moderna*. Turin: UTET, 1978.
- Bitossi, Carlo. "Il governo dei Magnifici: Patriziato e politica a Genova fra Cinque e Seicento." *Atti della Società Ligure di Storia Patria*, n.s. 26 (1986).
- Roggi, Piero. "Finanza pubblica, moneta e politica economica a Genova nel XVIII secolo," in *Storia di Genova*, vol. 7. Genoa: Società Ligure di Storia Patria, 2003.
- Thrasher, Peter Adam. *Pasquale Paoli: An Enlightened Hero, 1725–1807*. London: Constable, 1970. (Corsican revolt context)

### Grand Duchy of Tuscany
**Ruler**: **Francis I Stephen of Lorraine** (Francesco Stefano di Lorena), Holy Roman Emperor (1745–1765) and Grand Duke of Tuscany (1737–1765)  
**Regency**: Governed by regents on behalf of Francis; **Prince Marc de Beauvau-Craon** (1737–1757), then local council  
**Succession**: Second son **Peter Leopold** (Pietro Leopoldo) becomes Grand Duke 1765 (r. 1765–1790; enlightened reformer)

**House**: **Habsburg-Lorraine** (Francesco Stefano married Maria Theresa; Medici line extinct 1737)

**Capital**: Florence  
**Extent**:
- Florence, Pisa, Siena, Arezzo, Livorno (Leghorn), Pistoia, Lucca border zone (Lucca independent)
- Island of Elba (iron mines, Portoferraio fortress)

**Status**: Independent grand duchy under Habsburg-Lorraine; neutral in European wars.

**Economy 1763**:
- **Livorno free port** (established 1547–1593; peak 18th century) — grain, textiles, Levantine goods; Jewish and Greek merchant communities
- **Wine**: Chianti, Montepulciano
- **Silk** (Florence, Pisa)
- Olive oil
- **Alum** (Volterra region; papal monopoly broken)
- Iron (Elba)
- Tuscany = granary of Italy (wheat exports)

**Sources**:
- Wandruszka, Adam. *Pietro Leopoldo: Un grande riformatore*. Florence: Vallecchi, 1968.
- Diaz, Furio. *Il Granducato di Toscana: I Lorena dalla Reggenza agli anni rivoluzionari*. Turin: UTET, 1997.
- Malanima, Paolo. *L'economia toscana nell'età di Cosimo III*. Florence: Olschki, 1977.

### Duchy of Parma and Piacenza
**Ruler**: Duke **Philip** (Don Filippo di Borbone, Filippo I di Parma)  
**Reign**: 1748–1765  
**House**: Bourbon (son of Philip V of Spain and Elisabetta Farnese)  
**Succession**: Guglielmo du Tillot chief minister (enlightened reforms 1759–1771)

**Capital**: Parma  
**Extent**: Parma, Piacenza, Guastalla (from 1748)

**Status**: Bourbon duchy under Spanish influence; granted to Philip at Aix-la-Chapelle 1748 (compensating Spanish Bourbons after Austrian Succession war).

**Economy**: Agriculture (Po valley), Parma ham (prosciutto), cheese (Parmigiano-Reggiano origin), silk.

### Duchy of Massa and Principality of Carrara

**Status**: Small duchy, Modenese protectorate.

**Economy**: **Carrara marble** (white statuario marble — Michelangelo source; global export).

**Ruler**: **Oligarchic republic** (Council of Elders; *Gonfaloniere* elected every two months)  
**Capital**: Lucca  
**Extent**: City-state (Lucca and immediate countryside)

**Status**: Independent republic, Tuscan sphere.

**Economy**: Silk (Lucca silk textiles historically prestigious), olive oil, wine.

### Papal States (Stato Pontificio / Status Ecclesiasticus)
**Ruler**: Pope **Clement XIII** (Carlo Rezzonico, Venetian patrician)  
**Pontificate**: 1758–1769  
**Government**: Elective theocratic monarchy; College of Cardinals; Papal Curia  
**Cardinal Secretary of State 1763**: **Cardinal Luigi Maria Torrigiani** (1758–1769)

**Extent**:
- **Lazio**: Rome (capital), Viterbo, Frosinone, Velletri
- **Umbria**: Perugia, Spoleto, Terni, Orvieto
- **Marche**: Ancona, Ascoli, Macerata, Fermo, Urbino (from 1631)
- **Romagna**: Bologna, Ferrara (from 1598), Ravenna, Rimini, Forlì, Cesena
- **Enclaves**: Benevento (surrounded by Kingdom of Naples), Pontecorvo (between Naples and Papal Lazio)
- **Comtat Venaissin** (Avignon and Comtat in southern France; papal since 1274)

**Status**: Sovereign theocratic state; papal temporal power.

**1815 fate**: Restored after Napoleonic suppression (Rome annexed to French Empire 1809–1814); Congress of Vienna restores Papal States 1815 (minus Avignon, ceded to France 1791).

**Economy 1763**:
- **Alum** (Tolfa mines north of Rome — papal monopoly; used in dyeing textiles; revenue source)
- Agriculture: Campagna Romana (grain, livestock; malaria-ridden), wine (Frascati, Castelli Romani), olive oil
- Pilgrimage economy (Rome — Jubilee years, relics, indulgences)
- Ancona port (Adriatic trade)
- Salt (Cervia, Comacchio)
- Bologna university town, silk textiles

**Sources**:
- Gross, Hanns. *Rome in the Age of Enlightenment*. Cambridge, 1990.
- Vaussard, Maurice. *La vie quotidienne en Italie au XVIIIe siècle*. Paris: Hachette, 1959.
- Prodi, Paolo. *Il sovrano pontefice: Un corpo e due anime: la monarchia papale nella prima età moderna*. Bologna: Il Mulino, 1982.
- *Bullarium Romanum* (official papal documents, Latin).

### Kingdom of Naples (Regno di Napoli)
**Ruler**: King **Ferdinand IV of Naples** (Ferdinando IV)  
**Reign**: 1759–1806, 1815–1825 (as Ferdinand I of Two Sicilies post-1816)  
**House**: Bourbon (son of **Charles VII of Naples** / Charles III of Spain; Charles left for Spain 1759 when he inherited Spanish throne)

**Capital**: Naples

**Status**: Independent Bourbon kingdom, Spanish dynasty (Bourbon cadet branch).

### Kingdom of Sicily (Regno di Sicilia)
**Ruler**: **Ferdinand III of Sicily** (SAME PERSON as Ferdinand IV of Naples — personal union)  
**Reign**: 1759–1816 (then merged as Kingdom of Two Sicilies)

**Capital**: Palermo (separate viceregal administration from Naples)  
**Extent**: Island of Sicily

**Status**: Personal union with Naples under Bourbon crown (legally distinct kingdoms until 1816 unification).

**Economy (Naples + Sicily) 1763**:
- **Sicily**:
  - **Grain** (wheat — Sicily = breadbasket; export to Naples, Genoa)
  - **Sulphur** (Sicilian interior mines — global monopoly until 19th century; gunpowder, chemicals)
  - **Wine**: Marsala (fortified wine, commercialized 1770s by Woodhouse), Syracuse Moscato
  - **Tuna** (tonnare fisheries — Trapani, Favignana)
  - **Silk** (Messina, Catania)
  - Citrus (lemons, oranges — Palermo, Conca d'Oro)
- **Naples**:
  - Agriculture: Campania grain, wine (Lacryma Christi, Vesuvius slopes), olive oil
  - **Silk** (San Leucio royal silk manufactory founded 1778; earlier cottage production)
  - Livestock (Apulia, Basilicata)
  - Fishing (Amalfi coast, Bay of Naples)
  - Salt (Barletta, Margherita di Savoia)

**Sources**:

## ECONOMY BY REGION: 1763 DETAIL WITH LOCATIONS

### Northern Italy: Lombardy and Piedmont
**Regions**: Duchy of Milan (Austrian), Kingdom of Sardinia (Piedmont), Duchy of Parma, Duchy of Modena

**Economic Activities**:
- **Silk Production**: 
  - **Milan**: Raw silk (mulberry cultivation Po valley); silk weaving (Milanese luxury fabrics)
  - **Como**: Silk textiles (Como silk district — continuous to present)
  - **Turin**: Royal silk manufactory (Savoy court patronage)
- **Agriculture**: Po valley grain (wheat, rice in Vercelli/Novara lowlands), wine (Piedmont reds — Barolo region emerging), cattle
- **Rice**: **Vercelli, Novara** (Piedmont rice paddies; Italian risotto rice origin)
- **Metallurgy**: Iron (Val d'Aosta small-scale forges)
- **Cheese**: **Parma** (Parmigiano-Reggiano origins), **Lodi** (Grana Padano)

### Liguria: Republic of Genoa
**Economic Activities**:
- **Banking and Finance**: Genoa city (historically Europe's bankers; declining 18th century)
- **Silk Textiles**: Genoa, Savona (imported raw silk from Levant, Persia via maritime trade)
- **Maritime Trade**: Mediterranean, Levant, North Africa; **Genoa port** (entrepôt)
- **Coral**: Ligurian coast coral fishing (jewelry, devotional objects)
- **Olive Oil**: Ligurian Riviera
- **Salt**: Ligurian coast salterns

### Veneto and Venetian Territories
**Regions**: Republic of Venice (terra firma + lagoon + Istria + Dalmatia + Ionian Islands)

**Economic Activities**:
- **Glass**: **Murano** (Venetian Lagoon island) — luxury glassware, mirrors (monopoly weakening; competitors in Bohemia, France)
- **Maritime Trade**: Venice port (declining; Ottoman competition, Atlantic shift to Lisbon/Amsterdam/London); **Arsenale** (naval shipbuilding, declining)
- **Agriculture**: 
  - **Polesine** (Po delta) — grain, rice
  - **Valpolicella, Soave** (Verona hinterland) — wine
  - **Friuli** — grain, wine
- **Textiles**: Wool (Venetian terraferma; declining), silk (Vicenza, Verona)
- **Dalmatian Coast**: Timber (shipbuilding), wine (Hvar, Vis islands), olive oil, salt (Pag island)

### Tuscany: Grand Duchy
**Economic Activities**:
- **Wine**: 
  - **Chianti** (Florence-Siena hills) — red wine (Chianti Classico zone defined 1716 by Cosimo III)
  - **Montepulciano** — Vino Nobile
- **Olive Oil**: Tuscan hills (Lucca, Florence, Siena zones)
- **Port Trade**: **Livorno** (Leghorn) free port — grain imports (from Sicily, Black Sea), re-export to Europe; textile trade; Levantine goods; Jewish and Greek merchant communities (Sephardic Jews, Greek Orthodox)
- **Silk**: Florence, Pisa (luxury textiles)
- **Marble**: **Carrara** (white statuario marble; Apuan Alps quarries — Michelangelo source; exported via Carrara port to Europe)
- **Iron**: **Elba** (island mines; Portoferraio; iron ore export)
- **Alum**: **Volterra** region (alum for textile dyeing; competing with papal Tolfa)
- **Grain**: Tuscan Maremma, Val di Chiana (wheat; Tuscany = "granary of Italy")

### Papal States: Central Italy
**Regions**: Romagna, Marche, Umbria, Lazio

**Economic Activities**:
- **Alum**: **Tolfa** (north of Rome, Lazio) — papal alum monopoly (mining rights leased to contractors; alum used in textile dyeing across Europe; major revenue source)
- **Agriculture**:
  - **Campagna Romana** (Lazio countryside) — extensive grain cultivation, livestock (malaria-endemic lowlands; poor drainage)
  - **Frascati, Castelli Romani** (Alban Hills) — wine (papal court consumption, Rome market)
  - **Umbria** — grain, olive oil, wine
- **Port Trade**: **Ancona** (Adriatic port; trade with Balkans, Levant; Jewish merchant community; papal free port 1732)
- **Salt**: 
  - **Cervia** (Romagna coast) — sea salt (salterns; papal monopoly revenue)
  - **Comacchio** (Po delta lagoon) — salt, eel fishing
- **Textiles**: **Bologna** — silk textiles (Bologna silk district)
- **Pilgrimage Economy**: **Rome** — relics, indulgences, hospitality for pilgrims (Jubilee years 1750, 1775); papal court luxury consumption

### Kingdom of Naples (Mainland South)
**Economic Activities**:
- **Agriculture**:
  - **Campania** (Naples hinterland) — grain, wine (**Lacryma Christi** — Vesuvius slopes, Torre del Greco), olive oil, vegetables (San Marzano tomato region, though tomato cultivation minimal 1763)
  - **Apulia (Puglia)** — wheat (Tavoliere delle Puglie plain — "granary of Naples"), olive oil (Bari, Lecce zones; mass export), almonds
  - **Calabria** — olive oil, citrus, silk (Catanzaro, Reggio Calabria), bergamot (Reggio — unique to Calabria; perfume industry)
  - **Basilicata, Abruzzi, Molise** — sheep transhumance (wool; Abruzzi-Apulia seasonal migration), grain
- **Silk**: Calabria (Catanzaro silk district; raw silk and weaving)
- **Salt**: **Barletta, Margherita di Savoia** (Apulia coast salterns)
- **Wine**: Campania, Calabria (Cirò), Apulia (Primitivo zone emerging)
- **Fishing**: **Amalfi Coast, Bay of Naples** — anchovies, sardines, tuna

### Kingdom of Sicily (Island)
**Economic Activities**:
- **Grain (Wheat)**: Interior plains (Enna, Caltanissetta, Ragusa) — **Sicily = "granary of the Mediterranean"**; export to Naples, Genoa, Papal States, Malta; latifundia estates
- **Sulphur**: **Interior Sicily** (Caltanissetta, Enna, Agrigento provinces) — sulphur mining (Sicilian near-monopoly until 19th century; used in gunpowder, chemicals, bleaching, viticulture; export via Sicilian ports)
- **Wine**: 
  - **Marsala** (western Sicily) — fortified wine (commercialized 1770s by English merchant John Woodhouse; pre-1770s local sweet wine)
  - **Syracuse** — Moscato
  - **Etna** slopes — red and white wines
- **Tuna**: **Trapani, Favignana, Palermo** — *tonnare* tuna fisheries (traditional mattanza trap-net fishing; salted/preserved tuna export)
- **Silk**: **Messina, Catania** — silk production (raw silk, some weaving)
- **Citrus**: **Palermo (Conca d'Oro), Catania, Syracuse** — lemons, oranges, citron (export to northern Europe for scurvy prevention; prestige fruit)
- **Salt**: **Trapani** (western Sicily) — sea salt (salterns; Trapani salt trade to Mediterranean)
- **Cotton**: Minimal (expanding late 18th/19th century)
- **Pistachios**: **Bronte** (Etna foothills)

### Adriatic Coast: Venice, Papal Ancona
- **Venice**: Declining entrepôt; Levantine trade (spices, silk, cotton from Ottoman Empire); shipbuilding (Arsenale)
- **Ancona**: Papal free port (1732); trade with Balkans (timber, hides, wool), Levant (cotton, silk)

## ADDITIONAL NOTES: 1763 CONTEXT

### Political Context
- **Seven Years' War (1756–1763)**: Ends 15 February 1763 (Treaty of Hubertusburg, same day as research target). Italian states largely neutral or minor participants (Savoy provides troops to Austria).
- **Habsburg-Bourbon Rivalry**: Austria (Maria Theresa) vs. Bourbon powers (France, Spain, Naples, Parma). Italy = buffer zone.
- **Enlightenment Reforms**: Beginning in Italian states (Tuscany under Leopold from 1765; Naples under Tanucci; Lombard reformers under Austrian influence).

### Venice and Genoa: The "Phantom Tags"
Both republics are **fully sovereign and independent** in 1763, but **extinguished by 1797–1805** (Napoleon). The mod's 1815 data lacks tags for them because they no longer existed as political entities. Retargeting to 1763 requires:
1. **Creating VEN (Venice) and GEN (Genoa) tags**
2. Mapping Venetian terra firma, Istria, Dalmatia, Ionian Islands
3. Mapping Genoese Liguria (Corsica nominally Genoese but in revolt; ceded to France 1768)
4. Designing oligarchic republic government types (Venice = Great Council, Council of Ten; Genoa = doge + *alberghi* clans)
5. Economy scripts: Venetian glass (Murano), maritime trade decline; Genoese banking, silk

### Economic Character 1763 vs. 1815
- **1763**: **Pre-industrial**; artisan guilds, agriculture-dominant, maritime trade (Mediterranean-centered), luxury crafts (silk, glass, marble)
- **1815**: Early industrial stirrings (British textiles competition, steam power emerging); post-Napoleonic economic disruption; Continental System (1806–1814) devastated Mediterranean trade
- **Silk**: Italian silk production (Lombardy, Piedmont, Naples, Sicily) dominant in Europe 1763; competition from Bengal, China by 1815
- **Grain**: Sicilian wheat export dominant 1763; Black Sea (Odessa) competition by 1815
