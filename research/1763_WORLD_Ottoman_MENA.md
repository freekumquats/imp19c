**Research Date**: 2026-07-08  
**Bookmark Context**: Seven Years' War ended 15 February 1763 (Treaty of Hubertusburg)  
**For Project**: imp19c / Imperatrix: Victoria  
**Geographic Scope**: Ottoman core territories, Egypt, Iraq, Syria/Levant, Arabia, North Africa (Barbary), Persia

## Executive Summary

This report documents the political, territorial, and economic landscape of the Ottoman Empire, Middle East/North Africa, and Persia as of **16 February 1763**—one day after the Treaty of Hubertusburg ended the Seven Years' War in Europe. The research identifies critical differences between the 1763 and 1815 bookmarks for game implementation.

**Key Findings:**

1. **Ottoman Core (1763):** Sultan Mustafa III (r. 1757–1774) ruled with Grand Vizier Koca Ragıp Pasha (d. April 1763). The empire controlled Anatolia, Rumelia/Balkans, Syria, Iraq, Egypt, Hejaz, and the Barbary regencies nominally, but regional strongmen already wielded de facto autonomy.

2. **Autonomous Power Centers Emerging (1763 vs 1815):**
   - **Egypt:** Ali Bey al-Kabir held power as Sheikh al-Balad (1760–1766, 1767–1772), prefiguring later Mamluk autonomy; by 1815, Muhammad Ali Pasha governed as an autonomous dynasty-founder.
   - **Iraq:** Georgian Mamluks ruled Baghdad/Basra autonomously under nominal Ottoman suzerainty; this continued through 1831.
   - **Syria/Levant:** Zahir al-Umar controlled Galilee/Acre (cotton monopoly); Mount Lebanon under Shihab dynasty; Damascus governed by Ottoman-appointed pashas (Azm family influence waning post-1757).
   - **Greece/Balkans:** Fully under Ottoman control in 1763; by 1815, Greek independence stirring, Serbian autonomy emerging.

3. **Independent States Present in BOTH 1763 and 1815:**
   - **Morocco:** Sultan Mohammed III (Alaouite dynasty), fully independent
   - **Oman:** Ahmad bin Said Al Bu Said, controlling East African coast
   - **Yemen:** Imam Al-Mahdi Abbas (Qasimid/Zaidi imamate)

4. **STATE PRESENT IN 1763, ABSENT IN 1815:**
   - **First Saudi State (Emirate of Diriyah):** Founded 1744, ruled by Muhammad bin Saud (d. 1765), controlling Najd; destroyed by Ottoman-Egyptian forces in 1818.

5. **Persia (Major Dynasty Change):**
   - **1763:** Zand dynasty under Karim Khan Zand (Vakil, r. 1751–1779), capital Shiraz, controlled most of Persia except Khorasan (held by Afsharid Shahrokh).
   - **1815:** Qajar dynasty (Fath-Ali Shah, r. 1797–1834), capital Tehran—a completely different ruling house.

6. **North Africa:** Three autonomous Barbary regencies (Algiers under deys, Tunis under Husainid dynasty, Tripoli under Karamanli dynasty) nominally Ottoman but de facto independent; Morocco fully independent under Alaouites.

**Game Implementation Implications:** The 1763 bookmark requires representing the First Saudi State, the Zand dynasty (not Qajars), weaker Mamluk autonomy in Egypt (Ali Bey vs. Muhammad Ali), full Ottoman control of Greece/Serbia, and Zahir al-Umar's cotton emirate in Galilee.

## 1. OTTOMAN EMPIRE — CORE (1763)

### Sultan and Government

**Sultan:** Mustafa III (مصطفى ثالث)
- **Born:** 28 January 1717, Edirne Palace
- **Reign:** 30 October 1757 – 21 January 1774 (died of heart attack, age 56)
- **Buried:** Laleli Mosque, Istanbul
- **Succession:** First Ottoman succession to pass "from cousin to cousin" (succeeded Osman III)

**Background & Character:**
Son of Sultan Ahmed III and Mihrişah Kadın, Mustafa spent years imprisoned in Topkapı Palace following his father's deposition in the 1730 Patrona Halil revolt. He became heir in 1756 and took the throne in 1757. Contemporary accounts emphasize his "concern for justice" and efforts to boost Istanbul's prosperity through fiscal reforms, grain storage, aqueduct maintenance, and coinage regulation. He personally traveled to verify implementation of his laws. He was also a poet, writing under the pseudonym *Cihangir*.

**Grand Vizier (February 1763):**

**Koca Ragıp Pasha** (كوجه راغب پاشا, 1698–1763)
- **Tenure as Grand Vizier:** 12 January 1757 – 8 April 1763
- **Death:** 8 April 1763 (approximately two months after the 1763 game start date)
- **Background:** Born in Constantinople, son of Şevki Mustafa (Ottoman bureaucrat); served as chief treasurer in Baghdad, *reis ül-küttab* (foreign minister equivalent, 1740), and governor of Ottoman Egypt (1744–1748, resigned under pressure from local troops)
- **Relationship with Mustafa III:** Close personal ties—married the sultan's sister Saliha Sultan in 1758, earning the honorific *damat* ("bridegroom"). When he died, Mustafa III composed an elegy mourning his friend.
- **Policies:** Champion of peace policy toward neighbors; successfully kept the Ottoman Empire out of the Seven Years' War (1756–1763) despite Europe-wide conflict. Introduced administrative and financial reforms; "for the first time, Ottoman revenues exceeded expenditures" under his stewardship.
- **Successor:** Tevkii Hamza Hamid Pasha (April 1763), followed by Muhsinzade Mehmed Pasha, who also preferred peace but resigned in 1768 when Mustafa insisted on war with Russia.

**Government Structure:**
The central Ottoman government operated through the Imperial Council (*Divan-ı Hümayun*), led by the Grand Vizier, with authority delegated to provincial governors (*beylerbey*) of the eyalets. However, by 1763, many regions had achieved de facto autonomy under local strongmen (Mamluks in Egypt and Iraq, Georgian/Circassian military elites, Arab notables in Syria, hereditary dynasties in the Barbary regencies).

**Foreign Policy Initiatives (1757–1763):**
- **Prussia:** Admiring Frederick the Great's military skill, Mustafa established a peace treaty with Prussia in 1761, exchanging diplomats by 1763. He favored recruiting Prussian officers over French or British advisors to modernize the Ottoman army.
- **Military Reforms:** Limited progress before the 1768–1774 Russo-Turkish War; major reforms would come under French officer François Baron de Tott (modernizing artillery, founding Naval Engineering School in 1773).

### Territorial Extent (Core Eyalets)

The Ottoman Empire in February 1763 controlled the following core eyalets (administrative provinces led by beylerbeys):

**Anatolia (Core Turkish Lands):**
- Anatolia Eyalet, Karaman, Erzurum, Van, Trebizond (Lazistan), Sivas, Dulkadir (Maraş), Kars, Diyarbekir, Childir, Adana

**Rumelia / Balkans / Greece:**
- Rumelia Eyalet (central Balkans)
- Bosnia Eyalet
- Silistria Eyalet (Danube region)
- Morea Eyalet (Peloponnese, Greece; controlled 1715–1829)
- Archipelago (Aegean islands, naval domain of the Kapudan Pasha)

*Note:* Former Hungarian eyalets (Budin, Eger, Kanizsa, Temeşvar) had been lost to Austria by 1763. The Treaty of Belgrade (1739) returned northern Bosnia, Habsburg Serbia (including Belgrade), Oltenia, and southern Banat to Ottoman control, establishing a stable Danube-Sava border throughout the 18th century.

**Arab Provinces — Greater Syria:**
- Damascus Eyalet
- Aleppo Eyalet
- Sidon Eyalet
- Tripoli Eyalet (Tripoli-in-the-East, Lebanon coast)
- Rakka Eyalet

**Arab Provinces — Iraq:**
- Baghdad Eyalet
- Mosul Eyalet
- Basra Eyalet
- Shahrizor Eyalet

**Arab Provinces — Egypt & Hejaz:**
- Egypt Eyalet (de facto Mamluk control under nominal Ottoman suzerainty)
- Habesh Eyalet (also called "Mecca and Medina," covering the Hejaz along the Red Sea)

**North Africa (Barbary Regencies):**
- Algiers Eyalet (de facto independent "military republic" under deys since 1659)
- Tunis Eyalet (Husainid dynasty, autonomous since 1705)
- Tripolitania Eyalet (Karamanli dynasty, autonomous since 1711)

**Governance Model:**
Each eyalet was subdivided into *sanjaks* (livas) governed by sanjak-beys, who held near-absolute authority over military, financial, police, and judicial matters. In autonomous provinces (Egypt, Baghdad, Basra, Algiers, Habesh, Lahsa), the *timar* land-grant system did not apply; instead, governors sent fixed annual payments (*salyane*) to Istanbul—though by 1763, even these payments were often neglected or refused by de facto independent rulers.

### 1763 vs 1815 Delta: Ottoman Core

**Territorial Losses (1763 → 1815):**

1. **Balkans/Greece:**
   - **1763:** Greece (Morea, Archipelago, Rumelia) fully under Ottoman control
   - **1815:** Greek independence movement stirring (would culminate in 1821 Greek War of Independence); Serbia achieved autonomy under Miloš Obrenović (1817) after the First and Second Serbian Uprisings (1804, 1815)

2. **Egypt:**
   - **1763:** Ali Bey al-Kabir (Sheikh al-Balad, 1760–1772) held de facto power but still nominally under Ottoman suzerainty
   - **1815:** Muhammad Ali Pasha ruled as autonomous dynasty-founder (1805–1848), effectively independent; would conquer Sudan, Hejaz, and Syria in subsequent decades

3. **Iraq:**
   - **1763:** Georgian Mamluk pashas (Omar Pasha, 1762–1776) ruled Baghdad/Basra with substantial autonomy
   - **1815:** Mamluk rule continued (Dawud Pasha, 1816–1831); direct Ottoman control not reimposed until Ali Ridha Pasha in 1831

4. **Barbary Regencies:**
   - **1763:** Algiers, Tunis, Tripoli nominally Ottoman but de facto independent
   - **1815:** Same autonomy status, though European powers increasingly intervening (France would conquer Algiers in 1830)

5. **Arabia:**
   - **1763:** Hejaz (Mecca/Medina) under Ottoman-appointed Sharifs; First Saudi State controlled Najd
   - **1815:** First Saudi State destroyed (1818); Hejaz reconquered by Muhammad Ali Pasha on Ottoman orders; Second Saudi State would emerge in Nejd (1824)

**No Territorial Gains (1763 → 1815):**
The Ottoman Empire gained no new territories between 1763 and 1815. The period was marked by continuous territorial contraction, military defeats (Russo-Turkish Wars of 1768–1774, 1787–1792, 1806–1812), and the rise of autonomous provinces and nationalist movements.

**Administrative Continuity:**
The eyalet system remained in place until 1861–1866, when it was replaced by the vilayet system during the Tanzimat reforms. However, by 1815, effective central control had eroded significantly compared to 1763, with provincial strongmen, Mamluk dynasties, and Balkan autonomy movements challenging Istanbul's authority.

## 2. EGYPT (1763)

### Political Status and Rulers

**Status:** Ottoman eyalet with de facto Mamluk beylicate rule

**Sheikh al-Balad (Chief of the Country):** Ali Bey al-Kabir (علي بك الكبير, c. 1728–1773)
- **First term:** 1760–1766
- **Second term:** 1767–1772
- **Status in February 1763:** In power during his first term, but real power still contested with other Mamluks

**Background:**
Ali Bey was born in Abkhazia (or Alexandria), kidnapped at age 13 around 1741, and sold into slavery in Cairo. Purchased by two Jewish customs agents who gave him to Ibrahim Ketkhuda (a Mamluk leader of Georgian origin), he rose through Mamluk ranks to become *kashif* (district governor) by 1749 and *amir al-hajj* (commander of the pilgrimage caravan, the second-highest Mamluk office) in 1753/54. His military campaigns against Bedouin tribes earned him the nicknames *Bulut Kapan* ("he who catches clouds") and *Jinn Ali* ("Ali the demon").

**Mamluk Beylicate System:**
By the 1700s, Ottoman-appointed governors (pashas) sent from Constantinople were ceremonial figures with "continually decreasing respect." Real power rested with the **Mamluk beys**, particularly the two key offices:
1. **Shaykh al-Balad** ("chief of the country")—the strongman of the province, more powerful than the Ottoman governor
2. **Amir al-hajj** (commander of the pilgrimage)—the second-ranking position

Between 1688 and 1755, Mamluk beys—allied with Bedouin and factions within the Ottoman garrison—deposed at least thirty-four governors. By the 18th century, Mamluk beys controlled the treasury and provincial administrations, giving them power to challenge Ottoman authority at will.

**Factional Politics (1763):**
In his first term (1760–1766), Ali Bey was officially *shaykh al-balad*, but Abd al-Rahman held real power, using Ali Bey as a puppet. However, Ali Bey was building his power base:
- **1763:** He began consolidating control—exiling janissary officers from Alexandria, arresting four priests for ransom, and acquiring Mamluks (reaching 3,000 by 1766, appointing eight as beys)
- He was briefly forced into exile in Gaza, where he allied with Daher al-Umar (Zahir al-Umar) of Palestine
- Returned to Cairo in October 1767 through alliances, then systematically eliminated rivals via assassination and exile

**Path to Independence (post-1763):**
- **1769:** Ali Bey halted the annual tribute to the Sublime Porte and struck coins in his own name alongside the sultan's emblem, "effectively declaring Egypt's independence from Ottoman rule"
- **1770:** Conquered the Hejaz
- **1771:** Briefly occupied Syria, "thereby reconstituting the Mamluk state that had disappeared in 1517"
- **1772:** His trusted general Abu al-Dhahab was turned against him by an Ottoman agent and returned to Egypt, forcing Ali Bey out of power
- **8 May 1773:** Killed in Cairo

**Two Mamluk Factions:**
Egyptian politics were dominated by two rival Mamluk factions:
1. **Faqari** (white faction, pomegranate symbol, linked to Ottoman cavalry)
2. **Qasimi** (red faction, disc symbol, aligned with native troops)

These factions "wielded a significant amount of influence over Ottoman governors" and dominated 17th–18th century Egyptian politics.

### Territorial Control

**Egypt Eyalet** comprised:
- **Lower Egypt:** Nile Delta, including Alexandria (Mediterranean port), Rosetta (Rashid), Damietta (Dumyat)
- **Middle Egypt:** Cairo (capital), Giza, Faiyum
- **Upper Egypt:** Extending south along the Nile to Aswan
- **Red Sea Coast:** Including Suez (gateway to Red Sea trade routes)

**Note:** Egypt remained "semi-autonomous under the Mamluks" until the French invasion of 1798. The Sudan would not be conquered until 1820 under Muhammad Ali Pasha.

### Economy by Location

**Cairo (القاهرة):**
- **Capital and economic center** of Egypt
- Major trading hub connecting Mediterranean, Red Sea, and trans-Saharan routes
- Textile production (cotton, linen)
- Mamluk beys controlled treasury and tax collection
- Coffee transit trade from Yemen via the Red Sea

**Alexandria (الإسكندرية):**
- **Primary Mediterranean port**
- Grain exports to Constantinople and Mediterranean markets
- European merchant communities (French, British, Venetian, Greek)
- Cotton trade developing (though large-scale Delta cotton cultivation came later under Muhammad Ali from 1822)
- According to Cleveland and Bunton, Ali Bey "successfully expanded Egypt's trade with Britain and France" through Mediterranean ports, though Bidwell disputes the extent of European involvement

**Nile Delta (Lower Egypt):**
- **Wheat and grain production**—Egypt's primary export
- Rice cultivation in Delta marshlands
- Flax for linen production
- Rosetta and Damietta as secondary ports for grain shipments

**Red Sea Coast (Suez):**
- **Coffee trade**: Coffee from Mocha (Yemen) transited through the Red Sea to Suez, then overland to Cairo and Alexandria for European export (though Yemen's coffee monopoly was breaking down by mid-18th century as Europeans cultivated coffee in colonies)
- Spice and Indian Ocean goods transit route
- Connection to Hejaz pilgrimage routes

**Upper Egypt:**
- Grain production along the Nile
- Date cultivation
- Sugar cane (limited)

**Trade Routes:**
1. **Mediterranean:** Alexandria to Constantinople, Italian states, France
2. **Red Sea:** Suez–Mocha (Yemen)–Jeddah (Hejaz pilgrimage)–Indian Ocean
3. **Overland:** Trans-Saharan routes bringing enslaved people, gold, ivory from sub-Saharan Africa (though Sudan not yet under Egyptian control in 1763)
4. **Nile:** Internal river transport for grain and goods

**Economic Structure:**
The Mamluk beys controlled taxation, agricultural production, and trade revenues. Egypt's wealth derived primarily from:
- **Grain exports** (wheat to Constantinople and Mediterranean markets)
- **Textile production** (cotton, linen for local and export markets)
- **Transit trade** (coffee, spices, Red Sea goods passing through Egypt to Europe)
- **Pilgrimage revenues** (Hajj caravans passing through Egypt to Hejaz)

**Note on Sources:** Specific economic data for 1763 is limited; the above is reconstructed from general 18th-century patterns and the transitional period before Muhammad Ali's industrialization (1805–1840s).

## 3. IRAQ (1763)

### Political Status and Rulers (Mamluk Pashas)

**Status:** Ottoman eyalets (Baghdad, Basra, Mosul, Shahrizor) under autonomous Mamluk dynasty rule

**Ruling Dynasty:** Georgian (and Circassian) Mamluk Dynasty of Iraq
- **Founded:** 1704 by Hassan Pasha
- **Origins:** Elite of Georgian and Circassian origin, drawn from Caucasian military officers
- **Alternative name:** Sometimes called "Kölemen" (from Turkish *kul*, "slave/servant")
- **Duration:** 1704–1831 (ended when Ali Ridha Pasha reimposed direct Ottoman rule)

**Pasha in 1763:** Omar Pasha (عمر باشا)
- **Reign:** 1762–1776
- **Lineage:** Son of Ahmad Pasha (who ruled 1747 until death)
- **Predecessor:** Sulayman Abu Layla (1749–1762), who allowed the British East India Company to establish an agency in Basra in 1763

**Military Elite:**
By Ahmad Pasha's death in 1747, the Mamluks formed "a powerful, self-perpetuating elite corps of some 2,000 men" known as the **"Georgian Guard"**. Sulayman Abu Layla used this guard to seize Baghdad in 1749, "thereby inaugurating 84 years of Mamluk rule in Iraq" (counting from Sulayman's consolidation, though the dynasty began earlier under Hassan Pasha in 1704).

**Relationship with the Ottoman Empire:**
The Mamluks held **autonomy under nominal Ottoman suzerainty**, governing as an "autonomous state." Their relationship with Istanbul was pragmatic:
- The Porte acknowledged them as governors but demanded loyalty and occasional tribute
- The sultans occasionally used force to depose defiant pashas
- Success "depended on their ability to cooperate with their Ottoman suzerains and religious elite within Iraq"
- Despite tensions, the Mamluks expanded their territorial control and even brought Basra under Baghdad's authority

**Administrative Structure:**
The Mamluk pashas governed three key pashaliks:
1. **Baghdad Pashalik** (capital and heartland)
2. **Basra Pashalik** (Gulf port and trade hub)
3. **Shahrizor Pashalik** (Kurdish regions)

Mosul remained a separate eyalet, though the Mamluk pashas of Baghdad exercised influence over it.

### Territorial Control

**Baghdad Eyalet:**
- **Capital:** Baghdad (بغداد)
- **Core territory:** Central Iraq along the Tigris River
- **Sanjaks (1727–1740 period, representative):** Baghdad, Derne and Dertenk, Mendelcin, Cêssan Bedre, Herîr, Mendemi tribe territory, Government (Hükümeti) of Imadiyye
- **Area:** Approximately 62,208 square miles (19th-century figure)

**Basra Pashalik:**
- **Capital:** Basra (البصرة)
- **Territory:** Southern Iraq, Shatt al-Arab waterway, Persian Gulf coast
- **Strategic importance:** Gulf trade gateway, connection to Oman, India, East Africa
- **Brought under Baghdad's control** by the Mamluk pashas; countered threats from the Al-Muntafiq tribal confederation

**Shahrizor Pashalik:**
- **Territory:** Kurdish regions of northeastern Iraq
- **Administrative center:** Kirkuk or Sulaymaniyah (sources vary)

**Mosul Eyalet:**
- **Capital:** Mosul (الموصل)
- **Territory:** Northern Iraq along the Tigris, Kurdish and Arab populations
- **Status:** Technically separate eyalet, though influenced by Baghdad's Mamluk pashas

**Tribal and Religious Complexity:**
The Mamluk pashas ruled a diverse population:
- **Arab tribes:** Both Sunni and Shia, including powerful confederations like Al-Muntafiq in the south
- **Kurdish tribes:** Dominated northern and northeastern regions
- **Shia religious centers:** Najaf and Karbala (major pilgrimage sites)
- **Sunni urban centers:** Baghdad, Mosul, Basra

### Economy by Location

**Baghdad (بغداد):**
- **Capital and administrative center**
- Date cultivation in surrounding palmeries (Baghdad famous for high-quality dates)
- Textile production (silk, cotton)
- Grain production along the Tigris
- Transit trade hub connecting Persian Gulf, Anatolia, Syria, and Persia
- "Saw relative revival in the latter part of the 18th century under a largely autonomous Mamluk government" (partial recovery from earlier decline)

**Basra (البصرة):**
- **Primary port** for Persian Gulf trade
- **British East India Company agency established 1763** (permitted by Sulayman Abu Layla)
- Date cultivation (Basra dates among the finest in the world)
- Gateway for Indian Ocean goods: spices, textiles, indigo, coffee
- Connection to Omani maritime trade network
- European merchant presence (British, Dutch)
- Rice cultivation in marshlands north of Basra

**Mosul (الموصل):**
- Northern trade hub connecting Iraq to Anatolia and Syria
- Textile production (famous for muslin, from which "muslin" derives its name in some etymologies)
- Grain and livestock trade
- Kurdish tribal trade goods

**Najaf (النجف) and Karbala (كربلاء):**
- **Shia pilgrimage centers** (tombs of Imam Ali and Imam Husayn respectively)
- Pilgrimage revenues from Persian and Indian Shia visitors
- Religious endowments (*waqf*) supporting local economy
- Theological schools and scholarship

**Persian Gulf Coast (Basra hinterland):**
- Pearl fishing (though centered more in Bahrain and Trucial Coast)
- Date cultivation in coastal oases
- Transit point for goods moving between Mesopotamia and the Gulf

**Economic Policies of Mamluk Pashas:**
The regime "restored order and some degree of economic prosperity in the region" after earlier instability. Key policies:
- **Encouraged Omani and European trade** (especially British)
- **Countered tribal threats** to trade routes (notably Al-Muntafiq in the south)
- **Maintained control of Shia pilgrimage sites** (revenue source and political importance)
- **Tax collection** from agricultural lands and urban centers funded the Georgian Guard and administration

**Trade Routes:**
1. **Persian Gulf:** Basra–Muscat (Oman)–Bombay (India)–East Africa
2. **Overland to Syria:** Baghdad–Aleppo–Mediterranean ports
3. **Overland to Anatolia:** Mosul–Diyarbekir–Anatolia–Constantinople
4. **Overland to Persia:** Baghdad–Kermanshah–Isfahan/Shiraz
5. **Tigris/Euphrates river trade:** Internal transport of grain, dates, goods

**Primary Economic Products:**
- **Dates** (Baghdad and Basra palmeries)
- **Grain** (wheat, barley along the Tigris and Euphrates)
- **Textiles** (silk, cotton, muslin in Baghdad and Mosul)
- **Livestock** (sheep, goats, horses from Kurdish and Arab tribes)
- **Pilgrimage revenues** (Najaf and Karbala)
- **Transit trade** (Gulf goods passing through to Syria, Anatolia, Persia)

## 4. GREATER SYRIA / LEVANT (1763)

### Political Fragmentation and Key Rulers

Greater Syria (Bilad al-Sham / بلاد الشام) was administratively divided into Ottoman eyalets but politically fragmented among Ottoman-appointed governors, autonomous Arab strongmen, and hereditary dynastic enclaves. By 1763, three major power centers competed:

**1. Zahir al-Umar (Daher al-Umar) — Galilee and Acre**

**Ẓāhir al-ʿUmar al-Zaydānī** (ظاهر العمر الزيداني, c. 1689/90–1775)
- **Title (1768):** "Sheikh of Acre, Emir of Nazareth, Tiberias, Safed, and Sheikh of all of Galilee" (granted by Ottomans in 1768, formalizing status he already held)
- **Status in 1763:** Paramount Zayadina sheikh governing northern Palestine autonomously since the 1730s
- **Capital:** Acre (عكّا, Akka)—seized by force around 1744, formally appointed *multazim* (tax farmer) in July 1746, relocated headquarters there around 1750

**Territorial Control:**
- **Core:** Galilee region (Nazareth, Tiberias, Safed, Acre)
- **Expansion (1757):** Seized Haifa, Tira, Tantura (southward expansion)
- **Zenith (1771–1774):** Controlled Syrian coast "from Jaffa to Sidon," including influence in the Hauran

**Economic Power — Cotton Monopoly:**
Zahir controlled the Galilee's cotton crop and "eventually monopolized its high-demand trade with European merchants." He developed Acre into the primary outlet for this trade, "amassing substantial wealth" that funded his military and political operations. Cotton from Galilee became a major export to European textile manufacturers (especially French).

**Relationship with Ottoman Empire:**
Formal status remained limited to *multazim* (tax farmer). He clashed repeatedly with Damascus governors, including:
- **Sulayman Pasha al-Azm** (sieges of Tiberias, 1742–43)
- **Uthman Pasha al-Kurji** (major conflict, 1770–1771)

In early 1774, he briefly obtained a *malikane* (lifetime tax farm) of Sidon province, but the new sultan Abdul Hamid I voided it.

**Alliance with Ali Bey al-Kabir (Egypt):**
Zahir allied with Egypt's mamluk strongman Ali Bey al-Kabir and the Shia Metawalis (Jabal Amil) against Uthman Pasha. Their joint campaigns:
- Ousted Ottoman governors from Damascus and Sidon
- **Battle of Lake Huleh (2 September 1771):** Decisive victory
- Occupied Sidon
- Partnership "posed the most serious domestic challenge to Ottoman rule in the 18th century"

After Ali Bey died in captivity (April 1773), Zahir continued with Russian naval support, taking Jaffa and dislodging Ottomans from Beirut (1772).

**Downfall (1775):**
After the Russo-Turkish War ended in 1774, the Ottomans moved against Zahir:
- **March 1775:** Abu al-Dhahab (Ali Bey's former general) campaigned in Palestine, massacring Jaffa's population and briefly ousting Zahir from Acre
- **June 1775:** Abu al-Dhahab's sudden death (10 June) allowed Zahir to return
- **August 1775:** Ottoman admiral Hasan Pasha al-Jaza'iri blockaded Acre; betrayed by his lieutenant Dinkizli, Zahir tried to flee on 22 August, was shot and decapitated by a Maghrebi soldier
- By late 1776, Ahmad Pasha al-Jazzar (Ottoman-appointed) eliminated remaining Zaydani power

**2. Azm Family — Damascus**

**As'ad Pasha al-Azm** (أسعد باشا العظم, d. 1758)
- **Governor of Damascus:** 1743–1757
- **Status in 1763:** Dead; family influence waning after his deposition and execution (1757–1758)

**Azm Dynasty Background:**
The Azm family emerged in the mid-17th century when Ibrahim al-Azm restored order in Ma'arrat al-Nu'man. His sons received hereditary tax farms in Homs, Hama, and Ma'arrat al-Nu'man. Key governors:
- **Ismail Pasha:** Transferred to Damascus governorship in 1725
- **Sulayman Pasha:** Damascus 1733–1738, 1741–1743
- **As'ad Pasha:** Damascus 1743–1757 ("the greatest governor of Damascus in Ottoman times")

Family members held power in Damascus for 47 years between 1725 and 1783 (intermittently).

**Status in 1763:**
After As'ad Pasha's deposition in 1757 and subsequent execution, the family's dominant period ended. The Ottoman governor in Damascus around 1763 was likely **not an Azm**, as the article does not name a family member between As'ad's deposition (1757) and later intermittent appointments. The last Azm governor was Abdullah Pasha al-Azm (1795–1807, intermittently).

**Economic Base:**
- Hereditary tax farms in Homs, Hama, Ma'arrat al-Nu'man
- As'ad Pasha accumulated immense wealth (confiscated amounts "made the state revalue its currency")

**Architectural Legacy:**
"A building boom in Damascus" under Azm rule:
- Azm Palace (Damascus) and Azm Palace (Hama)—built by As'ad Pasha as residences
- Khan Sulayman Pasha, Khan As'ad Pasha
- Baths, schools, souqs

**3. Mount Lebanon — Shihab Dynasty**

**Mount Lebanon Emirate** (إمارة جبل لبنان)
- **Dynasty:** Shihab family (replaced Ma'an dynasty in 1697)
- **Status:** Ottoman *iltizam* (tax farm) rather than true principality
- **Religious character:** Shihab emirs were "nominally Sunni Muslim," unusual in a Druze-dominated feudal society

**Rulers in 1763:**
Following Milhim al-Shihab's abdication in 1753, administration passed to:
- **Ahmad and Mansur al-Shihab** (1754–1763)
- **Yusuf al-Shihab** (emerged as administrator of the Shuf by 1763)

**Political Structure:**
Starting in 1711, the Shihabs introduced "a unique system of fiscal cantons" in the Shuf and Kisrawan. Druze and Maronite sheikhs of the cantons "worked in cooperation," with the Shihab emir sitting atop the feudal hierarchy. The system depended on a "Druze-Christian alliance."

**Autonomy:**
Limited and precarious—governance functioned as a tax farm. Emirs served as *multazims* whose relationship with the Porte centered on "the payment of taxes." Milhim repeatedly avoided full tax payments, prompting a punitive Ottoman expedition in 1748.

**Religious Composition:**
- **Maronite Christians** (concentrated in Kisrawan)
- **Druze** (concentrated in the Shuf and Chouf Mountains)
- **Muslims** (minority)

Historically, "Druzes and the Christians in the Shuf Mountains lived in complete harmony."

### Territorial Control

**Damascus Eyalet (دمشق):**
- **Capital:** Damascus
- **Territory:** Southern Syria, Hauran, parts of Palestine
- **Governor in 1763:** Unknown (post-Azm period)

**Aleppo Eyalet (حلب):**
- **Capital:** Aleppo
- **Territory:** Northern Syria
- **Economic role:** Major trade hub connecting Anatolia, Persia, Iraq, and the Mediterranean

**Sidon Eyalet (صيدا):**
- **Capital:** Sidon
- **Territory:** Lebanese coast, parts of Palestine
- **Note:** Zahir al-Umar controlled much of this territory de facto, though formally a *multazim*

**Tripoli Eyalet (طرابلس الشام, Tripoli-in-the-East):**
- **Capital:** Tripoli
- **Territory:** Northern Lebanese coast
- **Distinction:** Not to be confused with Tripoli in North Africa

**Rakka Eyalet (الرقة):**
- **Territory:** Northern Syria along the Euphrates River
- **Character:** Frontier region with strong tribal influence

**Mount Lebanon (جبل لبنان):**
- **Autonomous enclave** within the broader Syrian eyalets
- **Territory:** Lebanon Mountains (Shuf, Kisrawan, Matn, Gharb)
- **Not a formal eyalet** but an *iltizam* governed by Shihab emirs

### Economy by Location

**Galilee and Acre (Zahir al-Umar's Domain):**
- **Cotton:** Galilee's primary export, monopolized by Zahir al-Umar for European markets (especially French textile industry)
- **Olive oil:** Olive cultivation in Galilee hills
- **Wheat:** Grain production in valleys
- **Acre port:** Developed by Zahir into major outlet for cotton and olive oil exports; European merchant communities
- **Silk:** Limited production compared to Mount Lebanon

**Mount Lebanon:**
- **Silk:** Primary export commodity—Mount Lebanon's mulberry groves and silk production supplied European markets (especially Lyon, France)
- **Olive oil:** Secondary export
- **Wine:** Christian (Maronite) communities produced wine
- **Wheat and barley:** Subsistence agriculture in valleys
- **Feudal economy:** Based on canton system with Druze and Maronite sheikhs controlling land

**Damascus:**
- **Trade hub:** Connecting Mediterranean (via Beirut), Anatolia, Egypt, Iraq, Arabia
- **Textiles:** Damask silk (Damascus gave its name to the fabric)
- **Agricultural goods:** Grain, fruit from the Ghouta oasis
- **Hajj pilgrimage:** Damascus as assembly point for the Hajj caravan (*mahmal*) to Mecca—major revenue source
- **Souqs:** Famous covered markets (Khan As'ad Pasha, Souq al-Hamidiyya)

**Aleppo (حلب):**
- **Major trade entrepôt:** Connecting Persia, Iraq, Anatolia, and the Mediterranean
- **Textiles:** Silk, cotton fabrics
- **Spices and goods from Persia and India** transiting to European markets
- **European merchant communities:** French, Venetian, English factors
- **Soap production:** Famous Aleppo soap (olive oil and laurel oil)

**Sidon and Lebanese Coast:**
- **Cotton exports:** From Galilee via Acre and Haifa
- **Silk exports:** From Mount Lebanon via Beirut and Sidon
- **Olive oil**
- **Fruit:** Citrus, figs

**Hauran (Southern Syria):**
- **Grain production:** Wheat and barley
- **Bedouin tribal economy:** Livestock (sheep, goats, camels)

**Trade Routes:**
1. **Mediterranean:** Acre, Beirut, Sidon, Tripoli to European ports (Marseille, Venice, Livorno)
2. **Hajj route:** Damascus–Medina–Mecca (annual pilgrimage caravan)
3. **Overland to Anatolia:** Aleppo–Adana–Konya–Constantinople
4. **Overland to Iraq:** Aleppo–Mosul–Baghdad
5. **Overland to Egypt:** Damascus–Gaza–Cairo
6. **Inland:** Damascus–Aleppo connecting southern and northern Syria

**Economic Summary:**
Greater Syria in 1763 was economically vibrant but politically fragmented. Zahir al-Umar's cotton monopoly in Galilee, Mount Lebanon's silk production, and Damascus/Aleppo's role as trade hubs generated significant wealth. However, power was divided among Ottoman governors, autonomous strongmen, and hereditary enclaves, creating instability that would persist into the 19th century.

## 5. ARABIA (1763)

*This section extends and integrates findings from research/1763_MENA_independent_states_partial.md*

### First Saudi State / Emirate of Diriyah

**Full details in:** research/1763_MENA_independent_states_partial.md, Section 3

**Summary for 1763:**

**Ruler:** Muhammad bin Saud (محمد بن سعود), founder
- **Founded:** 1744 through the Diriyah Pact (ميثاق الدرعية) with Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab
- **Death:** 1765 (succeeded by son Abdul-Aziz bin Muhammad)
- **Capital:** Diriyah (الدرعية) in Najd

**Status in 1763:**
The First Saudi State existed and was expanding, but still faced serious threats. In October 1764 (one year after the game start), the Ismāʿīlī Shīʿa of Najrān allied with the 'Ujman tribe and defeated the Saudis at the Battle of Hair, "killing around 500 men" and besieging Diriyya (though the siege failed).

**Territory:** Najd (central Arabian plateau)—did NOT yet control Hejaz (Mecca/Medina), which remained under Ottoman-appointed Sharifs. Saudi expansion into Hejaz came later (1803–1813).

**Ideology:** The Wahhabi movement (الدعوة الوهابية) sought to purify Islam through monotheism and fighting *shirk* (polytheism/idolatry).

**Major Opponent:** Dahham bin Dawas (دهام بن دواس), emir of Riyadh, fought the Saudis in "17 battles over 27 years."

**CRITICAL DELTA FOR 1763 vs 1815 BOOKMARK:**
- **1763:** First Saudi State exists as independent emirate in Najd
- **1815:** First Saudi State does NOT exist—destroyed by Ottoman-Egyptian forces in 1818
  - Last ruler Abdullah bin Saud executed in Istanbul in December 1818
  - Diriyah demolished by Ibrahim Pasha
  - Second Saudi State would not emerge until 1824

This is the single most important political difference between 1763 and 1815 Arabia.

### Hejaz (Sharifs of Mecca)

**Sharif of Mecca:** Masaad ibn Said (II) (مساعد بن سعيد)
- **Reign:** 1752–1759, 1760–1770 (in power throughout 1763)
- **Dynasty:** Banu Qatadah (descendants of Qatada ibn Idris, held emirate 1201–1925)
- **Lineage:** Hashemite—"always a Hashemite descended from the family of Muhammad"

**Status:** Semi-sovereign under Ottoman suzerainty
- **Ottoman sovereignty accepted:** 1517, when Sharif Barakat recognized the Sultan as Caliph
- **Succession:** "Not hereditary, owed its succession to direct nomination by the Ottoman Porte"
- **Autonomy:** Ottoman authority "only indirect," leaving real power with the Emir
- **Dual government:** Authority shared between Emir and Ottoman officials

**Territorial Control:**
- **Hejaz region:** Mecca (مكة), Medina (المدينة المنورة), Tihamah (coastal plain)
- **Principal cities:** Mecca, Medina, Jeddah (جدة, main port)
- **Population:** Arabs plus non-Arab Muslims (Bukharis, Javanese, Indians, Afghans)

**Economy:**
- **Pilgrimage revenues:** Central to the economy; Hajj pilgrims' spending, gifts, endowments
- **External support:** Holy cities depended on outside aid—Egypt historically supplied "most of their grain"
- **Jeddah port:** Main maritime trade hub on the Hejaz coast; Red Sea trade (coffee from Yemen, spices from India, pilgrims arriving by sea)
- **Pilgrimage security:** Emirs prioritized protecting Hajj caravans (from Damascus, Cairo, Iraq, Yemen)
- **Bedouin tribute and subsidies:** Payments to tribes to ensure safe passage of caravans

**1763 vs 1815 Delta:**
- **1763:** Sharifs governed under Ottoman suzerainty; First Saudi State in Najd had not yet threatened Hejaz
- **1803–1813:** Saudis conquered Mecca and Medina, deposing Sharif Ghalib ibn Musa'id (1803)
- **1812–1813:** Ottomans (via Muhammad Ali Pasha of Egypt) reconquered Hejaz: Medina (1812), Mecca (1813)
- **1815:** Sharifs restored under Ottoman suzerainty but weakened; First Saudi State being destroyed (1811–1818 campaign)

### Oman (Al Bu Said Dynasty)

**Full details in:** research/1763_MENA_independent_states_partial.md, Section 2

**Summary for 1763:**

**Ruler:** Ahmad bin Said Al Bu Said (أحمد بن سعيد البوسعيدي)
- **Elected Imam:** 9 June 1749
- **Ruled from:** Rustaq
- **Reign:** 1749–1783 (approximately 39 years, mostly peaceful)

**Status:** Fully independent; founder of the Al Bu Said dynasty (الأسرة البوسعيدية)

**Territorial Control:**
- **Oman:** Muscat, Rustaq, interior Oman
- **East African coast:** Zanzibar, Pemba, Mafia, Mombasa
  - Omani control established earlier under Yaruba dynasty (Fort Jesus at Mombasa fell to Omanis in 1698)
  - Muhammad (Ahmad's youngest son) later served as Governor-General of Mombasa

**Military and Economic Strength:**
- Built Oman's **first permanent standing army and navy**
- Controlled "trade movement between Africa and Asia"
- Maritime empire dominating western Indian Ocean
- Promoted farming and maritime trade

**Economy:**
- **Muscat:** Major port for Indian Ocean trade (spices, textiles, coffee, dates)
- **Dates:** Major export from interior oases
- **Frankincense and myrrh:** From Dhofar region
- **Maritime trade:** Controlling sea routes between East Africa, Arabia, Persia, and India
- **East African goods:** Ivory, enslaved people, cloves (later, 19th century)

**1763 vs 1815 Delta:**
- **Both dates:** Oman independent under Al Bu Said dynasty
- **Difference:** Ahmad bin Said ruled in 1763; by 1815, his descendants continued the dynasty (Said bin Sultan would rule 1804–1856, moving capital to Zanzibar in 1832)

### Yemen (Qasimid State)

**Full details in:** research/1763_MENA_independent_states_partial.md, Section 4

**Summary for 1763:**

**Ruler:** Imam Al-Mahdi Abbas (الإمام المهدي عباس)
- **Reign:** 1748–1775

**Status:** Independent Zaidi imamate (Qasimid State / الدولة القاسمية)
- Expelled Ottomans from Yemen by 1636
- Fully sovereign throughout 18th century
- Zaidi Shia Islam (different from Twelver Shia)

**Territorial Control:**
- **Capital:** Sana'a (صنعاء)
- **Highland Yemen:** Zaidi-controlled northern highlands
- **Lost territory:** Sultanate of Lahej (سلطنة لحج) broke away and became "completely independent" in 1740
- **Fragmentation:** "The imamate did not follow a cohesive succession mechanism, and family feuds and tribal insubordination led to political decline in the 18th century"

**Economy:**
- **Coffee:** Mocha (المخا) coffee trade—Yemen's famous export to Europe
  - **Economic decline:** Europeans broke Yemen's coffee monopoly in the first half of the 18th century by smuggling coffee plants and cultivating them in colonies (Java, Caribbean, Brazil)
  - By 1763, Yemeni coffee still significant but facing competition
- **Agriculture:** Highland terraced farming (coffee, qat, grains)
- **Red Sea trade:** Mocha port connecting to Egypt, Hejaz, India

**1763 vs 1815 Delta:**
- **Both dates:** Yemen independent under Zaidi imams, but politically fractured
- **1763:** Qasimid State still controlled much of highland Yemen despite internal weakness
- **1815:** Qasimid State remained independent; would not be reincorporated into Ottoman Empire until 1849
- **Difference:** Increasing fragmentation between 1763 and 1815; tribal autonomy grew stronger

## 6. NORTH AFRICA — BARBARY REGENCIES & MOROCCO (1763)

### Algeria (Regency of Algiers)

**Ruler (Dey) in 1763:** Unknown (specific ruler not identified for 1763)
- **Baba Ali Chaouch:** Ruled 1710–1718
- **Baba Mohammed ben-Osman:** Ruled 1766–1791 (began three years after 1763)

**Status:** *De jure* Ottoman eyalet and vassal state; *de facto* independent "military republic" since mid-17th century

**Government Structure:**
After the janissary revolution of 1659, the Regency became a **stratocracy** (military government) led by janissaries and corsairs. Rulers were elected by the *diwan* (council) rather than appointed by the sultan.

**Dey Succession System:**
- After 1671, **deys** led the country, chosen by the diwan
- Non-hereditary, elective system
- Titles: *dey*, *doulateli* ("head of state"), *hakem* ("military ruler")
- The sultan ceased appointing triennial pashas after 1659; the pasha title was retained "as a symbol of Ottoman suzerainty" only
- Violent turnover common—aghas who tried to extend terms were often assassinated

**Military Structure:**
- **Janissary corps (Odjak):** One of the empire's largest outside Constantinople—about **22,000** by mid-17th century
- Recruited from western Anatolia (not via *devshirme* child levy)
- **Corsairs:** Organized into *taife reisi* (council of captains) that funded privateering and guarded its interests against janissaries

**Territorial Extent:**
- Covered the **Central Maghreb** (*El-Maghrib El-Awsat*), roughly modern Algeria
- Borders: Morocco (west), Tunis (east)
- Interior governed by granting substantial autonomy to tribal communities

**Economy:**
- **Corsairing/privateering:** Historically primary revenue source—raiding European shipping, capturing slaves for ransom, plundered goods, tribute from European states seeking safe passage
- **Wheat exports:** By 18th century, as privateering declined, "wheat exports secured Algerian revenues"
- **Mediterranean trade:** Expanding diplomacy and commerce with European states
- **Trans-Saharan trade:** Gold, enslaved people, ivory from sub-Saharan Africa
- **Leather goods:** Algiers known for leather production

**1763 vs 1815 Delta:**
- **Both dates:** De facto independent under deys
- **1815:** Corsairing in decline; European powers (especially post-Napoleonic France) preparing for colonial intervention
- **1830:** France conquered Algiers, ending the Regency

### Tunisia (Regency of Tunis / Husainid Beylik)

**Ruler (Bey) in 1763:** Ali II ibn Hussein (علي الثاني بن حسين)
- **Reign:** 11 February 1759 – 26 May 1782 (in power throughout 1763)

**Dynasty:** Husainid dynasty (الأسرة الحسينية)
- **Founded:** 15 July 1705 by al-Husayn I ibn Ali
- **Predecessor:** Muradid dynasty
- **Succession rule:** Theoretically male primogeniture, though not consistently observed
- **Heir apparent title:** Bey al-Mahalla (led twice-yearly tax-gathering expedition)

**Status:** Ottoman eyalet under nominal suzerainty, but **de facto autonomous dynasty**

**Relationship with Ottoman Empire:**
- Ottomans officially treated beys as **beylerbeyis** (provincial governors)
- Acknowledged "their rights to hereditary succession"
- Despite nominal Ottoman oversight, beys governed largely independently
- **Independent diplomacy:** Negotiated own diplomatic arrangements with European nations (France, England, Italian states)

**Territorial Extent:**
- **Tunisia region:** Tunis (capital), Sfax, Sousse, Kairouan, Bizerte, Djerba island
- Boundaries: Algeria (west), Tripolitania (east)

**Economy:**
- **Corsairing:** Tunis participated in Barbary corsair raids, though to a lesser extent than Algiers
- **Grain exports:** Wheat and barley to European markets
- **Olive oil:** Major export from Tunisian groves
- **Trans-Saharan trade:** Gold, enslaved people, ivory transiting through Tunisia from interior Africa
- **Dates:** From Saharan oases (Tozeur, Nefta)
- **European trade:** French, Italian, British merchant communities in Tunis and coastal ports

**1763 vs 1815 Delta:**
- **Both dates:** Husainid dynasty autonomous under nominal Ottoman suzerainty
- **1815:** European economic influence growing; French protectorate would follow in 1881

### Tripolitania (Regency of Tripoli / Karamanli Dynasty)

**Ruler (Pasha) in 1763:** Ali I Karamanli (علي باشا القرمانلي)
- **Reign:** 1754–1793

**Dynasty:** Karamanli dynasty (الأسرة القرمانلية)
- **Founded:** 1711 by Ahmed Karamanli, a janissary and cavalry officer who "murdered the Ottoman governor of Tripolitania and seized the throne" in a coup
- **Succession:** Hereditary (Ahmed made his position hereditary)
- **Duration:** 1711–1835

**Status:** Autonomous dynasty ruling Ottoman Tripolitania
- After Ahmed persuaded the Ottomans to recognize him as governor, the region "otherwise acted as an independent kingdom"
- Paid only **nominal tribute** to the Ottoman padishah

**Territorial Control:**
- **Core:** Tripolitania (coastal Libya around Tripoli)
- **At height:** Extended to Cyrenaica (eastern Libya) and Fezzan (southern Libya)
- Covered most of present-day Libya

**Economy:**
- **Corsairing:** Ahmed Karamanli grew the economy largely through Mediterranean corsairs
- **Tribute from European states:** Nations wanting protected shipping had to "pay tribute to the pasha"
- **Trans-Saharan trade:** Routes toward Kanem–Bornu (in present-day Chad/Nigeria region) became important
- **Slave trade:** Gained prominence, especially later under Yusuf (1795–1832)
- **Dates:** From Fezzan oases

**Notable Later Events:**
- **Yusuf Karamanli (1795–1832):** Most famous ruler, fought First Barbary War with the United States (1801–1805)
- **1819 onward:** Economic decline after suppression of piracy by European powers
- **1832:** Yusuf abdicated to Ali II; civil war ensued
- **1835:** Ottoman Sultan Mahmud II intervened, deposed Ali II, ending the dynasty and Tripolitanian independence

**1763 vs 1815 Delta:**
- **1763:** Ali I Karamanli in power; corsairing economy thriving
- **1815:** Yusuf Karamanli ruled (1795–1832); corsairing in decline after Napoleonic Wars and U.S./European suppression
- **1835:** Ottomans reimposed direct control

### Morocco (Independent Alaouite Sultanate)

**Full details in:** research/1763_MENA_independent_states_partial.md, Section 1

**Summary for 1763:**

**Sultan:** Mohammed III / Sidi Muhammad ibn Abdallah (محمد بن عبد الله)
- **Dynasty:** Alaouite (الدولة العلوية)
- **Reign:** 1757–1790

**Status:** Fully independent—"the only North African nation to escape Ottoman dominion"
- Alawi dynasty resisted foreign control since seizing power in 1631
- Mawlay Isma'il (1672–1727) "put a definitive end to Ottoman attempts to gain influence in Morocco"

**Capitals:** Marrakesh and Fez (dual capitals historically)

**Key Developments (around 1763):**
- **1760–1764:** Founded Essaouira (الصويرة / Mogador) fortified port city, commissioning French engineer Théodore Cornut; completed by 1769–1770
- **1767:** Closed Agadir's harbor, redirecting European trade toward Essaouira
- **Governance:** Pursued decentralized governance and lighter taxation, relying on European trade revenue

**Territorial Control:**
- **Core Morocco:** Atlantic coast, Rif Mountains, Atlas Mountains, Anti-Atlas
- **Contested zones:** Saharan fringes with tribal autonomy
- **Not under Ottoman control:** Unique among North African states

**Economy:**
- **European trade:** Atlantic ports (Essaouira, Tangier, Salé, Rabat)
- **Grain exports:** Wheat and barley
- **Leather:** Famous Moroccan leather goods
- **Sugar:** Limited production in southern regions
- **Trans-Saharan trade:** Gold, enslaved people, salt caravans from Timbuktu/Sahel regions
- **Corsairing:** Salé corsairs (though declining by mid-18th century)

**1763 vs 1815 Delta:**
- **Both dates:** Morocco fully independent under Alaouite dynasty
- **1763:** Mohammed III ruling (1757–1790)
- **1815:** Sulayman ibn Mohammed ruling (1792–1822)

## 7. PERSIA (1763)

### Zand Dynasty and Karim Khan Zand

**Ruler:** Karim Khan Zand (کریم خان زند, c. 1705–1779)
- **Title:** *Vakil ol-Ra'aya* (وکیل الرعایا, "Deputy of the People" / "Representative of the People")
  - **Notably refused** the title "Shah," adopting a humbler designation
- **Reign:** 1751–1779 (in power throughout 1763)
- **Dynasty:** Zand dynasty (زندیان), founded 1751
- **Origins:** From the Zand tribe of the Laks (Kurdish/Lur ethnicity)
- **Religion:** Twelver Shia Islam

**Capital:** Shiraz (شیراز)
- Karim Khan made Shiraz the capital and extensively rebuilt it
- Population grew to roughly 40,000–50,000, surpassing Isfahan
- Major construction projects: castle, gardens, mosques, city walls, baths, caravanserai, bazaar
- Renovated the tombs of Hafez and Saadi (famous Persian poets)
- Attracted poets, craftsmen, and foreign traders

**Territorial Control in 1763:**
- **Core Persia:** Nearly all of Iran except Khorasan and Balochistan
- **Exclusions:** 
  - **Khorasan:** Held by Shahrokh Shah (Afsharid remnant) in Mashhad
  - **Balochistan:** Not under Zand control
- **Brief expansion:** Some Caucasian lands; temporarily held Basra (Iraq)
- Did not subdue Khorasan throughout his reign

**Governance Style:**
- Deliberately small bureaucracy
- **Personal rule:** Karim Khan personally handled state affairs, limiting power of vizier and chief revenue officer
- **Provincial administration:** Followed Safavid model
  - *Beglerbegis* (governors)
  - *Kalantar* (mayor)
  - *Darugha* (police chief)
  - *Kadkhuda* (neighborhood headman)
- **Tribal administration:** Created new posts
  - *Ilkhani* for Lur tribes
  - *Ilbegi* for Qashqai tribes
- **Religious policy:** Unlike Safavids, did not seek *ulama* (clerical) approval for authority

**Character and Rule:**
Contemporary and historical sources praise Karim Khan for:
- **Generosity, modesty, and fairness**
- Wore simple clothing, sat on an inexpensive carpet rather than a throne
- Sold crushed jewels to stabilize the treasury during fiscal crises
- The Oxford Dictionary of Islam calls him "**the most humane Iranian ruler of the Islamic era**"
- John Malcolm admired that he "obtained power without crime" and ruled with humanity and justice
- His street name in Shiraz survived the 1979 Islamic Revolution's taboo on past rulers

**Condition of Persia under Karim Khan:**
- Iran recovered from roughly 40 years of devastation following Nader Shah's death (1747)
- The country gained renewed "tranquillity, security, peace, and prosperity"
- **Peak of Zand rule:** 1765–1779 (golden period)
- Restored relations with Britain; permitted East India Company a southern trading post
- Economic revival centered on Shiraz as a commercial and cultural hub

**Death and Succession:**
- Died 1 March 1779
- Followed by civil war among successors
- No descendant matched his effectiveness
- Last Zand ruler: Lotf Ali Khan (1789–1794), the only one to use the title "Shah of Iran"
- Dynasty destroyed by Agha Mohammad Khan Qajar in 1794

**1763 Status of Karim Khan:**
By 1763, Karim Khan had been ruling for 12 years and had consolidated his power. That year (1763), he brought **Agha Mohammad Khan Qajar** and Hosayn Qoli Khan to his capital—the Qajar hostage who would later overthrow the Zand dynasty and found the Qajar dynasty that ruled in 1815.

### Territorial Fragmentation Post-Nader Shah

**Background: Nader Shah's Empire and Death**
- **Nader Shah** (r. 1736–1747) of the Afsharid dynasty conquered vast territories: Persia, Afghanistan, parts of the Caucasus, Central Asia, and briefly sacked Delhi (1739)
- **Assassination:** 1747, followed by immediate fragmentation of his empire

**Competing Powers in 1763:**

**1. Zand Dynasty (Central and Southern Persia):**
- **Karim Khan Zand** controlled most of Persia (see above)
- Core regions: Fars, Isfahan, Kerman, Azerbaijan, Luristan, Khuzestan
- Capital: Shiraz

**2. Afsharid Remnant (Khorasan):**
- **Shahrokh Shah** (شاهرخ شاه, 1734–1796), grandson of Nader Shah
- **First reign:** October 1748 – January 1750
- **Second reign:** 20 March 1750 – 1796 (in power throughout 1763)
- **Capital:** Mashhad (مشهد)
- **Territory:** Western Khorasan (roughly modern Khorasan Province)
- **Status:** Largely a figurehead; real power held by Kurdish, Arab, and Turkic tribal chieftains
- **Legitimacy claim:** Half-Safavid descent (mother was Safavid princess Fatemeh Soltan Begom), used to bolster dynastic legitimacy
- **Religious policy:** Championed Twelver Shi'ism, unlike his grandfather
- **External relations:**
  - **Ahmad Shah Durrani (Afghanistan):** Invaded Khorasan multiple times; after second campaign, "on 9 May 1755 Shahrokh Shah was officially restored as king"—effectively a Durrani client
  - **Karim Khan Zand:** Shahrokh remained independent of Zand control; Karim Khan never subdued Khorasan
- **Blinded during power struggles** but restored to throne
- **Death:** 1796, when Agha Mohammad Khan Qajar conquered Khorasan and tortured him to extract Nader's hidden treasures; "died at Damghan due to injuries"

**3. Other Regional Powers (not controlled by Zand or Afsharid):**
- **Balochistan:** Autonomous tribal regions
- **Afghan territories:** Under Ahmad Shah Durrani (Durrani Empire, 1747–1826)
- **Caucasus:** Parts contested or lost to Ottoman Empire and Russia

**Summary of Fragmentation (1763):**
Persia was divided into two main spheres:
1. **Zand (majority):** Most of Persia under Karim Khan Zand's stable, peaceful rule
2. **Afsharid (Khorasan):** Shahrokh Shah ruling Mashhad as Durrani client

This fragmentation would end with the Qajar conquest (1789–1794), reunifying Persia under a new dynasty.

### Economy by Location

**Shiraz (شیراز) — Zand Capital:**
- **Political and commercial center** under Karim Khan
- **Trade hub:** Attracted foreign traders (British East India Company permitted southern trading post)
- **Carpets:** Shiraz famous for Persian carpets and rugs
- **Wine:** Shiraz region historically known for wine (pre-Islamic tradition continued in some communities)
- **Population:** 40,000–50,000 by late 18th century (surpassing Isfahan)
- **Cultural center:** Poets, craftsmen, scholars attracted to Karim Khan's patronage
- **Construction boom:** Castle, gardens, mosques, bazaar, caravanserais built under Karim Khan

**Isfahan (اصفهان) — Former Safavid Capital:**
- **Status in 1763:** Declined from its Safavid-era glory but still significant
- **Carpets:** Isfahan carpets among the finest in the world (silk and wool)
- **Textiles:** Silk and cotton production
- **Metalwork:** Copper and brass crafts
- **Trade:** Still on routes connecting Gulf, Shiraz, Tehran, and the north

**Kerman (کرمان):**
- **Carpets:** Kerman carpets famous for intricate designs
- **Textiles:** Wool and cotton
- **Agricultural products:** Pistachios (Kerman pistachios renowned)
- **Trade routes:** Connecting southeastern Persia to Shiraz and the Gulf

**Gilan (گیلان) Province — Caspian Coast:**
- **Silk production:** Gilan was Persia's primary silk-producing region
- **Mulberry groves** supporting silkworm cultivation
- **Major export:** Silk exported via Caspian Sea (to Russia) and overland routes
- **Rice cultivation:** Caspian lowlands ideal for rice
- **Tea:** Limited cultivation (expanded later)
- **Humid climate:** Supporting agriculture distinct from arid plateau

**Bushehr (بوشهر) — Persian Gulf Port:**
- **Primary Gulf port** for Persia in the 18th century
- **Trade hub:** Connecting Persia to Oman, India, East Africa, and Arabian coast
- **Imports:** Spices, textiles, coffee, Indian Ocean goods
- **Exports:** Silk, carpets, dried fruits, horses
- **British East India Company presence:** Permitted by Karim Khan to establish trading post in southern Persia (likely Bushehr or nearby)
- **Pearl trade:** Transiting through Bushehr from Gulf pearl fisheries

**Mashhad (مشهد) — Afsharid Capital in Khorasan:**
- **Shia pilgrimage center:** Shrine of Imam Reza (eighth Twelver Shia Imam)
- **Pilgrimage revenues:** Major economic base
- **Trade routes:** Connecting Persia to Central Asia (Bukhara, Samarkand) and Afghanistan (Herat)
- **Religious endowments (*waqf*):** Supporting theological schools and local economy
- **Turquoise:** Nishapur (near Mashhad) famous for turquoise mines

**Tabriz (تبریز) — Azerbaijan:**
- **Major trade city** in northwestern Persia
- **Silk Road hub:** Connecting Persia to Anatolia, Caucasus, and Europe
- **Carpets:** Tabriz carpets famous for medallion designs
- **Textiles and metalwork**
- **Agricultural goods:** From Azerbaijan's fertile valleys

**Khuzestan (خوزستان) — Southwest Persia:**
- **Dates:** From palmeries along rivers
- **Sugar cane:** Limited cultivation
- **Strategic location:** Near Basra (Iraq) and Persian Gulf
- **Trade routes:** Connecting interior Persia to Gulf

**Trade Routes (1763):**

1. **Silk Road (overland):**
   - Persia to Central Asia (Bukhara, Samarkand) via Mashhad
   - Persia to Anatolia via Tabriz and Azerbaijan
   - Persia to Syria via Kermanshah and Baghdad

2. **Persian Gulf (maritime):**
   - Bushehr to Muscat (Oman), Bombay (India), Basra (Iraq), East Africa
   - Exports: Silk, carpets, dried fruits (dates, figs, apricots, pistachios), horses
   - Imports: Spices, coffee, textiles, indigo, pearls

3. **Caspian Sea (northern):**
   - Gilan to Russia (Astrakhan)
   - Silk exports to Russia and beyond

4. **Internal:**
   - Shiraz–Isfahan–Tehran–Tabriz (north-south axis)
   - Kerman–Shiraz–Bushehr (southeast to Gulf)

**Primary Economic Products:**

- **Silk:** Gilan province (major export)
- **Carpets:** Shiraz, Isfahan, Kerman, Tabriz (world-renowned Persian carpets)
- **Dried fruits:** Dates, figs, apricots, raisins, pistachios
- **Horses:** Persian and Arabian horses for export
- **Textiles:** Silk and cotton fabrics
- **Metalwork:** Copper, brass crafts
- **Turquoise:** Nishapur mines

**Economic Condition under Karim Khan:**
- **Recovery from 40 years of devastation** following Nader Shah's death and civil wars
- **Peace and prosperity (1751–1779):** Stable rule, low taxation, encouragement of trade
- **British East India Company relations restored:** Trading post permitted
- **Shiraz as economic hub:** Surpassed Isfahan as commercial center
- **Agricultural revival:** Karim Khan promoted farming
- **Tax policies:** Light taxation to encourage economic activity

**1763 vs 1815 Delta (PERSIA — MAJOR CHANGE):**

This is one of the most significant political differences between the two bookmarks:

**1763:**
- **Zand dynasty** ruled by Karim Khan Zand
- **Capital:** Shiraz
- **Peaceful, prosperous Persia** in recovery phase
- Khorasan under Afsharid Shahrokh

**1815:**
- **Qajar dynasty** ruled by Fath-Ali Shah Qajar (r. 1797–1834)
- **Capital:** Tehran (تهران)—a completely different city
- **Unified Persia** (Khorasan reintegrated)
- **Different ruling family, different governance style**
- Zand dynasty completely destroyed (1794)

This means the 1763 bookmark must represent the Zand dynasty, NOT the Qajars who ruled in 1815.

## 9. OTTOMAN VASSAL STATES (1763)

**Full details in:** research/1763_rulers_crimea_ottoman_vassals.md

This section summarizes key findings from that research file.

### Crimean Khanate

**Khan:** Qırım Giray (قرم كراى)
- **First reign:** 1758–1764 (in power throughout 1763)
- **Dynasty:** Giray dynasty (exclusively appointed from this family)
- **Second reign:** 1768–1769 (later)

**Status:** Ottoman vassal with exceptional autonomy
- **Scholarly characterization:** "Privileged vassal status" (Kolodziejczyk)—nuanced between vassalage and independence
- **Appointment:** Ottoman Sultan had full appointment and removal authority by 1763 (after 1584), selecting exclusively from Giray dynasty
- **Berat:** Khan required formal investiture from Sultan

**Unusual Vassal Model:**
- **NO regular tribute paid to Ottomans** (contrary to typical vassalage)
- **Reverse financial flow:** Ottomans paid subsidies and gifts to the Khanate
- **Military service exchange:** Primary obligation was providing light cavalry for Ottoman campaigns

**Military Obligations:**
- Crimean Tatar cavalry considered **"indispensable" (vazgeçilemez)** for Ottoman military operations
- Deployed in campaigns against Russia, Poland-Lithuania, Hungary, Austria, Persia
- Large contingents: tens of thousands of cavalry for major campaigns
- Famous for reconnaissance, raiding, and harassment tactics
- **Independent slave raids** into Eastern Europe (Ukraine, Poland, Russia) served Ottoman strategic interests

**Autonomy:**
- **High internal autonomy:** Full control over domestic administration, justice, taxation
- **Religious affairs:** Independent; Khan held title "Caliph of the North"
- **Economic policy:** Largely autonomous; controlled Black Sea slave trade
- **Constraints:** Limited foreign policy (especially with Russia required Ottoman coordination); Ottoman military presence in coastal fortresses (e.g., Caffa/Kefe)

**1763 vs 1815 Delta:**
- **1763:** Crimean Khanate exists as Ottoman vassal
- **1783:** Russia annexed Crimea
- **1815:** Crimean Khanate DOES NOT EXIST—absorbed into Russian Empire

### Danubian Principalities (Moldavia & Wallachia)

**Phanariote System:** After 1711 (Moldavia) and 1715 (Wallachia), Ottomans abandoned local elective systems and appointed **Phanariote Greeks** from Constantinople through a bribery-based system.

#### Moldavia

**Hospodar:** Grigore Callimachi (Γρηγόριος Καλλιμάχης)
- **First reign:** 1761–1764 (in power throughout 1763)
- **Second reign:** 1767–1769
- **Family:** Callimachi family (Phanariote Greeks)

**Status:** Ottoman vassal with high internal autonomy

**Appointment Process:**
- Candidates typically served as **chief dragoman of the Porte** (skilled in Ottoman statecraft)
- Positions **purchased through bribery**—"in exchange for a generous bribe"
- Hospodars often arrived not speaking Romanian, with retinues of family, favorites, and creditors
- Over Phanariote epoch, **31 princes from 11 families** ruled the two principalities
- Sometimes shuffled between Moldavia and Wallachia

**Tribute:** Moldavia paid tribute directly to the Porte; levels "directly proportional with Ottoman requests"; declined significantly after 1774 Treaty of Küçük Kaynarca (to ~3,100 gold coins by 1820s)

**Autonomy:**
- Retained internal self-government: own princes, courts, boyar councils
- Full control over domestic administration and justice
- Autonomous in religious affairs (Orthodox Christian)

**Constraints:**
- Princely appointments entirely dependent on Ottoman Sultan's decision
- Could not conduct independent foreign policy
- Required to participate in Ottoman military campaigns when requested
- **Russian intervention rights** established after 1774 Treaty

#### Wallachia

**Hospodar:** Constantin Mavrocordat (Κωνσταντίνος Μαυροκορδάτος)
- **Reign:** 1761–1763 (in power during February 1763; reign ending that year)
- **Family:** Mavrocordato family (most famous Phanariote hospodars)
- **Achievement:** Abolished serfdom in Wallachia (1746)

**Status:** Ottoman vassal with high internal autonomy

**Appointment Process:** Same bribery-based system as Moldavia
- **Wallachia commanded higher bribes** than Moldavia (wealthier province)
- Chief dragomans of the Porte primary candidates
- Positions financed by creditors, creating debt obligations

**Tribute:** Wallachia paid tribute to the Porte; dropped from ~50,000 to ~20,000 gold coins by 1820s after 1774 Treaty

**Autonomy:** Same high degree of internal autonomy as Moldavia

**Constraints:** Same as Moldavia—no independent foreign policy, military obligations, princely succession controlled by Sultan

### Comparative Notes: Crimea vs. Danubian Principalities

| Aspect | Crimean Khanate | Moldavia/Wallachia |
|--------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Appointment** | Sultan selected from Giray dynasty | Sultan appointed Phanariote Greeks via bribery |
| **Tribute** | None (reverse flow: Ottomans paid Crimea) | Regular tribute to Porte |
| **Military Obligation** | Massive cavalry contingents (primary obligation) | Participation when requested |
| **Internal Autonomy** | Very high | Very high |
| **Foreign Policy** | Constrained (Russia only with Ottoman approval) | Entirely controlled by Ottomans |
| **Religious Status** | Muslim (Khan = "Caliph of the North") | Orthodox Christian |
| **Economic Relationship** | Military service exchange | Tributary/fiscal extraction |
| **Ruler Ethnicity** | Crimean Tatar (Giray dynasty) | Greek (Phanariote families) |

**1763 vs 1815 Delta:**
- **Crimean Khanate:** Exists 1763, **ABSENT 1815** (annexed by Russia 1783)
- **Moldavia/Wallachia:** Vassal status continues 1763→1815, but **Russian influence increases** after 1774 Treaty of Küçük Kaynarca, creating dual Ottoman-Russian pressure by 1815
