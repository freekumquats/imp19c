# Sub-Saharan Africa: 16 February 1763

## Overview

This document covers the political, economic, and social landscape of Sub-Saharan Africa on **16 February 1763**, during the Seven Years' War (which ended with the Treaty of Paris, 10 February 1763—just six days before our target date). This research supports retargeting the Imperatrix: Victoria mod from its current 1815 start to 1763.

**Key Delta vs 1815**: The year 1763 precedes major transformations: the Sokoto Caliphate (founded 1804 via Fulani jihad), the Zemene Mesafint in Ethiopia (begins ~1769), British control of the Cape (1806/1814), the Zulu kingdom (emerges c.1816), and European interior colonization (post-1880s Scramble for Africa). In 1763, African polities remain sovereign and expansive, the Atlantic slave trade is at its 18th-century peak, and European presence is confined to coastal forts and the Cape.

|--------|---------------|--------------|-------|
| **Asante Empire** (Ashanti) | Kumasi | **Osei Kwadwo** (r. 1764–1777) OR **Kusi Obodom** (regent, d. 1764) | Asante was in transition: Asantehene Kusi Obodom died 1764; Osei Kwadwo succeeded. Wilks (1975) places Kusi Obodom as effective ruler in early 1763. Empire expanding; Golden Stool as legitimizing symbol. |

| **Kingdom of Dahomey** | Abomey | **Tegbesu** (r. 1740–1774) | Tributary to Oyo; annual tribute paid. Major slave-trade participant (Ouidah port); Amazonian warrior corps. Bay (1998) details Tegbesu's reign. |
| **Segu Bamana (Bambara) Empire** | Segu | **Ngolo Diarra** (r. 1766–1790?) OR late **Biton Coulibaly dynasty** | Transition period: Biton Coulibaly's dynasty ending; Ngolo Diarra founds new line mid-1760s. Roberts (1987) on Bambara states. |

**European Coastal Presence (1763)**: Forts only—no territorial control.
- **Gold Coast**: British (Cape Coast Castle), Dutch (Elmina, since 1637), Danish (Christiansborg/Osu), scattered forts. Competition for gold and slaves.
- **Slave Coast** (Bight of Benin): Ouidah (Dahomey's port); European factories (French, Portuguese, British). Porto-Novo.
- **Senegambia**: French Saint-Louis (Senegal River); British Fort James (Gambia River, ceded to Britain 1763 at Paris).

**Delta vs 1815**: In 1815, Oyo is in decline (internal strife, Fulani pressure from Sokoto Caliphate, founded 1804). Dahomey independent (Oyo tributary relationship weakened). Asante still powerful but facing British coastal opposition. **1763 Hausa states** (below) are independent; by 1815 they are subsumed into the **Sokoto Caliphate**.

| **Funj Sultanate of Sennar** | Sennar (Blue Nile) | **Badi IV Abu Shulukh** (r. 1724–1762) OR early **Nasir** (r. 1762–1769) | 1763 is transition: Badi IV died 1762; Nasir ruled briefly. Funj Sultanate (1504–1821) was a Sudanese Islamic state. Spaulding (1985). |

**Trans-Saharan Trade (1763)**: Salt (Taoudenni mines), gold (from Bambuk, Bure), slaves, kola nuts, textiles. Routes through Timbuktu, Gao, Agadez to North Africa (Tripoli, Tunis, Cairo). Hausa cities (Kano, Katsina) were entrepôts. Austen (2010) on trans-Saharan economies.

**Delta vs 1815**: By 1815, the **Sokoto Caliphate** (1804–1903) has conquered most Hausa states and neighboring regions, creating the largest state in West Africa. Usman dan Fodio's jihad (1804–1808) and successor emirates (Kano, Katsina, Zaria, etc.) under Sokoto suzerainty. In 1763, this polity does not exist; Hausa states are independent and often rival. **Map impact**: 1763 shows fragmented Hausa landscape; 1815 shows unified Sokoto Caliphate.

Omani Zanzibar ascendant (Said bin Sultan r. 1804–1856 moves capital to Zanzibar 1832). **Buganda** more powerful by 1815. Merina Kingdom (Madagascar) emerges as major power (Radama I, r. 1810–1828)—not prominent in 1763.

| **Lunda Empire** | Musumba (Mwant Yav's capital) | **Mwant Yav** (title of king) | Large empire (modern Angola/DRC/Zambia); tributary network; trade (slaves, ivory, copper). Vansina (1966). |

Ivory from interior. Copper from Katanga region (Luba/Lunda).

**Delta vs 1815**: Kongo further fragmented by 1815 (Portuguese control nominal). Angola's slave trade peaks in late 18thC, begins decline post-1815 (British abolitionist pressure; Brazil independence 1822). Rozvi Empire collapses in 1830s (Nguni invasions from south). Lunda/Luba still major in 1815 but facing new pressures (Chokwe expansion, Ovimbundu traders).

**Delta vs 1815**: British control Cape from 1806/1814. **Mfecane** begins (~1810s–1830s): Zulu expansion under Shaka (r. 1816–1828) causes mass migrations, state formation (Basotho under Moshoeshoe, Matabele/Ndebele under Mzilikazi, Swazi, Gaza). In 1763, none of this exists; southern Africa is relatively decentralized. **Major map difference**: no Zulu kingdom, no derivative states, British not Dutch.

## VII. ECONOMY BY REGION (c. 1763)

### A. Atlantic Slave Trade (Peak Period)

**Volume (1763)**: The Atlantic slave trade reached its 18th-century zenith in the 1760s–1780s. Estimated **~80,000 slaves/year** embarked from Africa in the 1760s (Eltis & Richardson 2010, *Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database*).

**Major Export Regions & Ports (1763)**:
1. **West-Central Africa** (Angola, Congo coast): ~40–50% of total volume. Ports: **Luanda**, **Benguela**, **Cabinda**, Loango. Destinations: Brazil (majority), Caribbean. Miller (1988) on Angola; slaves sourced from interior (Lunda, Luba, Ovimbundu raids).
2. **Bight of Benin** (Slave Coast: Dahomey, Oyo territories): ~15–20%. Ports: **Ouidah** (Dahomey), **Lagos**, Porto-Novo. Dahomey's Amazonian corps raided for slaves. Destinations: Brazil, Caribbean (Saint-Domingue). Law (1991).
3. **Gold Coast** (modern Ghana): ~10–15%. Ports: Anomabo, Cape Coast, Elmina. Asante expansion fed trade. Destinations: Caribbean, North America.
4. **Senegambia**: ~5%. Ports: Gorée (French), Fort James/Gambia. Smaller volume; competed with trans-Saharan routes.
5. **Southeast Africa** (Mozambique, Kilwa): ~5–10%. Ports: **Kilwa**, **Mozambique Island**, Inhambane. Destinations: Mascarene Islands (Réunion, Mauritius), Brazil. Alpers (2009).

**Economic Impact**: Depopulation of interior regions; enrichment of coastal/middleman states (Dahomey, Asante, Lunda); firearms imports fueled further raiding; agricultural disruption. Lovejoy (2012) estimates 12 million enslaved Africans forcibly transported across Atlantic (1500–1867), with 18thC as peak century.

### B. Gold Trade

**Gold Coast (Asante Empire)**: Asante controlled gold-producing regions (Akan goldfields). Exported via European forts (Cape Coast, Elmina). Wilks (1993) on Asante gold; some gold diverted to trans-Saharan routes.

**Bambuk & Bure** (upper Senegal/Niger): Gold fields controlled by Bambara states, Manding; exported north across Sahara to Maghreb. Declining by 1763 compared to earlier centuries.

**Zimbabwe Plateau (Rozvi)**: Gold trade much reduced from earlier (Mutapa) era; Portuguese competition, trade-route shifts.

### C. Ivory Trade

**East Africa**: Swahili coast (Kilwa, Mombasa) exported ivory to Oman, India, Persia, China. Yao and Makua hunters (interior). Sheriff (1987).

**West-Central Africa**: Angola, Lunda/Luba regions; some ivory via Luanda/Benguela to Europe. Secondary to slaves.

**Southern Africa**: Trekboers, Xhosa, San hunted elephants; ivory exported via Cape or traded north.

### D. Trans-Saharan Trade

**Commodities (1763)**:
- **Salt**: Taoudenni mines (modern Mali) → Timbuktu → distributed south. Essential for Sudanic diets.
- **Gold**: From Bambuk, Bure → north to Maghreb (Tripoli, Tunis, Morocco).
- **Slaves**: Sahel raids (Bornu, Hausa states, Wadai) → Fezzan → Tripoli/Tunis → Ottoman Empire. Austen (2010) estimates ~5,000–10,000 slaves/year across Sahara (18thC).
- **Kola nuts**: From forest zones (Asante, Kong) → Hausaland, Sahel (stimulant; Islamic societies).
- **Textiles**: North African cloth, European imports → West Africa. Opposite flow: West African cotton cloth.

**Key Routes**:
- **Timbuktu–Taoudenni–Morocco**
- **Kano–Agadez–Tripoli** (Hausa; major commercial artery)
- **Bornu–Fezzan–Tripoli**
- **Darfur–Nile Valley–Cairo**

Decline accelerates post-1800 due to Atlantic trade dominance, Saharan insecurity (Tuareg, Toubou), French colonial disruption (19thC).

### E. Cape Colony (VOC)

**Economy (1763)**: Provisioning station for VOC ships (Dutch East Indies–Europe route). Exports:
- **Wine**: Constantia wine (sweet dessert wine) famous in Europe. Produced by settlers (Simon van der Stel's estate, 1685).
- **Wheat**: For VOC ships, local consumption.
- **Meat**: Cattle, sheep (from trekboer pastoralists, Khoikhoi trade/raiding).

**Labor**: Slaves imported from Madagascar, East Africa (Mozambique), Indonesia (VOC territories). Khoikhoi dispossessed, employed as laborers; San marginalized. Penn (2005), Ross (1999) on Cape slavery.

**Delta vs 1815**: By 1815, British control brings shifts (ending VOC monopoly, opening to free trade, British settlers begin arriving 1820). Wine trade continues (Constantia prized by Napoleon).

### F. Ethiopian Highlands

**Economy (1763)**: Agriculture-based; **coffee** (native to Ethiopia, though major export era is 19th–20thC), grains (teff, barley, wheat), livestock. Trade via Red Sea ports (Massawa—under Ottoman/Egyptian control, leased to Ethiopian emperors). Crummey (2000). Coffee to Yemen; slaves, ivory, hides exported. Internal trade (salt bars as currency).

## IX. SUMMARY FOR MOD IMPLEMENTATION

### Core 1763 Political Map vs 1815

1. **Hausaland**: Replace unified Sokoto Caliphate (1815) with **independent Hausa city-states** (Kano, Katsina, Zazzau, Gobir, Daura, Rano, Biram). Each should be a separate polity on the 1763 map.
2. **Southern Africa**: **Dutch Cape Colony** (not British). No Zulu kingdom, no Basotho/Ndebele/Swazi states; fragmented Nguni/Sotho-Tswana chiefdoms instead.
3. **Ethiopia**: Centralized under **Emperor Iyoas I** (though weakening); not the Zemene Mesafint warlord fragmentation of 1815.
4. **Oyo Empire**: At territorial zenith; **Dahomey as tributary** (not independent).
5. **East Africa**: Omani control of Swahili coast, but **Zanzibar not capital** (Muscat is).

### Economic Focuses (1763)

- **Atlantic slave trade**: Luanda/Benguela (Angola), Ouidah (Dahomey), Gold Coast, Kilwa (E. Africa).
- **Gold**: Asante (Gold Coast), declining Saharan routes.
- **Trans-Saharan trade**: Kano–Tripoli, Timbuktu, salt/slaves/kola.
- **Cape**: VOC wine (Constantia), wheat, provisioning.
- **Ivory**: Swahili coast, southern Africa.

### Rulers (Key 1763 Figures for Events/Characters)

- **Asante**: Kusi Obodom (regent, d. 1764) or Osei Kwadwo (successor 1764).
