# Sweden in 1763: Ruler, Politics, and Territorial Status

**Bookmark date:** 1763.2.11 (day after Treaty of Paris; Pomeranian War ended 22 May 1762 via Treaty of Hamburg)

## Ruler and Political Authority

**King:** Adolf Frederick (Adolf Fredrik) of Sweden, reign 1751–1771

**Actual power structure:** Adolf Frederick reigns as figurehead during the Age of Liberty (1719–1772). Real authority rests with the **Riksdag of the Estates** (parliament), composed of four estates: nobles, clergy, burghers, and peasants. The king chairs a 16-member Privy Council with only two votes plus a tie-breaker—a constitutional arrangement designed to subordinate the crown.

**Factional politics (Hats vs. Caps):**

- **The Hats** (Mössor): Dominated politics in 1763. Mercantilist, pro-French faction. Sought to restore Sweden to great-power status; received French financial backing. Prosecuted the disastrous Russo-Swedish War (1741–1743) and committed Sweden to the Pomeranian theater in the Seven Years' War.

- **The Caps** (Kläder): Conservative faction initially led by Arvid Horn. Advocated caution, peace, and fiscal restraint. Gained Riksdag control in the 1765–1766 parliament, implementing austerity reforms ("Reduction Riksdag").

**Royal autonomy attempts:** Adolf Frederick twice attempted to escape Estates control: the failed Coup of 1756 (which nearly cost him his throne) and the December Crisis of 1768. Between those moments, he remained "a mere constitutional figurehead." By 1763, the Hat faction retained parliamentary control despite financial stagnation.

**Coming transition (note for later dates):** Gustav III (crown prince, b. 1746) would execute the "Revolution of 1772"—a self-coup restoring effective royal power, ending the Age of Liberty. In 1763, Gustav is a child; real politics remains Riksdag-Hats-vs-Caps.

**Cultural/media context:** Sweden enacted the landmark 1766 **Ordinance on Freedom of the Press**, abolishing censorship and opening public documents to inspection (with carve-outs for Lutheran religious writings). By 1763, the precedent is set—Sweden is "the most liberal power on the European continent, second only to Great Britain."

## Swedish Pomerania (1763)

**Territorial extent:** Western Pomerania north of the Peene River, plus the island of Rügen. This reflects the post-1720 truncation following the Great Northern War.

**Capital:** Stralsund (shifted from Stettin in 1720)

**Other major cities:** Greifswald

**How acquired:** Treaty of Stettin (1630, confirmed at Peace of Westphalia 1648 and Treaty of Stettin 1653)

**Seven Years' War outcome:** Sweden entered the Pomeranian War (1757–1762) hoping to recover territories lost in earlier conflicts. This failed. The **Treaty of Hamburg (22 May 1762)** ended Swedish participation, reaffirming the **pre-war status quo**—no territorial changes. Sweden's poor military performance further damaged its international prestige.

**Pomerania post-1762:** Swedish Pomerania remains under Swedish control through the Napoleonic Wars and is ceded to Prussia only at the **Congress of Vienna (1815)**, where it becomes the Prussian province of Neuvorpommern (New Western Pomerania). *Note: By the 1815 bookmark, this territory is already lost.*

## Finland (1763)

**Status:** Integral province of Sweden. For over six centuries—and continuing through 1763—Finland forms an integral part of the Swedish realm, providing manpower and strategic depth.

**Territorial extent (1763):** The whole of Finland as it stood after the Treaty of Nystad (1721) and Treaty of Åbo (1743), which ceded Old Finland (the southeast toward the Bay of Kronstadt) to Russia. The remaining core Swedish Finland comprises the western and central regions.

**Future territorial loss:** Finland remains Swedish until the **Finnish War (1808–1809)** and the **Treaty of Fredrikshamn (1809)**, when it becomes the autonomous **Grand Duchy of Finland** under Russian sovereignty. *Note: By the 1815 bookmark, Finland is no longer Swedish.*

## Denmark–Norway (1763 context)

**Status of the union:** The **Kalmar Union** (1397–1523) dissolved when Gustav Vasa was elected King of Sweden and declared Sweden independent (6 June 1523; formal Swedish secession confirmed by Denmark's Frederick I in 1524 via the Treaty of Malmö).

**Norwegian status (1763):** Norway remains under the Danish crown as a subordinate territory (since ca. 1536/1537 after the Count's Feud). The **Denmark–Norway union** persists as a stable arrangement and will not dissolve until 1814, when Norway is ceded to Sweden by Denmark (Napoleonic Wars settlement).

**Regional context for 1763:** Sweden and Denmark–Norway are neighbors at peace. Sweden maintains traditional Protestant/Anglican cultural alignment; Denmark–Norway similarly Protestant. Neither competes for Scandinavian dominance in this era—that contest resolved with Swedish loss of southern Sweden (Skåne, Halland, Blekinge) to Denmark in the 17th century. By 1763, the territorial frontier is stable.

## Summary for 1815 Comparison

- **Ruler (1763):** Adolf Frederick, figurehead; Riksdag (Hats faction) holds real power
- **Pomerania (1763):** Held; lost to Prussia 1815
- **Finland (1763):** Held; lost to Russia 1809
- **Territorial reality (1763 vs. 1815):** 1763 Sweden is larger (Pomerania + all of Finland); 1815 Sweden has contracted to mainland Scandinavia only
- **Political trajectory (1763→1815):** Age of Liberty ends 1772 (Gustav III coup); Sweden's great-power pretensions fade during the Napoleonic Wars; 1815 sees Sweden as a regional Nordic power, not a Baltic hegemon
