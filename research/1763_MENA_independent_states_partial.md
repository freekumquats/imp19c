# Independent and Autonomous States in the Middle East and North Africa (February 16, 1763)

## Research Summary

This report documents the independent and autonomous states in the Middle East and North Africa as of February 16, 1763, with sources in both English and Arabic. A key finding is the delta between 1763 and 1815: the **First Saudi State (Emirate of Diriyah)** existed in 1763 but was destroyed by Ottoman forces in 1818, making it absent from the 1815 start date.

---

## 1. Morocco (المغرب)

**Ruler:** Sultan Mohammed III / Sidi Muhammad ibn Abdallah (محمد بن عبد الله) of the Alaouite dynasty (الدولة العلوية)

**Status:** Fully independent; never under Ottoman control

**Key Context:**
- Mohammed III ruled from 1757 to 1790
- Morocco was "the only North African nation to escape Ottoman dominion" with the Alawi dynasty resisting foreign control since seizing power in 1631
- The Alawi sultan Mawlay Isma'il (1672–1727) had "put a definitive end to Ottoman attempts to gain influence in Morocco"
- In 1760–1764, Mohammed III began founding Essaouira (الصويرة/Mogador), commissioning French engineer Théodore Cornut to design the fortified port city; construction was completed by 1769–1770
- To boost the new Atlantic port, he closed Agadir's harbor in 1767 and redirected European trade toward Essaouira
- Pursued decentralized governance and lighter taxation, relying on European trade revenue

## 2. Oman (عُمان)

**Ruler:** Ahmad bin Said Al Bu Said (أحمد بن سعيد البوسعيدي), founder of the Al Bu Said dynasty (الأسرة البوسعيدية)

**Status:** Fully independent

**Key Context:**
- Ahmad bin Said was elected Imam on June 9, 1749, and ruled from Rustaq until his death in 1783
- Governed for approximately 39 mostly peaceful years despite facing plots from rival tribes and even two of his own sons
- Built Oman's first permanent standing army and navy: "For the first time in the history of Oman Ahmad bin Said maintained a permanent army and navy"
- Promoted farming and maritime trade
- **Control of East African Coast:** Oman controlled extensive East African territories including Zanzibar, Pemba, Mafia, and Mombasa
  - Early in his rise, "Ahmad bin Sa'id was recognized as Imam only by the governor of Zanzibar" among coastal regions
  - His youngest son Muhammad later served as Governor-General of Mombasa overseeing Oman's East African possessions
  - Omani control of East Africa had been established under the earlier Yaruba dynasty after expelling the Portuguese (Fort Jesus at Mombasa fell in 1698)
- The empire controlled "trade movement between Africa and Asia"

**Sources:**
- English: [Wikipedia: Ahmad bin Said al-Busaidi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmad_bin_Said_al-Busaidi)
- English: [Wikipedia: Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oman)
- Arabic: [Wikipedia: الإمبراطورية العمانية](https://ar.wikipedia.org/wiki/الإمبراطورية_العمانية) - تؤكد حكم أحمد بن سعيد البوسعيدي (1749–1783) والحروب ضد النفوذ البرتغالي (1750–1770) والسيطرة على شرق أفريقيا بما في ذلك زنجبار وبمبا ومافيا

## 3. First Saudi State / Emirate of Diriyah (الدولة السعودية الأولى / إمارة الدرعية)

**Ruler:** Muhammad bin Saud (محمد بن سعود), founder; succeeded by his son Abdul-Aziz bin Muhammad (عبد العزيز بن محمد) in 1765

**Status:** Independent emirate in Najd

**Key Context:**
- **Founded 1744** through the Diriyah Pact (ميثاق الدرعية) between Muhammad bin Saud and religious reformer Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab (محمد بن عبد الوهاب)
- **Existed in 1763** but was still expanding; Muhammad bin Saud died in 1765
- Capital: Diriyah (الدرعية) in Najd
- The Wahhabi movement (الدعوة الوهابية) sought to purify Islam: "إفراد الله بالعبادة، ومحاربة الشرك بجميع أنواعه" (monotheism, fighting shirk)
- By 1763, the state faced serious threats: in October 1764, the Ismāʿīlī Shīʿa of Najrān allied with the 'Ujman tribe and defeated the Saudis at the Battle of Hair, "killing around 500 men" and besieging Diriyya (though the siege failed)
- Major opponent: Dahham bin Dawas (دهام بن دواس), emir of Riyadh, fought the Saudis in "17 battles over 27 years"
- **Destroyed 1818:** Ottoman Sultan Selim III ordered Egyptian viceroy Muhammad Ali Pasha to recapture the Hejaz in 1807; his son Ibrahim Pasha besieged and captured Diriyah, which "surrendered in the winter of 1818"
- Last ruler Abdullah bin Saud (عبد الله بن سعود) was executed in Istanbul in December 1818
- Diriyah was demolished: "هدم الدرعية وتدمير العديد من البلدان في نجد وما حولها"

**CRITICAL DELTA: This state existed in 1763 but NOT in 1815**

- English: [Wikipedia: Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Abd_al-Wahhab)

## 4. Yemen (اليمن) - Qasimid State (الدولة القاسمية)

**Ruler:** Imam Al-Mahdi Abbas (الإمام المهدي عباس), ruled 1748–1775

**Status:** Independent Zaidi imamate

**Key Context:**
- The Qasimid State (Zaidi Imamate) was fully sovereign, having expelled the Ottomans from Yemen by 1636
- By mid-18th century, the state was fracturing due to internal weakness:
  - "The imamate did not follow a cohesive succession mechanism, and family feuds and tribal insubordination led to political decline in the 18th century"
  - Arabic: "لم تتبع الإمامة آلية متماسكة للخلافة، وأدت الخلافات العائلية والعصيان القبلي إلى التدهور السياسي في القرن الثامن عشر"
- **Territorial losses:** The Sultanate of Lahej (سلطنة لحج) broke away and "became completely independent" in 1740 after its 'Abdali sultan declared himself sovereign
- **Economic decline:** Europeans broke Yemen's coffee monopoly in the first half of the 18th century by smuggling coffee plants and cultivating them in their colonies
- The Qasimid State remained independent throughout this period; it would not lose coastal territories until after 1803 and was only reincorporated into the Ottoman Empire in 1849

**Sources:**
- English: [Wikipedia: Qasimid State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qasimid_State)
- English: [Wikipedia: Yemen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yemen)

## Key Historical Notes

1. **Morocco's Unique Status:** Morocco was the only North African territory to successfully resist Ottoman incorporation, maintaining continuous independence under the Alaouite dynasty from 1631 onward.

2. **Omani Maritime Empire:** Oman controlled a vast maritime empire stretching from the Arabian Peninsula to East Africa (Zanzibar, Mombasa, Pemba), dominating Indian Ocean trade routes.

3. **First Saudi State's Trajectory:** 
   - 1744: Founded through Wahhabi-Saudi alliance
   - 1763: Expanding but facing serious military challenges (Battle of Hair 1764)
   - 1765: Muhammad bin Saud dies, succeeded by Abdul-Aziz
   - 1801–1818: Peak expansion (capturing Mecca, Medina, Karbala) followed by Ottoman reconquest
   - 1818: Total destruction by Ibrahim Pasha
   - **Not present in 1815 game start**

4. **Yemen's Fragmentation:** While nominally independent under Zaidi imams, Yemen was politically fragmenting in the mid-18th century, with peripheral territories like Lahej breaking away and establishing autonomous sultanates.
