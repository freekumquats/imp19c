## II. JAPAN (日本)

### Political Structure 1763

**Government**: Tokugawa bakufu (Edo shogunate), *bakuhan* system (幕藩体制) with ~260 *daimyō* domains.

**Shogun**: **Tokugawa Ieharu** (徳川家治, 1737–1786, r. 1760–1786), 10th Tokugawa shogun. Still a minor in 1763 (age 17), actual governance dominated by senior councillor (*rōjū* 老中) **Matsudaira Takemoto** and chamberlain (*sobayōnin* 側用人) **Tanuma Okitsugu** (田沼意次), who will dominate 1767–1786 ("Tanuma era").

**Emperor**: **Go-Sakuramachi** (後桜町天皇, 1740–1813, r. 1762–1771), 117th emperor and 8th (and last until 2019) empress regnant. Acceded after her nephew Emperor Momozono died; ceremonial role in Kyoto.

### Sakoku Isolation

**1763 = peak *sakoku*** (鎖国 "closed country"). Tokugawa foreign policy since 1639:
- **Trade restricted** to Nagasaki (長崎): Dutch VOC on Dejima island, Chinese merchants in designated quarter.
- **Christianity banned**, remnant *kakure kirishitan* (hidden Christians) persecuted.
- **No Japanese may leave** or return on pain of death.
- **Limited intelligence flow** via Dutch *Oranda fūsetsugaki* (阿蘭陀風説書, Dutch reports) and Chinese merchants.

**Domains with foreign contact**:
- **Matsumae domain** (Hokkaido): Ainu trade.
- **Tsushima domain**: Korean trade via Pusan Waegwan (倭館).
- **Satsuma domain**: covert control of Ryukyu Kingdom (tributary to both Japan and Qing).

### Economy 1763

**Rice-based**: *kokudaka* system (石高, rice output as measure of domain wealth). Total ~30 million *koku*.

**Osaka** (大阪): "nation's kitchen" (*tenka no daidokoro* 天下の台所), commercial hub. Rice markets, merchant class rising despite *shi-nō-kō-shō* (士農工商) status system placing them lowest.

**Edo** (江戸, mod. Tokyo): political capital, pop. ~1 million (largest city globally 1763). *Sankin-kōtai* (参勤交代) alternate attendance system concentrates *daimyō* consumption in Edo.

**Mining**:
- **Sado Island** (佐渡): gold and silver mines (production declining from 17th-cent peak).
- **Ikuno** (生野, Tajima Province): silver.
- **Ashio** (足尾, Shimotsuke): copper.

**Foreign Trade (Nagasaki)**:
- **Exports**: copper (major), dried sea cucumber, abalone, shark fin, lacquerware, porcelain.
- **Imports**: Chinese silk, sugar, medicinal herbs; Dutch woollens, glassware, books.
- **Trade value**: ~6,000 *kan* (貫, tael equiv.) annual Dutch trade; Chinese trade larger.

**Sources**:
- 『鎖国』 (荒野泰典ほか編, 2019) — "Sakoku" in 日本の対外関係 series.
- Jansen, Marius B. *China in the Tokugawa World* (1992).
- Totman, Conrad. *Early Modern Japan* (1993).
- Vaporis, Constantine. *Breaking Barriers: Travel and the State in Early Modern Japan* (1994).

### Delta vs 1815

**1763**: Deep isolation, no Western knowledge penetration, stable Edo governance, Ming-loyalist fiction via Chinese calendar.  
**1815**: *Sakoku* maintained but pressure building; Phaeton incident (1808) British warship in Nagasaki; late-Edo fiscal strain, *rangaku* (蘭学, Dutch learning) spread, peasant uprisings (*ikki*).  
**Key**: 1763 → 1853 Perry = **90 years**. Bakumatsu (幕末) upheaval (1853–1868) is distant future from 1763; stable Tokugawa order appears permanent.

## III. KOREA — JOSEON DYNASTY (朝鮮)

### Political Structure 1763

**King**: **Yi Geum** (이금 / 李昑), temple name **Yeongjo** (영조 / 英祖), r. 1724–1776. Age 69 in 1763, one of Joseon's longest-reigning and most reform-minded kings. Enacted *Tangpyeongchaek* (탕평책 / 蕩平策, "Policy of Impartiality") to reduce factional strife between Noron (老論) and Soron (少論) factions.

**The Sado Incident (1762)**: Crown Prince **Sado** (사도세자 / 思悼世子), Yeongjo's son, executed by being locked in a rice chest and left to die (July 1762, one year before 1763 start). Officially due to mental illness and erratic behaviour; factional politics implicated. Sado's son **Yi San** (age 11 in 1763) named heir, later King **Jeongjo** (r. 1776–1800).

**Government**: Confucian bureaucracy, *Uijeongbu* (의정부 / 議政府, State Council) with *Yeonguijeong* (영의정 / 領議政, Chief State Councillor). Six Ministries (六曹 / 육조): Personnel, Taxation, Rites, War, Punishments, Public Works.

### Qing Tributary Relationship

**Tributary status** since 1637 (after second Manchu invasion). Annual tribute missions to Beijing; Joseon king receives investiture. Symbolic subordination; internal autonomy maintained. Missions route via Uiju (의주 / 義州) on Yalu River.

**Korean identity**: "Little China" (*Sojunghwa* 소중화 / 小中華) ideology — Korea as preserver of Confucian civilization after Ming fall (1644). Anti-Qing sentiment persists culturally despite political submission.

### Economy 1763

**Agriculture-based**: Rice (south), millet/barley (north). Population ~14 million.

**Capital**: **Hanseong** (한성 / 漢城, mod. Seoul), pop. ~200,000.

**Trade**:
- **Official tribute trade** with Qing (Beijing).
- **Private border trade** at Uiju and Hoeryeong (회령 / 會寧).
- **Japanese trade** via **Waegwan** (왜관 / 倭館) at Pusan (부산 / 釜山), restricted enclave for Tsushima domain. Treaty of Gyehae (계해약조 / 癸亥約條, 1443/renewed) limits trade.
- **Ginseng** (인삼 / 人蔘) major export to China and Japan.

**Currency**: Copper coins, rice, and cloth as media of exchange. *Sangpyeong Tongbo* (상평통보 / 常平通寶) copper coinage from 1678.

**Manufacturing**: Porcelain (백자 / 白磁 white porcelain), cotton textiles, paper.

**Sources**:
- 『英祖實錄』 (*Yeongjo Sillok*, Veritable Records of Yeongjo) — primary source.
- JaHyun Kim Haboush, *The Confucian Kingship in Korea: Yŏngjo and the Politics of Sagacity* (2001).
- Sei-Woo Choe, *The Chosŏn Ilbo* and Joseon fiscal records.
- 박현순, 『조선후기 대청무역 연구』 (Park Hyeon-sun, *Study of Late Joseon-Qing Trade*, 2013).

### Delta vs 1815

**1763**: Yeongjo's Tangpyeong reforms at peak; Sado trauma fresh; no Catholic persecution wave yet (1801 *Sinyu* persecution).  
**1815**: Jeongjo's enlightened reign (1776–1800) over; Sunjo (순조 / 純祖, r. 1800–1834) under in-law *Andong Kim* (안동 김씨 / 安東金氏) clan dominance; Catholic persecutions (1801, 1839, 1846); Qing decline visible. Social strain but no Donghak (동학 / 東學) uprising (1894) yet.

## IV. VIETNAM — DIVIDED REALM (1763)

**CRITICAL DIFFERENCE FROM 1815**: Vietnam in 1763 is **divided** between two rival regimes under a puppet emperor. By 1815, unified under Nguyễn dynasty (from 1802).

### A. The Lê Dynasty (Nominal)

**Emperor**: **Lê Hiển Tông** (黎顯宗, r. 1740–1786), of the Restored Lê dynasty (Lê Trung hưng / 黎中興, 1533–1789). **No real power**; controlled by Trịnh lords in north. Capital **Thăng Long** (昇龍, mod. Hanoi).

### B. Trịnh Lords (Đàng Ngoài / 塘外, Outer Realm — NORTH)

**Lord**: **Trịnh Sâm** (鄭森, r. 1762–1782), 10th Trịnh lord. Real power in north; Lê emperor his puppet.

**Territory**: Tonkin (Đàng Ngoài), north of Gianh River (sông Gianh / 𤅷ngang). Capital **Thăng Long**.

**Economy**:
- **Red River Delta** (đồng bằng sông Hồng / 洞平宋紅): intensive rice cultivation, pop. ~7 million.
- **Trade**: Chinese border trade (Guangxi/Yunnan); tribute missions to Qing as "Annam" (安南).
- **Crafts**: silk, ceramics, lacquerware.

**Government**: Centralized bureaucracy, Confucian examination system, modelled on Ming/Qing.

### C. Nguyễn Lords (Đàng Trong / 塘中, Inner Realm — SOUTH)

**Lord**: **Nguyễn Phúc Khoát** (阮福濶, r. 1738–1765), 7th Nguyễn lord. **Independent** from Trịnh/Lê; does not recognize Lê emperor's authority.

**Territory**: South of Gianh River to Mekong Delta. Capital **Phú Xuân** (富春, mod. Huế).

**Economy**:
- **Mekong Delta** (đồng bằng sông Cửu Long / 洞平宋九龍): recently colonized from Khmer; rice frontier. Pop. ~2.5 million.
- **Trade**: maritime trade with China (Guangdong), Siam, Europeans (Portuguese, Dutch, French). Port **Hội An** (會安) major hub.
- **Ethnic diversity**: Vietnamese (Kinh), Cham, Khmer, Chinese (Hoa / 華人) settlers.

**Military**: Naval strength; expansion into former Champa and Khmer territories.

### Division Context (1627–1802)

**Trịnh-Nguyễn split** since 1627. Intermittent wars, armistice from 1673. **De facto two states** under Lê fiction.

**Tây Sơn Rebellion** (1771–1802) will destroy both; Nguyễn Ánh (future Gia Long) unifies Vietnam 1802 as Nguyễn dynasty.

**Sources**:
- Keith W. Taylor, *A History of the Vietnamese* (2013).
- Li Tana, *Nguyễn Cochinchina: Southern Vietnam in the Seventeenth and Eighteenth Centuries* (1998).
- 陳荊和 (Trần Kinh Hòa), 『越南史略』 (*Brief History of Vietnam*, 1954/1964).
- 黎崱, 『安南志略』 (*Annam zhi lüe*, 15th cent., on Đại Việt).
- Nola Cooke, "Regionalism and the Nature of Nguyễn Rule in Seventeenth-Century Đàng Trong (Cochinchina)", *JSEAS* 29:1 (1998).

### Delta vs 1815

**1763**: Two regimes (Trịnh north, Nguyễn south); Lê puppet dynasty; no Tây Sơn yet.  
**1815**: Unified **Nguyễn dynasty** (Gia Long 1802–1820); capital Huế; Confucian state modelled on Qing; French missionary presence increasing (later pretext for colonization).

## V. SIAM — AYUTTHAYA KINGDOM (1763)

**CRITICAL DIFFERENCE FROM 1815**: Ayutthaya in 1763 is still standing. Sacked by Burma 1767 → replaced by Thonburi/Chakri (Bangkok) by 1815.

### Political Structure 1763

**King**: **Ekkathat** (เอกทัศน์, r. 1758–1767), last king of Ayutthaya. Weak rule; court factionalism.

**Capital**: **Ayutthaya** (อยุธยา / 大城), on Chao Phraya River delta, pop. ~150,000. Major Southeast Asian entrepôt 16th–18th cent.

**Territory**: Central Thailand (Chao Phraya basin), suzerainty over Lanna (เชียงใหม่, Chiang Mai), Laos kingdoms (Luang Prabang, Vientiane, Champasak), northern Malay sultanates, Cambodia (contested with Vietnam).

### Burmese Threat

**Konbaung Burma** rising under Alaungpaya (r. 1752–1760) and son **Hsinbyushin** (r. 1763–1776). First Burmese invasion 1759–60 repelled. **Second invasion 1765–67 will destroy Ayutthaya** (April 1767).

### Economy 1763

**Rice**: Chao Phraya delta major exporter. Chinese junks buy rice for southern China.

**Trade**:
- **Port of Ayutthaya**: cosmopolitan hub. Chinese, Indian, Persian, Arab, European (Dutch VOC, English EIC, French, Portuguese) merchants.
- **Exports**: rice, teakwood, tin, hides, ivory.
- **Imports**: Chinese porcelain, silk, Indian textiles, firearms.

**Chinese settlers** (Teochew, Hokkien) dominate commerce; tax farming.

**Tin**: Phuket (ภูเก็ต), Phang Nga (พังงา) mines (southern peninsula).

**Sources**:
- Dhiravat na Pombejra, "VOC Employees and Their Relationships with Mon and Siamese Women", *Itinerario* 16:1 (1992).
- Chris Baker and Pasuk Phongpaichit, *A History of Ayutthaya* (2017).
- Nidhi Eoseewong (นิธิ เอียวศรีวงศ์), *Pen and Sail: Literature and History in Early Bangkok* (2005).
- ชาญวิทย์ เกษตรศิริ (Chanwit Kasetsiri), 『ศึกอยุธยาครั้งที่สอง』 (*The Second Fall of Ayutthaya*, 1977).

### Delta vs 1815

**1763**: **Ayutthaya** capital, 4 years before fall.  
**1815**: **Bangkok** (Rattanakosin / รัตนโกสินทร์) established 1782 by Chakri dynasty. King **Rama II** (r. 1809–1824). Thonburi (1767–1782) and Taksin interregnum erased. Siam recovered from Burmese devastation; expansion into Cambodia and Laos; modernization not yet begun (Rama IV from 1851).

**Political map**: Ayutthaya (1763) vs Bangkok (1815) = different dynasty, capital, territorial configuration.

## VI. BURMA — KONBAUNG DYNASTY (1763)

### Political Structure 1763

**King**: **Hsinbyushin** (ဆင်ဘြူရှင်, r. 1763–1776), 2nd Konbaung king. Acceded January 1763 after father Naungdawgyi died. Aggressive expansionist.

**Dynasty**: **Konbaung** (ကုန်းဘောင်, 1752–1885), founded by Alaungpaya (r. 1752–1760), who unified Burma after Mon rebellion destroyed Toungoo dynasty (1752).

**Capital**: **Shwebo** (ရွှေဘို, Alaungpaya's birthplace, early Konbaung capital); later moved to **Ava** (Inwa / အင်းဝ, 1765).

**Territory**: Central Burma (Irrawaddy basin), Arakan (Rakhine / ရခိုင်), Mon lands (Lower Burma). Vassals: Shan states, Manipur (claimed).

### Military Expansion 1763

**Invasion of Siam** (1765–67): Hsinbyushin's armies will sack Ayutthaya 1767.

**Sino-Burmese War** (1765–1769): Qing invasions from Yunnan (four invasions 1765–69) repelled; treaty 1769 establishes tributary status to Qing but effective independence.

### Economy 1763

**Rice**: Irrawaddy delta.

**Teak**: forests for shipbuilding.

**Trade**:
- **Syriam** (Thanlyin / သန်လျင်, near Rangoon): port for European trade (Dutch, English, French).
- **Exports**: teak, rubies (Mogok / မိုးကုတ်), ivory, cotton.
- **Inland trade**: Yunnan border; Manipur.

**Sources**:
- Victor Lieberman, *Strange Parallels: Southeast Asia in Global Context, c. 800–1830* (2003, 2009).
- Thant Myint-U, *The Making of Modern Burma* (2001).
- Michael Aung-Thwin and Maitrii Aung-Thwin, *A History of Myanmar Since Ancient Times* (2012).
- မဟာရာဇဝင်တော်ကြီး (*Maha Yazawin Gyi*, Great Chronicle of Kings) — Burmese chronicle.

### Delta vs 1815

**1763**: Konbaung ascendant, about to destroy Ayutthaya; Qing war imminent.  
**1815**: Konbaung peak territorial extent (Arakan, Assam, Manipur, Tenasserim); **Bodawpaya** (r. 1782–1819) reign; First Anglo-Burmese War (1824–26) ahead, will cost Arakan and Tenasserim to British.

## VII. MAINLAND SOUTHEAST ASIA — LAOS AND CAMBODIA

### A. Lao Kingdoms (1763)

**Three kingdoms** after 1707 split of Lan Xang:

1. **Luang Prabang** (ຫຼວງພະບາງ / 龍坡邦): King **Sotika-Koumane** (r. 1753–1771). Northern Laos, Mekong upper reaches. Burmese tributary.
2. **Vientiane** (ວຽງຈັນ / 萬象): King **Ong Boun** (r. 1760–1778). Central Laos, Mekong middle. Siamese tributary.
3. **Champasak** (ຈຳປາສັກ / 占巴塞): King **Sayakuman** (r. 1738–1791). Southern Laos. Siamese tributary.

**Economy**: subsistence rice, forest products (benzoin, sticklac, ivory), Mekong trade.

**Sources**:
- Martin Stuart-Fox, *A History of Laos* (1997).
- Mayoury Ngaosrivathana and Pheuiphanh Ngaosrivathana (eds.), *The Enduring Sacred Landscape of the Naga* (2009).

### B. Cambodia (1763)

**King**: **Outey II** (r. 1762–1779, intermittent). Khmer kingdom weakened; under pressure from both Siam (Ayutthaya) and Vietnam (Nguyễn).

**Capital**: **Udong** (អូរដុង), on Tonle Sap.

**Territory**: Diminished from Angkor peak; Mekong Delta ceded to Vietnam (Nguyễn) by 18th cent.

**Economy**: Rice (Tonle Sap/Mekong), fish, trade with Chinese and Vietnamese.

**Sources**:
- David Chandler, *A History of Cambodia* (4th ed., 2008).
- Khin Sok, *Le Cambodge entre le Siam et le Viêt Nam* (1991).

### Delta vs 1815

**1763**: Lao kingdoms independent tributaries of Siam/Burma; Cambodia contested between Ayutthaya Siam and Nguyễn Vietnam.  
**1815**: Lao kingdoms firmly under Siamese (Bangkok) suzerainty; Cambodia puppet of Siam (Vietnamese influence returns later 19th cent.). Weaker autonomy.

## VIII. INSULAR & MARITIME SOUTHEAST ASIA (1763)

### A. Dutch East Indies — VOC Control

**Power**: **Dutch East India Company** (VOC / Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie) dominant. HQ **Batavia** (Batavia / 巴達維亞, mod. Jakarta), pop. ~150,000.

**Territory controlled/influenced**:
- **Java**: VOC controls north coast (*pasisir*) directly; interior sultanates (Mataram-successor states) under indirect rule.
- **Moluccas** (Maluku / 摩鹿加): **clove and nutmeg monopoly** (Ambon, Banda, Ternate). Forced cultivation (*extirpatie* = destruction of unauthorized spice trees).
- **Malacca** (Melaka / 麻六甲): VOC-controlled port (seized from Portugal 1641).
- **Ceylon** (Sri Lanka): VOC coastal zones (cinnamon).
- **Makassar** (Sulawesi): VOC post after 1669 Gowa defeat.

**Trade**:
- **Intra-Asian trade**: Chinese silk/porcelain, Indian textiles, Japanese copper, Southeast Asian spices → Europe.
- **VOC exports to Europe**: spices (cloves, nutmeg, mace, pepper), coffee (Java), sugar, tin, hardwoods.
- **Chinese traders** (*Orang Cina*) intermediaries in Batavia, major commercial role; VOC restrictions after 1740 Chinese massacre.

### B. Java — Divided Sultanates (1763)

**Mataram succession wars** (1704–1757) ended by VOC intervention; Java divided:

1. **Surakarta** (Surakarta / 梭羅 / Solo): **Susuhunan Pakubuwono III** (r. 1749–1788). VOC-backed. Capital **Surakarta**.
2. **Yogyakarta** (Yogyakarta / 日惹): **Sultan Hamengkubuwono I** (r. 1755–1792). Rival court. Capital **Yogyakarta**.
3. **Mangkunegaran** (1757) and **Pakualaman** (1813) appanages later split off.

**Economy**:
- **Rice**: Java's fertile volcanic soils.
- **Sugar**: VOC forced cultivation beginning.
- **Coffee**: VOC introduces Arabica coffee (Priangan highlands); becomes major export.
- **Batik**: textile production (court/commercial).

**Population**: Java ~10 million (largest population center in SE Asia).

**Sources**:
- M.C. Ricklefs, *A History of Modern Indonesia Since c. 1200* (4th ed., 2008).
- Remco Raben (ed.), *Representing the Japanese Occupation of Indonesia* (1999).
- Merle Ricklefs, *Jogjakarta under Sultan Mangkubumi 1749–1792* (1974).
- Anthony Reid, *Southeast Asia in the Age of Commerce* (1988, 1993).

### C. Malay Peninsula & Sumatra — Sultanates (1763)

**Aceh Sultanate** (northern Sumatra): **Sultan Badruddin** (r. 1760–1764). Declining from 17th-cent peak; pepper trade reduced; internal instability.

**Johor-Riau Sultanate**: fractured; **Bugis** (Buginese from Sulawesi) warlords control sultan. **Abdul Jalil Riayat Shah IV** nominal ruler. Capital **Riau** (Bintan Island).

**Siak**: Sultanate on Sumatra east coast; rival to Johor.

**Perak, Kedah, Kelantan**: Malay sultanates on peninsula; tin production (Perak, Kedah). Siamese suzerainty (Kedah). Trade with VOC, English, Chinese.

**Tin mining**: **Perak** (霹靂) and **Selangor** major sources; Chinese miners (*kongsi* 公司 associations). Tin export to China and India.

**Sources**:
- Barbara Watson Andaya and Leonard Y. Andaya, *A History of Malaysia* (3rd ed., 2016).
- Anthony Reid, *An Indonesian Frontier: Acehnese and Other Histories of Sumatra* (2005).
- Takeshi Ito, "The World of the Adat Aceh: A Historical Study of the Sultanate of Aceh" (PhD diss., ANU, 1984).

### D. Borneo (1763)

**Brunei Sultanate**: **Sultan Muhammad Tajuddin** (r. 1750–1778). Controls northern Borneo coast; peak territorial extent already past (16th cent.). Trade in camphor, sago, gold, birds' nests.

**Banjarmasin Sultanate** (southeast Borneo): Dutch influence; pepper, gold.

**Interior**: Dayak peoples; *headhunting* cultures.

### E. Spanish Philippines (1763)

**Status February 1763**: **Manila under British occupation** (captured Oct 1762 during Seven Years' War). Treaty of Paris (10 Feb 1763) stipulates return to Spain; **actual return delayed until 1764**.

**Spanish colonial government**: Captaincy General of the Philippines, part of Viceroyalty of New Spain (Mexico). Capital **Manila** (馬尼拉).

**Acting Spanish authority** (1762–64): **Simón de Anda y Salazar**, acting Governor, operates from **Bulacan** (resistance government against British).

**Economy**:
- **Manila-Acapulco Galleon Trade**: silver from Mexico → Manila → Chinese silk/porcelain → Acapulco. Major trans-Pacific trade artery (since 1565).
- **Agriculture**: rice, tobacco (Cagayan Valley), abaca (Manila hemp), sugar.
- **Chinese traders** (*Sangley* / 生理 / 常來): dominant in Manila commerce; periodic anti-Chinese violence.
- **Tribute system**: *encomienda* extraction from indigenous Filipinos.

**Population**: ~1.5 million (Luzon, Visayas, Mindanao).

**Sources**:
- Nicholas P. Cushner, *Spain in the Philippines* (1971).
- William Lytle Schurz, *The Manila Galleon* (1939/1985).
- Nicholas Tracy, *Manila Ransomed: The British Assault on Manila in the Seven Years War* (1995).

### Delta vs 1815

**1763**: VOC peak power; Java divided sultanates; Aceh independent but declining; Manila British-occupied (temporary); galleon trade continuing.  
**1815**: VOC dissolved (1799), replaced by Dutch government control (Dutch East Indies); Java under **Raffles** interregnum (British, 1811–16) then Dutch return; **Padri War** (1803–38) in Sumatra; Aceh still independent; galleon trade ended (Mexican independence 1821); British **Raffles** founds **Singapore** 1819 (not yet in 1763/1815).

**Key**: 1763 = **VOC commercial empire**; 1815 = transition to **territorial colonialism** (Dutch state, British EIC expansion).

## IX. RYUKYU KINGDOM (琉球)

**Status 1763**: **Dual tributary** to both **Qing China** and **Satsuma domain** (Japan). Formally independent kingdom; covertly controlled by Satsuma since 1609 invasion.

**King**: **Shō Boku** (尚穆, r. 1752–1794).

**Capital**: **Shuri** (首里), on Okinawa Island.

**Economy**: Entrepôt trade between China, Japan, Korea, Southeast Asia. Tribute missions to China (Fuzhou / 福州 port of entry). Satsuma extracts trade profits.

**Sources**:
- Gregory Smits, *Visions of Ryukyu: Identity and Ideology in Early-Modern Thought and Politics* (1999).

### Major Differences

## XII. SYNTHESIS: 1763 EAST/SOUTHEAST ASIA FOR GAME START

### Key Characteristics (1763)

1. **Regional fragmentation**: Vietnam divided, Java divided, Lao kingdoms separate, Burma/Siam rivalry.
2. **Pre-colonial**: VOC commercial empire, not yet territorial colonialism; British/French imperial expansion in India but limited Southeast Asia presence.
3. **Tributary systems**: Qing overlordship (Korea, Vietnam, Burma, Ryukyu); Siamese suzerainty (Laos, Cambodia); interlocking hierarchies.
4. **Isolation**: Japan *sakoku* at peak; Korea "Little China" insularity.
5. **Trade networks**: intra-Asian (China-Southeast Asia-India) more important than European; VOC dominant but not hegemonic.
6. **Dynastic transitions imminent**: Ayutthaya will fall (1767), Tây Sơn will erupt (1771), Burma-Qing war (1765), Tanuma era (1767).

### Implications for 1763 Game Start

**Mission trees**: Must account for:
- **Vietnam unification arc** (Tây Sơn/Nguyễn, 1771–1802).
- **Siam collapse/restoration arc** (1767 sack → Thonburi → Chakri).
- **Burma-Qing conflict** (1765–69).
- **VOC→Dutch state transition** (post-1799).
- **Japan Perry arc** must still trigger ~90 years after start (1763 → 1853).

**Economy**: Pre-industrial; rice/spice/metals/silk trade; no steamships, no industrialization. Chinese commercial networks central.

**Great Power system**: European powers peripheral except VOC; Qing dominant regional power but not intervening in Southeast Asia (except Burma war). Intra-Asian balance of power.

**Tech level**: Matchlock firearms, junks/galleons, no telegraph/railroads. Military parity between Asian states and Europeans (Burma defeats Qing; Ayutthaya falls to Burma not Europeans).
