## 2. TREATY OF PARIS TERRITORIAL SETTLEMENT (10 February 1763)

The Treaty of Paris, signed six days before the target date, concluded the Seven Years' War and fundamentally reshaped global colonial holdings. This was the dominant political event shaping European politics in February 1763.

### French Territorial Losses

**North America:**
- **Canada** (New France): Ceded to Britain
  - Initially limited to territory around Quebec and Montreal as Province of Quebec (1763)
  - Did not initially extend to Great Lakes or as far north as Rupert's Land
- **Louisiana east of Mississippi River**: Ceded to Britain
  - Britain organized as part of West Florida and other territories
- **Cape Breton Island**: Ceded to Britain
- **Louisiana west of Mississippi River and New Orleans**: Ceded to Spain via earlier Treaty of Fontainebleau (3 November 1762)

**Caribbean:**
- **Retained:** Saint-Domingue (Haiti - the "Pearl of the Antilles"), Martinique, Guadeloupe (both returned after war), Saint Pierre and Miquelon (fishing rights), French Guiana
- Saint-Domingue produced ~40% of Europe's sugar and 60% of coffee by 1780s

**Africa:**
- **Senegal trading posts**: Status in Treaty (retained Saint-Louis and Gorée)

**India:**
- **Five Comptoirs retained with restrictions:**
  - Pondichéry (Pondicherry) - capital, 293 km², returned 1765
  - Chandernagore (Bengal) - established 1692
  - Yanam (Andhra/Coromandel coast) - acquired 1723
  - Mahé (Malabar Coast) - acquired 1725
  - Karikal (Coromandel Coast) - acquired 1739
- Military restrictions imposed
- Obligation to support British client states
- Effectively ended French imperial ambitions in India

**Summary:** France lost nearly entire North American empire, reduced to Caribbean sugar islands and Indian trading posts with no military power.

**Sources:** Wikipedia - Treaty of Paris (1763), French colonial empire, French India

---

### Spanish Territorial Changes

**Gains:**
- **Louisiana west of Mississippi River** (~878,745 sq mi)
  - Acquired from France via Treaty of Fontainebleau (3 November 1762)
  - All Mississippi drainage west of river, from Gulf to Canadian border
  - From Mississippi basin to Rocky Mountains
  - Population ~125,000 by 1785
  - Compensation for Spanish wartime losses

**Losses:**
- **Florida** (East and West Florida): Ceded to Britain
  - **East Florida**: Capital St. Augustine, most of present-day Florida
  - **West Florida**: Capital Pensacola, Apalachicola River to Mississippi River
  - West Florida northern boundary: 31st parallel (extended to 32°28' in 1767)

**Recovered Territories:**
- **Cuba** (including Havana): Returned by Britain in exchange for Florida
  - Havana population ~50,000 by 1763
  - Spain began constructing Fort San Carlos de la Cabaña (largest Spanish fort in New World) upon regaining control
- **Manila and the Philippines**: Returned by Britain

**Net Result:** Spain lost Florida but gained vast Louisiana territory, offsetting losses. Retained massive South American empire intact.

**Sources:** Wikipedia - Seven Years' War, Spanish Louisiana, Treaty of Fontainebleau (1762), East Florida, West Florida

---

### British Territorial Gains

**North America:**
- **Province of Quebec** (from France): Former New France
- **East and West Florida** (from Spain)
- **Confirmation of Rupert's Land** (Hudson's Bay Company): One-third of modern Canada

**Caribbean:**
- **British Ceded Islands** (from France): Grenada, Tobago, Saint Vincent, Dominica

**India:**
- Secured dominance after Battle of Plassey (1757)
- French reduced to five trading posts with no military power
- East India Company controlled Bengal (richest Mughal region), Madras Presidency, Bombay Presidency

**Net Result:** Britain emerged as world's dominant maritime and colonial power.

**Sources:** Wikipedia - British Empire, Royal Proclamation of 1763, Treaty of Paris (1763)

## 3. SUBJECT AND DYNASTIC RELATIONSHIPS

### Britain-Hanover Personal Union (1714-1837)

The Anglo-Hanoverian personal union maintained Hanover as "a separately ruled territory with its own governmental bodies" despite sharing a monarch (George III) with Britain.

**Governance Structure:**
- **Privy Council of Hanover** (*Geheimer Rat*): Held executive authority
  - Members of generally equal rank
  - Power "especially peaked after the accession of George III"
- **German Chancery** (*Deutsche Kanzlei*): Housed in St James's Palace, London
  - One minister always accompanied elector-king in London as head
  - Primary duty: "transmit memoranda between Privy Council of Hanover and the Elector"
- **Premiere Minister (1753-1770):** Gerlach Adolph, Baron von Münchhausen
  - Effectively directed day-to-day governance despite formal equality

**Key Features:**
- George III "never visited Hanover" - governed remotely
- Formal treaties required whenever Hanoverian forces joined British operations
- Demonstrated juridical separation despite personal union

**Sources:** Wikipedia - Electorate of Hanover, Privy Council of Hanover, German Chancery, George III

---

### Bourbon Family Compact (1761)

The **Third Family Compact** was signed 15 August 1761 between France (Louis XV) and Spain (Charles III), also involving Spanish Bourbon branches ruling Naples and Parma.

**Background:**
- France's duc de Choiseul pursued alliance as Seven Years' War went poorly
- Charles III feared "France would sell out Spain's interests in a peace with Britain"
- Two monarchies agreed to settle conflict with Britain jointly

**Outcomes (by February 1763):**
- **Disastrous for Spain:**
  - Britain captured Havana and Manila in 1762
  - Spain's 1762 invasion of Portugal "decisively defeated, losing upwards of 25,000 men"
  - Lost Florida to Britain under Treaty of Paris (1763)
  - Regained Havana and Manila
- **France compensated Spain:**
  - Ceded Louisiana west of Mississippi via Treaty of Fontainebleau (1762)

**Post-War Status:** Alliance formally intact but tested by defeat; Spain beginning Bourbon Reforms to strengthen empire.

**Sources:** Wikipedia - Pacte de Famille

---

### Spanish Colonial Viceroyalties

By 1763, **three major viceroyalties** existed:

1. **Viceroyalty of New Spain** (est. 1535)
   - Capital: Mexico City
   - Territory: Present-day Mexico, Central America (except Panama), much of US west of Mississippi
   - Included newly acquired Louisiana (1762)

2. **Viceroyalty of Peru** (est. 1542)
   - Capital: Lima
   - Territory: Modern Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador, Chile, southern territories (Buenos Aires, Charcas)
   - Note: Viceroyalty of Río de la Plata not created until 1776

3. **Viceroyalty of New Granada** (permanently reestablished 1739)
   - Capital: Santa Fe de Bogotá
   - Territory: Modern Colombia, Panama, Venezuela, northern Ecuador

**Administrative Hierarchy:**
- Viceroyalty → Audiencias (superior tribunals) → Captaincies General/Governorships → Local districts (*corregidores* or *cabildos*)
- Viceroys served as monarch's direct deputies
- Governed "overseas Provinces" of Crown of Castile under Laws of the Indies
- Answered to Council of the Indies in Madrid

**Bourbon Reforms (Post-1763):**
The 1763 defeat triggered a "revolution in government":
- **Intendancy system** (1764-1789): Replaced *corregidores* with French-style intendants directly responsible to Crown
- "Decentralizing administration at the expense of viceroys"
- Peninsular Spaniards systematically replaced Creoles (by 1807: only 12 of 99 audiencia judges were Creoles)
- **José de Gálvez's *visita general*** to New Spain (1765-1771): Reorganized tax collection, template for empire-wide reforms
- Charles III centralized colonial administration into single ministry

**Sources:** Wikipedia - Viceroyalty, Bourbon Reforms, Viceroyalty of New Spain, Viceroyalty of Peru

---

### Portuguese Brazil

**Administrative Structure (1763):**
- Operated under viceregal governance after 1640 (governors "started to use the title of *Vice-rei*")
- **CRUCIAL 1763 CHANGE:** Capital moved from Salvador to **Rio de Janeiro in 1763**
- Prior administrative division:
  - State of *Brasil* (capital Salvador → Rio de Janeiro 1763)
  - State of *Maranhão* subdivided 1737 into:
    - *Maranhão e Piauí*
    - *Grão-Pará e Rio Negro* (capital Belém do Pará)
- Full unification into **Viceroyalty of Brazil** occurred later in 1775

**Governance:**
- "Absolute monarchy" as Portuguese colony
- Tightly controlled trade - restricted to Portugal and other Portuguese colonies
- Local governance through city councils (*câmara municipal*) staffed by prominent colonists

**Pombaline Reforms (begun 1755):**
Marquis of Pombal (Count of Oeiras in 1763):
- "Abolished the enslavement of natives and legal discrimination against Europeans who married them"
- Aimed to convert indigenous populations into assimilated European-style peasantry

**Economic Transition:**
- Shifting from sugar dominance (95% of exports 1600-1650)
- To gold/diamond mining era following Minas Gerais discoveries

**Sources:** Wikipedia - Colonial Brazil

---

### Dutch Republic Governance

The Dutch Republic was a **confederation of seven provinces** retaining "substantial autonomy from the federal assembly."

**Structure:**
- **States General**: Federal assembly, described as "Confederal parliamentary republic"
- **Stadtholder:** William V (1751-1795)
  - Office effectively hereditary by this era
  - Prince of Orange typically "holding most or all stadtholderships, making him effectively head of state"
- Each province maintained own stadtholder and "municipal rights"

**Provincial Power:**
- **Province of Holland dominated financially**
  - Amsterdam alone "contributed the largest quota in taxes to States of Holland"
  - States of Holland "in turn contributed over half the quota to States General"
  - Gave Holland outsized influence in foreign affairs

**Political Factions:**
- **Orangists**: "Favoured a powerful stadtholder"
- **Republicans**: "Favoured a strong States General"

**Era Characteristics (1751-1795):**
- "Regency and indolent rule (1752-1779)" under William V
- Economic decline beginning after 1730
- Led to later political instability of Patriottentijd (1780-1787)

**Sources:** Wikipedia - Dutch Republic

## 4. ECONOMY c.1763 BY SUB-REGION

**IMPORTANT NOTE:** 1763 is **pre-industrial**. NO modern factories yet. Cottage industries, workshops, and proto-industrial production dominate. British Industrial Revolution beginning but not yet widespread.

### France

**Wine Regions (PRIMARY EXPORT):**
- **Bordeaux** (Aquitaine/Gironde):
  - Major Atlantic port and wine production center
  - Export hub for regional wines
  - Slave trade and sugar refineries on Atlantic coast
- **Burgundy** (Côte d'Or):
  - Premium wine region
  - Established medieval wine trade routes
- **Champagne** (Reims/Épernay):
  - Sparkling wine production developing (Dom Pérignon method 17th century)
- **Loire Valley, Rhône Valley**: Additional wine production

**Silk Industry:**
- **Lyon**: European silk capital
  - Specialized silk weaving and textile production
  - High-value luxury goods for European markets
  - Guild-based artisan production

**Atlantic Trade Ports:**
- **Nantes** (Loire estuary):
  - Major slave trade port
  - Sugar refineries processing Caribbean imports
  - Colonial trade hub
- **Bordeaux** (Gironde estuary):
  - Slave trade and colonial goods
  - Wine export
  - Sugar refineries
- **La Rochelle**: Secondary Atlantic trade port

**Other Manufacturing:**
- Paris: Luxury goods, printing, varied crafts
- Rouen: Textiles
- Pre-industrial scattered cottage production

**Economic Status Post-War:** Devastated by Seven Years' War losses; massive colonial revenue lost with Canada and Louisiana cessions.

**Sources:** Academic economic history sources on 18th-century French economy (specific production volumes require archival research)

### Great Britain (+ Ireland)

**Wool Production (TRADITIONAL STRENGTH):**
- **Yorkshire** (West Riding):
  - Woolen cloth production
  - Halifax, Leeds centers
- **East Anglia** (Norfolk, Suffolk):
  - Worsted fabrics
  - Norwich major center
- **Cotswolds** (Gloucestershire):
  - Traditional wool production
  - Stroud scarlet cloth famous

**Coal Mining (INDUSTRIAL REVOLUTION FUEL):**
- **Newcastle and Durham** (Northeast):
  - Major coalfields supplying London via coastal shipping ("sea coal")
  - Driving early steam engine development (Newcomen engines for mine drainage)
- **Midlands Coalfields**:
  - Staffordshire, Derbyshire, Nottinghamshire
  - Supporting early iron industry

**Iron Production (EARLY INDUSTRIAL):**
- **Coalbrookdale** (Shropshire):
  - Abraham Darby's coke-smelting process (1709)
  - **Iron Bridge** construction 1779 (later, but ironworks active 1763)
- **Forest of Dean** (Gloucestershire): Charcoal iron
- **Sheffield** (Yorkshire): Steel and cutlery production
- Weald (Sussex/Kent): Declining charcoal iron industry

**Atlantic Trade Ports:**
- **Liverpool** (Lancashire):
  - Major slave trade port (surpassing Bristol)
  - Atlantic triangular trade
  - Cotton imports from colonies (pre-cotton boom)
- **Bristol** (Somerset):
  - Slave trade, sugar, tobacco imports
  - Atlantic trade and re-export hub
- **Glasgow** (Scotland):
  - Tobacco re-export to Europe
  - Emerging colonial trade center

**Other Production:**
- **London**: Financial center, global trade hub, varied manufacturing
- **Birmingham**: Metal goods, "toy trade" (small metal items)
- **Manchester**: Beginning textile production (cotton boom comes later)

**Economic Status Post-War:** Dominant global maritime power; Bengal revenues beginning to flow; national debt increased but manageable.

**Sources:** Academic economic history sources on 18th-century British economy

---

### Spain

**American Silver Routes (CROWN REVENUE BACKBONE):**
- **Cádiz** (Andalusia):
  - Primary port for American trade after 1717 (replaced Seville)
  - Silver fleets (*flotas*) from New Spain (Mexico), Peru
  - Casa de Contratación moved here from Seville
  - Trade monopoly enforcement
- **Seville** (Andalusia):
  - Former monopoly port (until 1717)
  - Still significant trade and administrative center
  - Royal mint

**Colonial Trade Monopoly System:**
- **Casa de Contratación**: Colonial trade regulatory body
- Fleet system (*flota* and *galeones*) maintained
- Bourbon Reforms post-1763 would gradually liberalize
  - *comercio libre* reforms 1765-1778
  - Breaking Cádiz monopoly incrementally

**Spanish Production:**
- **Merino Wool** (Castile): High-quality wool, major export
- **Silk** (Granada, Valencia, Murcia): Silk production
- **Wine and Olive Oil**: Mediterranean agricultural exports
- Limited manufacturing compared to Britain/France

**Economic Status Post-War:** Devastated by Seven Years' War; lost Florida, but gained Louisiana. Charles III initiating Bourbon Reforms to strengthen economy and colonial administration.

**Sources:** Academic sources on Spanish colonial trade and Bourbon economic reforms

---

### Portugal

**Brazilian Gold (PEAK ERA 1700-1760):**
- **Minas Gerais** gold and diamond mines
- Shipped via **Lisbon**
- Gold output declining by 1763 from peak decades
- Crown took royal fifth (*quinto*)

**Brazilian Sugar:**
- **Bahia** (Salvador region): Major sugar production
- **Pernambuco** (Recife region): Sugar plantations
- Shipped to Lisbon for re-export
- Facing Caribbean competition by 1763

**Port Wine Trade:**
- **Douro Valley** (northern Portugal): Wine production
- **Porto**: Export hub for fortified port wine
- **Methuen Treaty (1703)** with Britain:
  - Portuguese wines entered Britain at preferential tariffs
  - Britain gained access to Portuguese markets
  - Created asymmetric dependency: Britain industrial goods, Portugal wine/colonial goods

**Other Economic Features:**
- **Lisbon**: Capital, primary trade hub
- Rebuilt after 1755 earthquake (Pombaline Lisbon)
- Re-export economy: Brazilian gold/sugar → European markets

**Economic Status Post-War:** Allied with Britain during Seven Years' War; economy dependent on Brazilian gold (declining) and sugar, plus port wine exports.

**Sources:** Academic sources on Portuguese colonial economy; Methuen Treaty analysis

---

### Dutch Republic

**VOC Spice Trade (GLOBAL REACH):**
- **Dutch East India Company** (Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie - VOC)
  - Nutmeg monopoly from Moluccas (Banda Islands conquest 1609-1621)
  - Spices from East Indies: cloves, mace, cinnamon from Ceylon
  - Trade network spanning Batavia (Jakarta), Malacca, Cape of Good Hope, Ceylon
  - Asian intra-regional trade (Japan-China-Southeast Asia)
- Decline beginning: English competition, corruption, declining profits

**Textiles:**
- **Leiden**: Major textile production center
  - Woolen cloth (say, laken)
  - Peak production mid-17th century; declining by 1763
- **Haarlem**: Linen bleaching and production
- Facing British competition by 1763

**Financial Markets:**
- **Amsterdam**: Global financial center
  - **Bank of Amsterdam** (*Amsterdamsche Wisselbank*, founded 1609): Europe's premier exchange bank
  - Stock exchange (*beurs*): World's first formal stock exchange (1602)
  - International credit and trade finance
  - Beginning relative decline vs. London

**Other Economic Features:**
- **Shipbuilding**: Major industry (Zaanstreek region)
- **Herring Fisheries**: North Sea fishing fleets
- **Carrying Trade**: Dutch ships transporting goods between other nations
- Decline accelerating after 1730

**Economic Status Post-War:** Neutral in Seven Years' War but in secular decline; VOC profits declining; British competition rising; still significant financial center.

**Sources:** Academic sources on Dutch Golden Age decline, VOC economic history, Amsterdam financial markets

## 6. SUMMARY: 1763 vs. 1815 BASELINE

### Political Differences:
- **1763**: Post-Seven Years' War; British dominance just established; Spain/France defeated
- **1815**: Post-Napoleonic Wars; Congress of Vienna settlement; different power balance

### Territorial Differences:
- **United States**: Does not exist in 1763 (independence 1776-1783)
- **Louisiana**: Spanish possession 1763 (French 1800-1803, US 1803+)
- **Florida**: British 1763-1783 (Spanish before/after)
- **Latin America**: All colonial in 1763 (independent by 1825)
- **Haiti**: French Saint-Domingue in 1763 (independent 1804)
- **Netherlands**: Dutch Republic 1763 (Kingdom of Netherlands 1815)
- **Austrian Netherlands**: Habsburg 1763 (Dutch Kingdom 1815)

### Economic Differences:
- **Pre-Industrial** in 1763: Cottage industries, workshops, early coal/iron
- **Industrial Revolution underway** by 1815: Factories, mechanization, steam power widespread in Britain
- **Colonial trade patterns**: Different after American independence and Napoleonic Wars
- **Dutch decline**: More advanced by 1815; British/French dominance complete

### Governmental Differences:
- **Absolute monarchies** more intact in 1763
- **1815**: Post-French Revolution; constitutional experiments; different governmental forms
- **Colonial administration**: Bourbon Reforms just beginning 1763; fully implemented by 1815
