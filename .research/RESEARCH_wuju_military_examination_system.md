- **Third Class (第三甲)**: "賜同武進士出身"

**Sources**:

- English Wikipedia: Imperial examination, Xiucai

### Prestige Relative to Civil Examinations

The military examination was **consistently regarded as inferior** to the civil examination. Multiple sources confirm:

- "較為不受重視" (relatively less esteemed) — ZH Wikipedia 武舉
- "亦低於文科出身的進士" (ranked below civil jinshi) — ZH Wikipedia 科舉  
- "Military degrees were seen as inferior to their civil counterpart" — EN Wikipedia Imperial examination
- "Never had a significant impact on the Chinese officer corps" and "neglected by the government" — EN Wikipedia Xiucai
- "Time and again abolished and restored" (時而廢止，時而恢復) across dynasties — ZH Wikipedia 科舉

**Assessment**: **WELL-ESTABLISHED** across multiple sources. The lower prestige is a consistent historical pattern.

## 2. THE EXAM CONTENT

### Qing Dynasty Three-Level Structure

#### A. Provincial Exam (鄉試 xiangshi) - Held October

**Two divisions**:

1. **External Test (外場 waichang)** - Primary emphasis:
   - **馬射/馬箭** (mǎshè/mǎjiàn) - Mounted archery
   - **步射/步箭** (bùshè/bùjiàn) - Standing/foot archery  
   - **技勇** (jìyǒng) - Skills and strength tests

2. **Internal Test (內場 neichang)** - Secondary:
   - **默寫武經** (mòxiě wǔjīng) - Writing out military classics from memory

**Note**: "以外場為主" (external test was emphasized) — ZH Wikipedia 武進士

**Source**: Chinese Wikipedia 武進士 article

#### B. Metropolitan Exam (會試 huishi) - Held September

- **Same format** as provincial exam
- **Candidates**: 武舉人 (wǔjǔrén) from provincial exams
- **Passers became**: 武貢士 (wǔgòngshì), qualifying them for the palace exam

**Source**: Chinese Wikipedia 武進士 article

#### C. Palace Exam (殿試 dianshi) - 3 days at Hall of Supreme Harmony (太和殿)

Personally administered by the emperor.

- **Day 1**: 馬步箭 (mǎbùjiàn) - Mounted and standing archery
- **Day 2**: 弓刀石 (gōngdāoshí) - Bow, sword, stone
  - **拉硬弓/開弓** (lā yìnggōng/kāigōng) - Drawing the hard bow
  - **舞刀** (wǔdāo) - Wielding the sword/saber  
  - **掇石/舉石** (duōshí/jǔshí) - Lifting stone weights
- **Day 3**: Presentation and awaiting imperial ranking

**Sources**: Chinese Wikipedia 武進士, 武狀元 articles

### Equipment Standards (Qing Dynasty)

According to Chinese Wikipedia 武舉 article:

- **Bows**: Divided into **8-力, 10-力, 12-力** (lì = draw weight units)
- **Sabers**: **80斤, 100斤, 120斤** (jīn = catties)  
  - Approximate modern weight: 48kg, 60kg, 72kg (using 0.6 kg/catty)
- **Stones**: **200斤, 250斤, 300斤**  
  - Approximate modern weight: 120kg, 150kg, 180kg

**Note**: The exact conversion of 力 (lì) bow draw weight to modern pounds is **uncertain** from these sources. Historical Qing catty (斤) is approximately 596.8 grams.

**Source**: Chinese Wikipedia 武舉 article — **DOCUMENTED** but conversions approximate

### Written Portion: The Seven Military Classics (武經七書)

Required reading material from Song dynasty onward, described as "武學科舉必讀的兵學教材" (required reading for military exams).

1. **孫子兵法** (Sūnzǐ Bīngfǎ) - *Sun Tzu's Art of War* by Sun Wu, 13 chapters
2. **吳子兵法** (Wúzǐ Bīngfǎ) - *Wuzi* by Wu Qi (paired with Sun Tzu as "孫吳兵法")
3. **六韜** (Liù Tāo) - *Six Secret Teachings* attributed to Jiang Shang, six volumes
4. **三略** (Sān Lüè) - *Three Strategies* attributed to Huang Shigong  
5. **司馬法** (Sīmǎ Fǎ) - *Methods of the Sima* by general Tian Rangju of Qi
6. **尉繚子** (Wèiliáozǐ) - *Weiliaozi* by Wei Liao
7. **唐太宗李衛公問對** (Táng Tàizōng Lǐ Wèigōng Wèn Duì) - *Questions and Replies* attributed to Li Jing of the Tang dynasty

**Format**: **策論** (cèlùn) - Policy essays on military strategy and tactics

**Source**: Chinese Wikipedia 武經七書 article — **WELL-ESTABLISHED**

### Evolution Across Dynasties

- **Tang Dynasty**: Pure physical tests — weightlifting, mounted/foot archery, cavalry spear technique; candidates needed imposing physique (軀幹雄偉，有將帥之姿 "physique befitting a general")

- **Song Dynasty**: Added strategic knowledge — "副之策略" — tested knowledge of military classics like Sun Tzu's *Art of War*

- **Ming Dynasty**: **"先之以謀略，次之以武藝"** (strategy first, martial arts second) — failing the written strategy test **disqualified** candidates from the martial portion

- **Qing Dynasty**: **Reversed the Ming approach** — "改為先試馬步射...之後比併力氣" (changed to test mounted/foot archery first, then strength) — physical tests came first, only passers took the written exam

**Sources**: Chinese Wikipedia 武舉, 武狀元, 武舉人 articles — **WELL-ESTABLISHED**

## 3. APPOINTMENT & CAREER

### Imperial Bodyguard Appointments (侍衛)

System established in **Yongzheng 5 (1727)** specifically for Han military examination graduates.

| Rank Achieved in Palace Exam | Appointment Title | Rank |
|-------------------------------|-------------------|------|
| **武狀元** (1st place) | **一等侍衛** (yīděng shìwèi) | First-rank bodyguard |
| **武榜眼、武探花** (2nd/3rd place) | **二等侍衛** (èrděng shìwèi) | Second-rank bodyguard |
| **二甲 top 10** (Second class, top 10) | **三等侍衛** (sānděng shìwèi) | Third-rank bodyguard |
| **三甲** (Third class) | **藍翎侍衛** (lánlíng shìwèi) | Blue-feather bodyguard |

**Important Restriction**: Han bodyguards could serve in the **outer guard** but were generally **barred from the inner palace**. 

- **Only one exception** in Qing history: **Yang Fang** (楊芳) during Jiaqing era, specially appointed as 御前侍衛 (imperial presence bodyguard)

**Source**: Chinese Wikipedia 侍衛處 article — **WELL-ESTABLISHED**, specific dates and titles confirmed

### Green Standard Army (綠營) Career Path

Sources confirm that military examination graduates entered the **Green Standard Army officer corps**:

**Characteristics of Green Standard**:
- "Originally intended to be a **professional force**" (vs. hereditary Banner system)
- Comprised "largely of **Han Chinese**"  
- Officers were "a mix of Banner and Green Standard officers"
- Maintained Ming-era ranks after Qing conquest

**However**: Specific appointment ranks for **武舉人** or lower **武進士** grades into Green Standard positions are **NOT FOUND** in accessible English-language sources.

**Sources**: 
- English Wikipedia: Green Standard Army, Military of the Qing dynasty
- Assessment: Green Standard connection **CONFIRMED**, specific ranks **UNCERTAIN**

### Banner vs. Han Chinese Recruitment Pathways

#### Eight Banners (八旗) - Manchu/Mongol/Han Bannermen

- **Hereditary system**: Banner membership passed by birth, not examination
- Officers promoted through **hereditary titles** and merit in banner service  
- Upper Three Banners (直屬皇帝) under direct imperial command
- Lower Five Banners commanded by hereditary princes
- John Ross (missionary) observed: Bannermen's claim "to be military men is based on their descent rather than on their skill in arms" and pay granted "because of their fathers' prowess"

**Sources**: English Wikipedia Eight Banners, Military of the Qing dynasty

#### Military Examination (武舉) - Primarily Han Chinese

- The wǔjǔ was **primarily a Han Chinese pathway** into military officership
- Opened access to imperial bodyguard positions (from 1727) and Green Standard officer corps
- English Wikipedia states "selected Han Chinese who had passed the **highest level of martial exams**" were eligible for emperor's personal bodyguards

**Sources**: English Wikipedia Military of the Qing dynasty

**Assessment**: The Banner (hereditary) vs. examination-based (Han Chinese) distinction is **WELL-ESTABLISHED**. The wǔjǔ was the primary avenue for Han Chinese martial talent, while Manchu/Mongol officers advanced through banner hereditary and merit systems.

### Career Success Rate

Multiple sources note that **few high-ranking generals emerged from the military examination system**:

- "中國歷史上由武舉出身的高級將領不多" (Few high-ranking generals in Chinese history came from wǔjǔ background) — ZH Wikipedia 武舉人
- **Notable exceptions**: 
  - **Guo Ziyi** (郭子儀) - Tang general
  - **Qi Jiguang** (戚繼光) - Ming general, a 武舉人 from Jiajing era

**Source**: Chinese Wikipedia 武舉人 article — **DOCUMENTED**

## 4. PRESTIGE & DECLINE

### Social Status

As documented in Section 1, military degrees consistently ranked below civil equivalents. Additional context:

- The system "was neglected by the government"
- "Time and again abolished and restored" (時而廢止，時而恢復) across dynasties  
- Even the top **武狀元** ranked socially below a civil **進士**
- The system "never had a significant impact on the Chinese officer corps"

**Sources**: EN Wikipedia Imperial examination, Xiucai; ZH Wikipedia 科舉, 武舉

### Late Qing Reform Proposals

#### Shen Baozhen (沈葆楨)

- **1872 and 1874**: Proposed reforming military examinations to include **mathematics**
- **Throne rejected**: "the pool of candidates were still too small to justify an examination"
- Also proposed **abolition** of military examinations based on "obsolete weaponry such as archery"
- Suggested direct appointment of Tongwen Guan mathematics graduates to Zongli Yamen as if they were exam graduates

**Source**: English Wikipedia Self-Strengthening Movement — **WELL-ESTABLISHED**

#### Other Reformers

- **Li Hongzhang** (李鴻章) - **1864**: Suggested incorporating Western technology into Imperial examinations
- **Feng Guifen** (馮桂芬) - **1861**: Proposed mathematics and science  
- **Ding Richang** (丁日昌) - **1867**: Similar proposals for modernization

**Source**: English Wikipedia Self-Strengthening Movement

### Final Abolition

**Date**: **1901** (光緒27年 Guangxu 27)

**Decree**: "清廷在1901年廢除武舉考試" (Qing court abolished wǔjǔ examination in 1901)

**Context**: 
- Part of **New Policies** (新政/庚子新政) reforms after Boxer Rebellion
- Military training system modernized: "abolished the test of traditional Chinese Martial and founded the training system for officers"
- **1903**: Central Training Command established to coordinate national army training
- New officer training followed modern academy model, largely based on **Japanese and German systems**

**Sources**: 
- Chinese Wikipedia 庚子新政 article (1901 date)
- English Wikipedia New Policies article (1901 date, context)

**Civil Examination Abolition** (for comparison):
- **September 2, 1905**: Yuan Shikai (袁世凱) and Zhang Zhidong (張之洞) memorialized to "立停科舉" (immediately stop imperial examinations)
- Formalized 1906

**Sources**: English Wikipedia Yuan Shikai; Chinese Wikipedia 張之洞

**Assessment**: The **1901 abolition date** for military examinations is **WELL-ESTABLISHED**. This was **4 years before** the civil examination abolition in 1905.

### Rationale for Abolition

Sources consistently cite:

1. **Obsolete content**: Testing archery, swords, and stone-lifting was militarily irrelevant in the age of firearms and modern warfare
2. **Modernization need**: Modern army required academy-trained officers with technical/scientific education  
3. **Western/Japanese models**: New military academies established following German and Japanese training systems

**Assessment**: The obsolescence rationale is **WELL-ESTABLISHED** and repeatedly documented.

### Last Military Zhuangyuan

Chinese Wikipedia 武舉 article lists military zhuangyuan "從順治三年(1646年)至光緒二十四(1898年)年間...合共109人" (from Shunzhi 3, 1646 to Guangxu 24, 1898, totaling **109 people** across Qing dynasty).

**Last recorded**: **1898** (光緒二十四年) - 3 years before formal abolition in 1901

**Source**: Chinese Wikipedia 武舉 article — **DOCUMENTED**

## 5. PERIODISATION - 1763 (HIGH QING / QIANLONG REIGN)

### Status in 1763: Fully Operational

The military examination was a **fully operational standing institution** in 1763.

**Evidence**:

1. **Qianlong reign produced the most military zhuangyuan**: "乾隆朝...武狀元最多，為二十七人" (Qianlong reign had the most military zhuangyuan, **27 people** — more than any other Qing reign)

2. **Specific 1763 military zhuangyuan identified**: 
   - **Year**: 乾隆二十八年癸未科 (1763年) - Qianlong 28, guiwei examination year
   - **Name**: **De Hao** (德灝)
   - **Background**: 滿洲正黃 (Manchu, Plain Yellow Banner)

**Source**: Chinese Wikipedia 武舉 article — **CONFIRMED FOR 1763**

**Assessment**: The system was **demonstrably functioning** in 1763 with a documented graduate that year.

### Qianlong-Era Reforms

**NOT FOUND** in accessible sources. 

- The Chinese Wikipedia article on Qianlong does not mention military examination reforms
- The Kangxi period (earlier) had "four reforms" according to academic paper by Zhao Fuxue et al. (2020) identified in Google Scholar, but full text was not accessible

**Assessment**: Whether Qianlong made specific reforms to the wǔjǔ system is **UNCERTAIN**.

### Broader Military Context (1763)

- **Qianlong's Ten Great Campaigns** (十全武功): Military expansion ongoing during this period
- **Peak of Qing power**: High Qing period, military actively engaged in frontier campaigns  
- **Banner system dominant**: Eight Banners remained the elite force
- **Green Standard operational**: Large Han Chinese regular army existed alongside banners

The military examination operated in this context, providing a pathway for Han Chinese martial talent into the officer corps, though the system was already considered less prestigious than civil examinations and less militarily significant than Banner promotion pathways.

**Conclusion**: The wǔjǔ was a **fully functioning standing institution in 1763**, with documented graduates that year. It operated throughout the Qianlong reign, which produced more military zhuangyuan than any other Qing reign period (27 total).

## RECOMMENDED GAME IMPLEMENTATION NOTES

For a game set in **1763** (Qianlong 28):

1. **System is operational**: Wǔjǔ should be a functioning mechanic, at peak activity
   - Qianlong reign produced most military zhuangyuan (27 total)
   - 1763 specifically had a documented graduate (De Hao, Manchu Plain Yellow Banner)

2. **Degree ladder**: All levels appropriate for implementation
   - 武秀才/武生 (wuxiucai/wusheng) - Military licentiate
   - 武舉人 (wujuren) - Military provincial graduate  
   - 武進士 (wujinshi) - Military presented scholar
   - 武狀元 (wuzhuangyuan) - Military optimus

3. **Exam content**: Physical prowess + military classics knowledge
   - **Physical tests**: Mounted archery, standing archery, drawing hard bow (8-12 力), wielding saber (80-120 斤), lifting stones (200-300 斤)
   - **Written test**: Seven Military Classics (武經七書), policy essays (策論)
   - **Qing structure**: Physical first, only passers take written (reversed from Ming)

4. **Appointments**: 
   - **Top graduates** (武狀元/武榜眼/武探花) → Imperial bodyguards (一等/二等侍衛)
   - **Lower 武進士** → Third-rank bodyguards or Green Standard positions
   - **Note**: Han bodyguards restricted to outer guard, rarely inner palace

5. **Prestige mechanic**: Should rank **consistently below** civil examination equivalents
   - A 武進士 has lower social status than a civil 進士
   - A 武狀元 ranks below a civil 狀元
   - "Neglected by government" — fewer career prospects

6. **Ethnic dimension**: **Primarily a Han Chinese pathway**
   - Manchu/Mongol officers should advance through **Banner hereditary/merit system**
   - Eight Banners membership is hereditary, not examination-based
   - Wǔjǔ opens Han Chinese access to imperial bodyguard (from 1727) and Green Standard officer corps

7. **Historical endpoint**: System abolished **1901**, well outside 1763-era gameplay window

8. **Notable**: 1763 graduate was **Manchu** (De Hao), showing system was ethnically mixed but primarily served Han Chinese pathway to military officership
