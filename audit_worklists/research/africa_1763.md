# Sub-Saharan Africa Political Geography: 1763 Ground Truth

## WEST AFRICA

### Asante Empire / Ashanti (STRONG TAG)
- **Native name**: Asanteman (Twi)
- **Capital**: Kumasi
- **Key cities**: Kumasi, Dwaben, Bekwai, Mampong, Nsuta, Juaben, Kokofu, Asumegya
- **Territorial extent**: Central Ghana forest zone; by 1763 controlled Akan goldfields and was expanding toward coast (Fante wars ongoing)
- **Centralization**: STRONG - hierarchical confederacy under Asantehene with Golden Stool ideology, standing army, tribute system
- **Justification**: Founded c.1701 under Osei Tutu, by 1763 the Asante were the dominant military power in the Gold Coast interior, having defeated Denkyira (1701) and expanded through confederation and conquest. McCaskie (1995) *State and Society in Pre-Colonial Asante* details the mature bureaucratic structure by mid-18th century.
- **Source**: McCaskie, T.C. (1995). *State and Society in Pre-Colonial Asante*. Cambridge University Press.

### Oyo Empire (STRONG TAG)
- **Native name**: Ilú Ọ̀yọ́ (Yoruba)
- **Capital**: Oyo-Ile (Old Oyo)
- **Key cities**: Oyo-Ile, Ibarapa, Igboho, Shaki, Kisi, Iseyin, tributary over Egba/Egbado
- **Territorial extent**: Southwest Nigeria (Yorubaland) extending to tributary control over Dahomey (1730-1823), reaching to coast through Egbado corridor
- **Centralization**: STRONG - imperial structure with Alaafin (divine king), Oyo Mesi council, cavalry-based military, tributary network
- **Justification**: At peak power in mid-18th century (c.1650-1780s). Law (1977) notes Oyo's empire reached maximum extent by 1760s, controlling trade routes from interior to coast and extracting tribute from multiple subject states including Dahomey after 1730 conquest.
- **Source**: Law, Robin (1977). *The Oyo Empire c.1600-c.1836*. Oxford: Clarendon Press.

### Kingdom of Dahomey (STRONG TAG)
- **Native name**: Danhomè (Fon)
- **Capital**: Abomey
- **Key cities**: Abomey, Allada, Whydah/Ouidah (conquered 1727), Cana, Zagnanado
- **Territorial extent**: Southern Benin, between Volta and Ouémé rivers; tributary to Oyo from 1730
- **Centralization**: STRONG - highly centralized absolute monarchy with standing army (including female soldiers/Mino), slave-trade monopoly, administrative provinces
- **Justification**: Under King Tegbesu (r.1740-1774) Dahomey was a centralized slaving state paying annual tribute to Oyo but maintaining internal autonomy. Bay (1998) describes the palace-centered bureaucracy and military mobilization capacity.
- **Source**: Bay, Edna G. (1998). *Wives of the Leopard: Gender, Politics, and Culture in the Kingdom of Dahomey*. University of Virginia Press.

### Kingdom of Benin (STRONG TAG)
- **Native name**: Ẹdo (Edo language)
- **Capital**: Benin City (Ẹdo)
- **Key cities**: Benin City, Ughoton (port), Udo, Urhonigbe
- **Territorial extent**: South-central Nigeria (Edo State region), reduced from 16th-17th century peak but still significant
- **Centralization**: STRONG - divine kingship (Oba) with titled palace chiefs, guilds, tributary system; declined from peak but still centralized
- **Justification**: By 1763 Benin had contracted from its 16th-17th century apogee but remained a centralized kingdom with elaborate palace administration. Ryder (1969) notes continued royal authority and trading networks despite territorial losses to Yoruba and Igbo expansion.
- **Source**: Ryder, A.F.C. (1969). *Benin and the Europeans 1485-1897*. London: Longmans.

### Hausa City-States (MULTIPLE TAGS or DECENTRALIZED TAG GROUP)
- **Native name**: Kasashen Hausa (Hausa)
- **Major states**: 
  - **Kano** (capital: Kano) - major commercial/manufacturing center
  - **Katsina** (capital: Katsina) - Saharan trade terminus
  - **Zazzau/Zaria** (capital: Zaria) - slave-raiding state
  - **Gobir** (capital: Alkalawa) - northern defensive frontier
  - **Kebbi** (capital: Birnin Kebbi)
  - **Kano** (capital: Kano)
- **Territorial extent**: Northern Nigeria, southern Niger; each city-state controlled surrounding hinterland
- **Centralization**: DECENTRALIZED - seven (or more) independent walled city-states, no unified authority; each had centralized urban government but were rivals, not confederated
- **Justification**: The Hausa Bakwai (seven true Hausa states) were politically fragmented in 1763, frequently warring with each other. Each city-state had a Sarki (king) and hierarchical administration but no overarching unity until 1804 Fulani jihad. Smith (1997) emphasizes their commercial sophistication but political disunity.
- **Source**: Smith, M.G. (1997). *Government in Kano 1350-1950*. Westview Press.
- **Recommendation**: Either separate tags for major states (Kano, Katsina, Zaria, Gobir) OR a single "Hausa" culture with multiple small tags

### Kanem-Bornu Empire (STRONG TAG)
- **Native name**: Sultanate of Bornu (Kanuri language region)
- **Capital**: Ngazargamu (until 1808)
- **Key cities**: Ngazargamu, Dikwa, Monguno, Maiduguri region
- **Territorial extent**: Northeast Nigeria, southern Chad, northern Cameroon; controlled Saharan trade routes to Tripoli
- **Centralization**: STRONG - Sayfawa dynasty sultanate with feudal provincial structure, Islamic administration, tributary network
- **Justification**: Under Mai (king) Ali Dunama (r.1747-1792), Bornu maintained a centralized Islamic state with diplomatic ties to Ottoman Tripoli and control of central Sahel trade. Brenner (1973) details the continuing strength of Sayfawa administration despite earlier Tuareg/Fulani pressures.
- **Source**: Brenner, Louis (1973). *The Shehus of Kukawa: A History of the al-Kanemi Dynasty of Bornu*. Oxford: Clarendon Press.

### Bamana Empire of Segu (STRONG TAG)
- **Native name**: Segu fanga (Bambara/Bamana)
- **Capital**: Ségou (on Niger River)
- **Key cities**: Ségou, Nyamina, Markala
- **Territorial extent**: Central Mali along middle Niger River; controlled Niger riverine trade between Timbuktu and upper Niger
- **Centralization**: STRONG - military kingdom with ton-dyon (slave army), expansionist under Ngolo Diara dynasty (1766 coup just after our period)
- **Justification**: Founded c.1712 by Mamari Kulubali, by 1763 Segu was a major military state controlling the Niger bend. The state relied on a standing slave army and extracted tribute from surrounding regions. Roberts (1987) describes the militaristic centralized structure.
- **Source**: Roberts, Richard (1987). *Warriors, Merchants, and Slaves: The State and the Economy in the Middle Niger Valley, 1700-1914*. Stanford University Press.

### Kingdom of Kong (STRONG TAG)
- **Native name**: Kong Empire (Dyula/Juula trading diaspora)
- **Capital**: Kong (northern Ivory Coast)
- **Key cities**: Kong, Bobo-Dioulasso, Bondoukou, Dabakala
- **Territorial extent**: Northern Ivory Coast, southern Burkina Faso; commercial empire linking forest and savanna trade
- **Centralization**: STRONG - Dyula merchant-warrior state, Islamic, military expansion under Sekou Ouattara dynasty (founded 1710)
- **Justification**: Established c.1710 by Sekou Ouattara, Kong was at its expansionist peak in mid-18th century, controlling caravan routes and imposing political authority over wide region. Wilks (1982) documents Kong's commercial-military hybrid structure.
- **Source**: Wilks, Ivor (1982). "Wangara, Akan and Portuguese in the Fifteenth and Sixteenth Centuries" in *Journal of African History* 23(3-4).

### Futa Jallon (STRONG TAG)
- **Native name**: Fuuta Jaloo (Pular/Fulfulde)
- **Capital**: Timbo (alternating with Fugumba)
- **Key cities**: Timbo, Fugumba, Labé, Mamou
- **Territorial extent**: Guinea highlands (Fouta Djallon massif), Guinea-Conakry
- **Centralization**: STRONG - theocratic imamate, confederal structure with alternating Almamys (from Soriya and Alphaya clans), standing army, Islamic scholarly tradition
- **Justification**: Founded c.1725-1750s after Fulani jihad against local Jallonke/Susu rulers. By 1763 the imamate was consolidating control under dual Almamy rotating system. Barry (1998) details the theocratic confederation structure.
- **Source**: Barry, Boubacar (1998). *Senegambia and the Atlantic Slave Trade*. Cambridge University Press.

### Mossi Kingdoms (MULTIPLE TAGS - DECENTRALIZED)
- **Native name**: Moogo (Moore language) - plural Mooose
- **Major kingdoms**: 
  - **Ouagadougou** (Wogodogo) - most powerful
  - **Yatenga** (capital: Ouahigouya)
  - **Tenkodogo** - ancestral center
  - **Fada N'gourma**
- **Territorial extent**: Central Burkina Faso
- **Centralization**: DECENTRALIZED - multiple independent kingdoms sharing Mossi culture and origin myths, each with centralized monarchy (Mogho Naba) but politically separate
- **Justification**: The Mossi kingdoms successfully resisted both Songhai and later jihadist conquest, maintaining independence and traditional religion. Each kingdom had hierarchical structure but they were rivals, not unified. Izard (1985) documents their political separation despite cultural unity.
- **Source**: Izard, Michel (1985). *Gens du pouvoir, gens de la terre: Les institutions politiques de l'ancien royaume du Yatenga*. Cambridge University Press.
- **Recommendation**: Separate tags for Ouagadougou and Yatenga at minimum

### Songhai Successor States (NO TAG - FRAGMENTED)
- **Context**: After Moroccan conquest (1591), Songhai Empire fragmented into:
  - Dendi kingdom (small rump state on Niger, capital Lulami) - possible minor tag
  - Arma rulers of Timbuktu (Moroccan military caste, much degraded by 1763)
  - Tuareg confederations controlling desert
- **Centralization**: FRAGMENTED - no effective centralized authority in former Songhai territory by 1763; Timbuktu under weak Arma, Dendi a minor chiefdom
- **Justification**: Kaba (1981) describes the complete dissolution of Songhai imperial structures by mid-18th century, with Timbuktu controlled by competing Tuareg and Arma factions and only Dendi maintaining nominal continuity.
- **Source**: Kaba, Lansine (1981). "Archers, Musketeers and Mosquitoes: The Moroccan Invasion of the Sudan and the Songhay Resistance" in *Journal of African History* 22(4).
- **Recommendation**: Dendi as minor tag if desired; Timbuktu under Tuareg or as free city

### Tuareg Confederations (DECENTRALIZED CULTURE - POSSIBLE TAG)
- **Groups**: Kel Adagh, Kel Ayr, Kel Geres, Iwillimiden, Kel Tadmakkat
- **Territorial extent**: Saharan Mali, northern Niger, southern Algeria
- **Centralization**: STATELESS/DECENTRALIZED - segmentary lineage society organized in drums (Ettebel) under amenukals (chiefs), loose confederal structures, primarily pastoralist with some oasis control
- **Justification**: Tuareg society was organized in competing confederations without permanent centralized state structures. Raiding, pastoralism, and control of trans-Saharan routes. Norris (1975) emphasizes the fluid, non-centralized nature of Tuareg political organization.
- **Source**: Norris, H.T. (1975). *The Tuaregs: Their Islamic Legacy and its Diffusion in the Sahel*. Warminster: Aris and Phillips.
- **Recommendation**: Culture-only, possibly with regional minor tags for major drums if game mechanics require

## CENTRAL AFRICA

### Kingdom of Kongo (DECLINING TAG - FRAGMENTED)
- **Native name**: Wene wa Kongo, Kongo dya Ntotila (Kikongo)
- **Capital**: São Salvador (Mbanza Kongo) - but much contested/ruined after civil wars
- **Key cities**: Mbanza Kongo, Soyo (independent by 1763), Mbata, Mbamba
- **Territorial extent**: Northern Angola, western DRC, Congo-Brazzaville; GREATLY reduced from 16th-century extent
- **Centralization**: FRAGMENTED - after 1665 Battle of Mbwila and ensuing civil wars, Kongo fractured into competing provinces; by 1763 the Manikongo (king) had minimal authority outside capital region, with Soyo effectively independent
- **Justification**: Post-1665 civil wars devastated centralized authority. Thornton (1983) describes Kongo by mid-18th century as a weak confederation of rival provinces with the king lacking coercive power. Soyo was an independent duchy.
- **Source**: Thornton, John K. (1983). *The Kingdom of Kongo: Civil War and Transition, 1641-1718*. Madison: University of Wisconsin Press.
- **Recommendation**: Weak tag for Mbanza Kongo heartland; Soyo as separate minor tag

### Kingdom of Loango (STRONG TAG)
- **Native name**: Lwããngu (Vili)
- **Capital**: Loango (Buali)
- **Key cities**: Loango, Cabinda, Malemba, Kilongo
- **Territorial extent**: Coastal Congo-Brazzaville and Cabinda enclave, major slave-trading port
- **Centralization**: STRONG - centralized monarchy with Ma-Loango (king), commercial state focused on Atlantic trade
- **Justification**: Independent from Kongo since 16th century, Loango was a major slaving state in 18th century with centralized royal administration controlling coastal trade. Martin (1972) documents the kingdom's political structure and commercial role.
- **Source**: Martin, Phyllis M. (1972). *The External Trade of the Loango Coast, 1576-1870*. Oxford: Clarendon Press.

### Lunda Empire (STRONG TAG)
- **Native name**: Mwant Yaav (title of emperor, Lunda language)
- **Capital**: Musumba (in Katanga region)
- **Key cities**: Musumba, Kazembe (subordinate kingdom), various royal settlements
- **Territorial extent**: Southeastern DRC (Katanga), northern Zambia, eastern Angola; vast empire through tributary system
- **Centralization**: STRONG - expansive empire under Mwant Yaav, feudal tributary network with positional succession system (perpetual kinship), integration through marriage alliances
- **Justification**: Founded mid-17th century, by 1760s Lunda was at its maximum territorial extent under Mwant Yaav Muteba. The empire used a sophisticated tributary system with officials holding hereditary titles. Miller (1976) details the imperial structure.
- **Source**: Miller, Joseph C. (1976). *Kings and Kinsmen: Early Mbundu States in Angola*. Oxford: Clarendon Press. [Also Vansina, Jan (1966). *Kingdoms of the Savanna*.]

### Luba Empire (STRONG TAG)
- **Native name**: Luba/Baluba kingdom
- **Capital**: Mwibele (in central Katanga)
- **Key cities**: Mwibele and various royal capitals (capital moved with rulers)
- **Territorial extent**: Central-southern DRC (Katanga/Shaba region)
- **Centralization**: STRONG - sacred kingship (Mulopwe) with spiritual authority, bambudye secret society providing political cohesion, tributary network
- **Justification**: Luba empire provided the political model for many Central African states (including Lunda). By 1763 under Mulopwe Kadilo, the empire controlled trade routes and extracted tribute. Reefe (1981) describes the centralized cult of kingship.
- **Source**: Reefe, Thomas Q. (1981). *The Rainbow and the Kings: A History of the Luba Empire to 1891*. Berkeley: University of California Press.

### Kuba Kingdom (STRONG TAG)
- **Native name**: Bushong/Kuba (Bushoong language)
- **Capital**: Nsheng (Mushenge)
- **Key cities**: Nsheng and dispersed royal villages
- **Territorial extent**: South-central DRC (Kasai region), between Sankuru and Kasai rivers
- **Centralization**: STRONG - centralized under nyim (divine king), elaborate court culture, artistic production, age-grade system
- **Justification**: Under Nyim Kot aMbweeky I (r.1710-1785), Kuba was a stable centralized kingdom famous for its art and textile production. Vansina (1978) documents the sophisticated political system and cultural fluorescence.
- **Source**: Vansina, Jan (1978). *The Children of Woot: A History of the Kuba Peoples*. Madison: University of Wisconsin Press.

### Kazembe Kingdom (STRONG TAG - or Lunda Sub-tag)
- **Native name**: Mwata Kazembe (Lunda offshoot)
- **Capital**: Mwansabombwe (Luapula valley)
- **Key cities**: Mwansabombwe and mobile royal settlements
- **Territorial extent**: Luapula valley (northern Zambia/southeastern DRC), controlling copper and ivory trade to East African coast
- **Centralization**: STRONG - Lunda offshoot kingdom, semi-autonomous under Mwata Kazembe, trading connection to Swahili coast
- **Justification**: Founded c.1740 as eastern Lunda expansion; by 1763 under Kazembe III Lukwesa Ilunga, functionally independent but acknowledging Mwant Yaav. Controlled lucrative trade to Kilwa. Cunnison (1959) details the political structure.
- **Source**: Cunnison, Ian (1959). *The Luapula Peoples of Northern Rhodesia*. Manchester University Press.

### Matamba Kingdom (DECLINING TAG)
- **Native name**: Matamba (Kimbundu)
- **Capital**: Santa Maria de Matamba
- **Key cities**: Capital and tribute centers
- **Territorial extent**: Eastern Angola, between Kwango and Cuanza rivers
- **Centralization**: WEAK by 1763 - once powerful under Queen Nzinga (d.1663), by mid-18th century declining under Portuguese pressure
- **Justification**: Matamba resisted Portuguese conquest in 17th century but by 1763 was contracting. Still nominally independent but weakened. Miller (1976) notes the kingdom's gradual subordination to Portuguese sphere.
- **Source**: Miller, Joseph C. (1976). *Kings and Kinsmen: Early Mbundu States in Angola*. Oxford: Clarendon Press.

### Ndongo (NO TAG - PORTUGUESE-CONTROLLED)
- **Context**: The Kingdom of Ndongo (Angola) was conquered by Portuguese by 1670s and integrated into colonial system as Angola. By 1763, no independent Ndongo state existed.

## EAST AFRICA / HORN

### Ethiopian Empire (STRONG TAG)
- **Native name**: Ityop'iya/Abyssinia, Mängəstä Ityop'ya (Amharic/Ge'ez)
- **Capital**: Gondar (Gondarine period capital, 1636-1855)
- **Key cities**: Gondar, Axum, Adwa, Debre Birhan, Harar (Muslim city-state, contested)
- **Territorial extent**: Ethiopian highlands (modern Ethiopia except Somali-inhabited lowlands), nominal suzerainty over parts of Eritrea
- **Centralization**: DECLINING - Gondarine period showing fractures; by 1763 beginning of "Era of Princes" (Zemene Mesafint, 1769-1855) with regional warlords undermining imperial authority, but still nominally centralized under Emperor (Nəgusä nägäst)
- **Justification**: Emperor Iyoas I (r.1755-1769) nominally ruled but provincial rases (princes) were increasingly autonomous. Crummey (2000) describes Gondar's waning authority but continued existence of imperial structure until 1769 deposition.
- **Source**: Crummey, Donald (2000). *Land and Society in the Christian Kingdom of Ethiopia: From the Thirteenth to the Twentieth Century*. University of Illinois Press.

### Funj Sultanate of Sennar (DECLINING TAG)
- **Native name**: Saltanat al-Funj (Arabic), Sennar
- **Capital**: Sennar (on Blue Nile)
- **Key cities**: Sennar, Arbaji, Halfaya
- **Territorial extent**: Central Sudan (Blue Nile region, between Ethiopian highlands and White Nile), roughly modern Sudan south of Egyptian border
- **Centralization**: DECLINING - Islamic sultanate, but by 1763 power shifting from Sultan to Hamaj regents (who seized effective power 1761/62); still structured state but in transition
- **Justification**: Founded c.1504, Funj was a major Islamic state in 18th century. The Hamaj coup of 1761-62 created a regency that dominated sultans thereafter, but state structures remained. Spaulding (1985) details the political transition.
- **Source**: Spaulding, Jay (1985). *The Heroic Age in Sinnar*. East Lansing: Michigan State University Press.

### Sultanate of Darfur (STRONG TAG)
- **Native name**: Dār Fūr (Arabic: "Land of the Fur")
- **Capital**: El Fasher (al-Fashir)
- **Key cities**: El Fasher, Kebkabiya, Nyala
- **Territorial extent**: Western Sudan (Darfur region), controlling Saharan trade routes
- **Centralization**: STRONG - centralized Islamic sultanate under Keira dynasty, elaborate administrative hierarchy, controlled desert trade
- **Justification**: Under Sultan Muhammad Tayrab (r.1752-1785), Darfur was at its peak, defeating Funj and controlling trade to Egypt. O'Fahey (1980) documents the centralized fiscal-military state.
- **Source**: O'Fahey, R.S. (1980). *State and Society in Dar Fur*. London: C. Hurst & Co.

### Swahili Coast City-States (MULTIPLE TAGS - DECENTRALIZED under Omani suzerainty)
- **Major cities**: 
  - **Kilwa** (Kilwa Kisiwani) - declining from medieval peak
  - **Zanzibar** - rising under Omani Busaidi control (formal Omani capital moved here 1832, but significant by 1763)
  - **Mombasa** (Fort Jesus, Omani-controlled since 1698)
  - **Pate** (Pate Island) - independent sultanate
  - **Lamu** - city-state
  - **Malindi**, **Bagamoyo**
- **Territorial extent**: East African coast from southern Somalia to northern Mozambique
- **Centralization**: DECENTRALIZED - individual city-states, most under loose Omani (Busaidi dynasty) suzerainty after Portuguese expulsion (1698-1720s), but Pate and others maintained independence or nominal autonomy
- **Justification**: After Portuguese ouster, the Omani Busaidi dynasty claimed suzerainty over Swahili coast, but local sultans retained autonomy. Pate and other cities resisted full Omani control until 19th century. Sheriff (1987) describes the decentralized structure and Omani overlay.
- **Source**: Sheriff, Abdul (1987). *Slaves, Spices and Ivory in Zanzibar*. London: James Currey.
- **Recommendation**: Major cities as separate small tags (Kilwa, Mombasa, Pate, Lamu, Zanzibar); or under Omani tag with autonomy mechanics

### Kingdom of Buganda (STRONG TAG)
- **Native name**: Buganda (Luganda)
- **Capital**: Rubaga (Kampala area - capitals moved)
- **Key cities**: Royal capitals rotated; court at Mengo, Nabulagala, etc.
- **Territorial extent**: North shore of Lake Victoria, modern central Uganda
- **Centralization**: STRONG - highly centralized under Kabaka (king), hierarchical chieftaincy, standing army, expansionist
- **Justification**: Under Kabaka Mawanda (r.c.1740-1741), Buganda consolidated power in Great Lakes region. Highly organized central administration with appointed chiefs. Kiwanuka (1972) describes the centralized bureaucratic structure.
- **Source**: Kiwanuka, M.S.M. Semakula (1972). *A History of Buganda: From the Foundation of the Kingdom to 1900*. New York: Africana Publishing.

### Kingdom of Bunyoro (STRONG TAG - rival to Buganda)
- **Native name**: Bunyoro-Kitara (Runyoro)
- **Capital**: Masindi (region - capitals moved)
- **Key cities**: Royal settlements in Masindi region
- **Territorial extent**: Western Uganda, east of Lake Albert; former dominant power in Great Lakes, declining relative to Buganda by 1763
- **Centralization**: STRONG - Babiito dynasty, centralized under Omukama (king), but losing territory to Buganda
- **Justification**: Bunyoro was the historically dominant kingdom but by mid-18th century was on defensive against Buganda expansion. Still centralized but contracting. Uzoigwe (1973) details the rivalry and Bunyoro's decline from hegemonic position.
- **Source**: Uzoigwe, G.N. (1973). "Pre-Colonial Markets in Bunyoro-Kitara" in *Comparative Studies in Society and History* 14(4).

### Kingdom of Rwanda (STRONG TAG)
- **Native name**: Rwanda (Kinyarwanda)
- **Capital**: Royal court itinerant (later fixed at Nyanza)
- **Key cities**: Royal centers moved with Mwami
- **Territorial extent**: Modern Rwanda, beginning expansion under Mwami Cyilima II Rujugira (r.c.1756-1765+)
- **Centralization**: STRONG - highly centralized under Mwami (king), hierarchical ubuhake cattle clientage system, ritual kingship, standing army, expansion underway
- **Justification**: Under Mwami Rujugira, Rwanda was centralizing and expanding, developing sophisticated administrative system. Vansina (2004) documents the mid-18th century as a period of state consolidation and military expansion.
- **Source**: Vansina, Jan (2004). *Antecedents to Modern Rwanda: The Nyiginya Kingdom*. Madison: University of Wisconsin Press.

### Kingdom of Burundi (STRONG TAG)
- **Native name**: Burundi (Kirundi)
- **Capital**: Royal court itinerant (Muramvya region)
- **Key cities**: Royal centers in central plateau
- **Territorial extent**: Modern Burundi
- **Centralization**: STRONG - centralized under Mwami (king), ganwa princely class, cattle-based hierarchy similar to Rwanda
- **Justification**: Burundi was a centralized kingdom with elaborate court culture. The Mwami exercised authority through ganwa (royal princes) and a system of chiefs. Trouwborst (1962) describes the centralized political structure.
- **Source**: Trouwborst, A.A. (1962). "Le Burundi" in *Les anciens royaumes de la zone interlacustre méridionale*. Tervuren: Musée Royal de l'Afrique Centrale.

### Nilotic Pastoralist Peoples (NO TAG - STATELESS)
- **Groups**: Dinka, Nuer, Shilluk, Maasai, Turkana, Samburu, etc.
- **Territorial extent**: South Sudan, northern Uganda, northern Kenya, northern Tanzania
- **Centralization**: STATELESS - segmentary lineage societies, age-set systems, no permanent centralized authority (exception: Shilluk had reth/king but limited authority)
- **Justification**: Nilotic pastoralists were organized through kinship and age-grades without state structures. Even the Shilluk reth had ritual authority but limited coercive power. Johnson (1994) describes the acephalous organization.
- **Source**: Johnson, Douglas H. (1994). *Nuer Prophets: A History of Prophecy from the Upper Nile in the Nineteenth and Twentieth Centuries*. Oxford: Clarendon Press.
- **Recommendation**: CULTURE-ONLY (use existing or add: dinka, nuer, shilluk, maasai cultures)
- **Note**: Maasai in 1763 were still migrating south through Rift Valley, not yet in full southern Kenya/Tanzania range

## SOUTHERN AFRICA

### Rozvi Empire / Mutapa State (DECLINING TAG)
- **Native name**: Rozvi (Shona language family)
- **Capital**: Danangombe (Great Zimbabwe long abandoned)
- **Key cities**: Danangombe, Khami, various zimbabwes (stone enclosures)
- **Territorial extent**: Zimbabwe plateau, southern Mozambique inland; successor to Mutapa/Great Zimbabwe tradition
- **Centralization**: DECLINING - Rozvi confederacy dominated plateau after conquering Mutapa Empire c.1684; by 1763 still dominant but showing fractures, tributary system weakening
- **Justification**: The Rozvi Changamire dynasty ruled a confederacy of Shona chiefdoms but by mid-18th century was declining from 17th-century peak. Beach (1980) describes the loose tributary structure and incipient fragmentation.
- **Source**: Beach, D.N. (1980). *The Shona and Zimbabwe, 900-1850*. New York: Africana Publishing.
- **Recommendation**: TAG for Rozvi heartland, but weak/declining

### Merina Kingdom (Madagascar) (STRONG TAG - emerging)
- **Native name**: Fanjakan'Imerina (Malagasy)
- **Capital**: Antananarivo (captured 1794 - before that, Ambohimanga)
- **Key cities**: Ambohimanga (capital until 1790s), Antananarivo (captured 1794)
- **Territorial extent**: Central highlands of Madagascar; in 1763 still consolidating, major expansion post-1787 under Andrianampoinimerina
- **Centralization**: EMERGING STRONG - By 1763 under Andrianjafy (r.1770-1787), Merina was a small highland kingdom beginning consolidation; MAJOR expansion only after 1787
- **Justification**: In 1763 Merina was one of several competing highland kingdoms (Imerina itself divided until 1794). Consolidation and imperial expansion came later under Andrianampoinimerina (r.1787-1810) and Radama I. Campbell (2005) notes 1763 predates unification.
- **Source**: Campbell, Gwyn (2005). *An Economic History of Imperial Madagascar, 1750-1895*. Cambridge University Press.
- **Recommendation**: Small TAG for Merina heartland (Ambohimanga); or merge with other highland groups for 1763

### Sakalava Kingdoms (Madagascar) (DECENTRALIZED - MULTIPLE TAGS)
- **Native name**: Sakalava (Malagasy)
- **Major kingdoms**: 
  - **Menabe** (southern west coast, capital Morondava area)
  - **Boina** (northern west coast, capital Majunga/Mahajanga)
- **Territorial extent**: Western Madagascar coast
- **Centralization**: DECENTRALIZED - Two major kingdoms (Menabe and Boina), independent of each other, each with centralized monarchy but no unity; slave-raiding and cattle-based economies
- **Justification**: Menabe and Boina were separate Sakalava kingdoms, rivals not confederates. Both engaged in slave trading and raids on highlands. Vérin (1986) emphasizes their political separation.
- **Source**: Vérin, Pierre (1986). *The History of Civilisation in North Madagascar*. Rotterdam: A.A. Balkema.
- **Recommendation**: Separate tags for Menabe and Boina

### Betsimisaraka Confederation (Madagascar) (DECENTRALIZED TAG)
- **Native name**: Betsimisaraka (Malagasy: "the many inseparable")
- **Capital**: Fenerive (Fenoarivo Atsinanana)
- **Key cities**: Fenerive, Tamatave (Toamasina)
- **Territorial extent**: Eastern Madagascar coast
- **Centralization**: DECENTRALIZED - Loose confederation under Ratsimilaho (d.1751) and successors; fragmented after his death but nominal unity maintained
- **Justification**: Founded early 18th century by Ratsimilaho, uniting coastal groups. After his death (1751) the confederation weakened but continued in name. Vérin (1986) describes the weak confederal structure.
- **Source**: Vérin, Pierre (1986). *The History of Civilisation in North Madagascar*. Rotterdam: A.A. Balkema.
- **Recommendation**: Weak TAG or culture-only with regional minor tags

### Zulu / Nguni Chiefdoms (NO TAG - STATELESS/PRE-STATE)
- **Native name**: Zulu is ONE clan among many Nguni-speaking groups
- **Groups**: Hundreds of small chiefdoms including Zulu, Mthethwa, Ndwandwe, Qwabe, etc.
- **Territorial extent**: KwaZulu-Natal region, South Africa
- **Centralization**: STATELESS - Small-scale chiefdoms, no centralized authority; Shaka's Zulu kingdom NOT founded until 1816 after Mfecane
- **Justification**: In 1763 the Nguni-speaking peoples were organized in hundreds of small independent chiefdoms of a few thousand people each. The Zulu were one minor clan. The Zulu kingdom arose only after 1816 under Shaka. Hamilton (1998) emphasizes pre-Shakan fragmentation.
- **Source**: Hamilton, Carolyn (ed.) (1998). *The Mfecane Aftermath: Reconstructive Debates in Southern African History*. Johannesburg: Witwatersrand University Press.
- **Recommendation**: CULTURE-ONLY (existing "zulu" culture name is anachronistic for 1763 but acceptable as umbrella term for Nguni)

### Xhosa Chiefdoms (DECENTRALIZED - CULTURE or MINOR TAGS)
- **Native name**: AmaXhosa (Xhosa)
- **Major chiefdoms**: Gcaleka, Rharhabe, Ndlambe (subdivisions emerging from 1770s conflicts)
- **Territorial extent**: Eastern Cape, South Africa; in 1763 the Xhosa were expanding southwest, just beginning contact with expanding Cape Colony
- **Centralization**: DECENTRALIZED - Multiple independent chiefdoms with common culture; paramount chiefs had limited authority over subordinate chiefs
- **Justification**: Xhosa society was organized in chiefdoms with segmentary structure. The major split into Gcaleka and Rharhabe occurred mid-18th century. Peires (1981) describes the decentralized political organization and autonomy of local chiefs.
- **Source**: Peires, J.B. (1981). *The House of Phalo: A History of the Xhosa People in the Days of Their Independence*. Berkeley: University of California Press.
- **Recommendation**: CULTURE-ONLY with possible minor tags for major chiefdoms (Gcaleka, Rharhabe) if mechanics require; or single weak TAG

### Sotho-Tswana Peoples (NO TAG - STATELESS/DECENTRALIZED)
- **Groups**: Basotho, Batswana and many sub-groups (Kwena, Ngwato, Kgatla, Hurutshe, Rolong, etc.)
- **Territorial extent**: Interior highveld/plateau (Free State, Lesotho, Botswana)
- **Centralization**: STATELESS/DECENTRALIZED - Dispersed agricultural villages and small chiefdoms, no large-scale political authority; major kingdoms (Lesotho, Tswana states) emerged only in 19th century
- **Justification**: In 1763 Sotho-Tswana peoples lived in small-scale chiefdoms and villages without centralized states. The kingdom of Lesotho was founded only in 1820s under Moshoeshoe I. Legassick (1969) emphasizes pre-state organization.
- **Source**: Legassick, Martin (1969). "The Sotho-Tswana Peoples before 1800" in *African Societies in Southern Africa*, ed. Leonard Thompson. London: Heinemann.
- **Recommendation**: CULTURE-ONLY

### Khoikhoi Peoples (NO TAG - STATELESS, largely displaced)
- **Native name**: Khoikhoi (pastoralists) vs San (hunter-gatherers) - both "Khoisan" language family
- **Groups**: Cape Khoikhoi (largely displaced by 1763), Nama (further north)
- **Territorial extent**: Western South Africa, Namibia; by 1763 Cape Khoikhoi largely dispossessed by Dutch expansion, Nama further north still independent
- **Centralization**: STATELESS - Pastoralist bands, no centralized authority; decimated by disease and Dutch expansion in Cape
- **Justification**: Khoikhoi societies were organized in small pastoral groups without political hierarchy. By 1763 the Cape Khoikhoi were largely reduced to labor for colonists or had fled inland. Elphick (1985) documents the collapse under colonial pressure.
- **Source**: Elphick, Richard (1985). *Khoikhoi and the Founding of White South Africa*. Johannesburg: Ravan Press.
- **Recommendation**: CULTURE-ONLY (not politically organized for tags)

### San Peoples (NO TAG - STATELESS)
- **Native name**: San (many groups: !Kung, Ju/'hoansi, G/wi, etc.)
- **Territorial extent**: Scattered across southern Africa (Kalahari, Namibia, Botswana, South Africa)
- **Centralization**: STATELESS - Hunter-gatherer bands, egalitarian, no political centralization
- **Justification**: San peoples lived in small mobile foraging bands with egalitarian organization. Lee (1979) describes the lack of political hierarchy.
- **Source**: Lee, Richard B. (1979). *The !Kung San: Men, Women and Work in a Foraging Society*. Cambridge University Press.
- **Recommendation**: CULTURE-ONLY

### Dutch Cape Colony (COLONIAL TAG - VOC)
- **Name**: Cape Colony (Kaapkolonie), controlled by Dutch East India Company (VOC)
- **Capital**: Cape Town (Kaapstad)
- **Key cities**: Cape Town, Stellenbosch, Swellendam, Paarl
- **Territorial extent**: Small area around Cape Town, expanding inland; by 1763 roughly 100-150km radius from Cape Town, borders still fluid
- **Centralization**: COLONIAL ADMINISTRATION - VOC company rule, governor appointed from Batavia/Amsterdam, centralized but small settler population
- **Justification**: Founded 1652, by 1763 the Cape was a VOC refreshment station with expanding settler (Boer) frontier. Giliomee (2003) describes the VOC administration and frontier expansion.
- **Source**: Giliomee, Hermann (2003). *The Afrikaners: Biography of a People*. University of Virginia Press.
- **Recommendation**: TAG for Cape Colony (use Dutch tag or separate VOC Cape tag)

## ADDITIONAL REGIONS / GROUPS

### Portuguese Angola (COLONIAL TAG)
- **Capital**: Luanda (São Paulo de Luanda)
- **Key cities**: Luanda, Benguela, Massangano
- **Territorial extent**: Coastal Angola and interior penetration along slave-trade routes; by 1763 limited to coast and major fortified interior posts
- **Centralization**: COLONIAL - Portuguese crown colony, but interior control tenuous, reliant on African intermediaries
- **Justification**: Portuguese presence in Angola dated to 1575; by 1763 controlled coast and some interior but most inland was under African polities tributary or trading with Portuguese.
- **Source**: Miller, Joseph C. (1988). *Way of Death: Merchant Capitalism and the Angolan Slave Trade, 1730-1830*. Madison: University of Wisconsin Press.

### Portuguese Mozambique (COLONIAL TAG)
- **Capital**: Mozambique Island (Ilha de Moçambique)
- **Key cities**: Mozambique Island, Sofala, Inhambane, Sena, Tete (Zambezi valley prazos)
- **Territorial extent**: Mozambique coast and Zambezi valley prazo system; interior under African rulers
- **Centralization**: COLONIAL - Portuguese crown colony, but prazos (land grants) were semi-autonomous Afro-Portuguese fiefdoms; weak inland authority
- **Justification**: Portuguese had coastal forts and Zambezi valley prazos (estates) but interior was controlled by African states. Newitt (1995) describes the weak colonial overlay.
- **Source**: Newitt, Malyn (1995). *A History of Mozambique*. Indiana University Press.

### Omani Empire / Busaidi Dynasty (TAG - but centered in Arabia)
- **Capital**: Muscat (Oman)
- **East African holdings**: Nominal suzerainty over Swahili coast (Mombasa, Zanzibar, etc.) since c.1698, but control loose until 19th century
- **Justification**: The Busaidi dynasty of Oman claimed suzerainty over East African coast after expelling Portuguese, but local autonomy remained high until direct rule consolidated in 19th century.
- **Recommendation**: If Omani tag exists for Arabia, extend to Swahili coast cities as tributaries; or represent Swahili cities as independent/autonomous
