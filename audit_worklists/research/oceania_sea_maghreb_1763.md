## A. OCEANIA + AUSTRALASIA

### AUSTRALIA — CULTURE-ONLY (NO TAGS)

**Status**: Entirely pre-contact Aboriginal peoples with NO state-level political organization.

**Major cultural/linguistic zones**:
1. **Pama-Nyungan zone** (90% of continent): Covers most of Australia except the northwest/north
2. **Non-Pama-Nyungan zone** (Arnhem Land, Kimberley, Daly River region)
3. **Tasmanian/Palawa** (Tasmania): Isolated since ~10,000 BP

**Classification**: CULTURE/POPULATION ONLY — absolutely no polity tags.

**Justification**: Aboriginal societies were organized as bands/clans with fluid, overlapping territories defined by kinship and Dreaming tracks, not bounded states. No hierarchical political authority, taxation, or territorial sovereignty in the sense required for a Paradox tag.

**Sources**: 
- Mulvaney & Kamminga (1999), *Prehistory of Australia*, Allen & Unwin
- Gammage (2011), *The Biggest Estate on Earth*, Allen & Unwin
- Sutton & Walshe (2021), *Farmers or Hunter-gatherers?*, Melbourne University Press

---

### NEW GUINEA — CULTURE-ONLY (NO TAGS)

**Status**: Pre-contact Papuan and Austronesian-speaking peoples; no centralized polities in 1763.

**Cultural zones**:
- **Papuan highlands** (Trans-New Guinea phylum): Stateless agricultural societies
- **Austronesian coastal** (Malayo-Polynesian): Maritime/trading groups, no state formation
- **Papuan Tip, Sepik, other lowland groups**

**Classification**: CULTURE/POPULATION ONLY — no tags.

**Justification**: Big-man political systems with achieved leadership, village-level autonomy, no hereditary hierarchy or territorial sovereignty. States emerge only under colonial pressure (post-1880s).

**Sources**:
- Denoon & Snowden (1981), *A Time to Plant and a Time to Uproot*, Institute of Papua New Guinea Studies
- Swadling (1996), *Plumes from Paradise*, Papua New Guinea National Museum

### NEW ZEALAND — TAG-WORTHY MĀORI IWI

**Status**: Māori iwi (tribes) with hierarchical organization, defined rohe (territories), rangatira (chiefs), and pā (fortifications). These qualify as tags.

#### Major Iwi (1763)

| Iwi | Rohe (Territory) | Key Locations | Capital/Primary Pā | Justification |
|-----|------------------|---------------|---------------------|---------------|
| **Ngāpuhi** | Northland (Te Tai Tokerau) | Bay of Islands, Kaikohe | Kaikohe area | Largest North Island iwi; hierarchical rangatira, extensive rohe, warfare capacity |
| **Ngāti Whātua** | Tāmaki Makaurau (Auckland) | Auckland isthmus, Kaipara | Maungawhau/Mount Eden | Controlled strategic isthmus; organized warfare against Ngāpuhi |
| **Waikato-Tainui** | Waikato region | Waikato River basin | Ngāruawāhia area | Descended from Tainui waka; large federated iwi with paramount chiefs |
| **Ngāti Maniapoto** | King Country | Inland Waikato/Waitomo | Te Kūiti area | Strong territorial control; later Kīngitanga stronghold |
| **Ngāti Tūwharetoa** | Taupō, central North Island | Lake Taupō, Tongariro | Taupō pā | Controlled volcanic plateau; distinct rohe |
| **Te Arawa** | Rotorua, Bay of Plenty | Rotorua lakes, Maketū | Ōhinemutu (Rotorua) | Confederation from Te Arawa waka; geothermal resources |
| **Ngāti Porou** | East Coast/East Cape | Gisborne to East Cape | Whakatō area | Largest East Coast iwi; strong maritime tradition |
| **Ngāti Kahungunu** | Hawke's Bay, Wairarapa | Napier, Hastings, Wairoa | Heretaunga (Hastings) | Second-largest iwi; extensive coastline control |
| **Te Āti Awa / Taranaki** | Taranaki region | New Plymouth, Mt Taranaki | Pukerangiora pā | Confederation; Taranaki wars base |
| **Ngāi Tahu** | Most of South Island (Te Waipounamu) | Canterbury, Otago, Westland | Kaiapoi (Canterbury) | Dominated South Island by 1763 after conquest of Kāti Māmoe/Waitaha |
| **Kāti Māmoe** | South Island (diminished) | Central/southern SI | (Absorbed by Ngāi Tahu) | Earlier South Island iwi, largely conquered by Ngāi Tahu by 1760s |

**Classification**: TAG-WORTHY — each major iwi should have a tag.

**Justification**: Māori iwi exhibited: (1) hereditary rangatira with mana/authority; (2) defined territorial boundaries (rohe) defended by warfare; (3) hierarchical hapu (sub-tribe) structure; (4) fortified settlements (pā); (5) resource management systems (rāhui). This meets the threshold for Paradox polity tags.

**Sources**:
- Anderson (2016), *Tangata Whenua: A History*, Bridget Williams Books
- Ballara (1998), *Iwi: The Dynamics of Māori Tribal Organisation*, Victoria University Press
- Crosby (1999), *The Musket Wars*, Penguin NZ

## B. MARITIME SOUTHEAST ASIA

### DUTCH VOC TERRITORIES

**Entity**: Verenigde Oostindische Compagnie (VOC) — Dutch East India Company

**Extent (1763)**:
- **Java**: Batavia (Jakarta) + coastal outposts (Semarang, Surabaya), interior Javanese kingdoms as vassals
- **Maluku** (Moluccas): Ambon, Ternate (vassal sultanate), Tidore (VOC-controlled), Banda Islands
- **Sulawesi**: Makassar (Fort Rotterdam) and coastal factories
- **Malay Peninsula**: Malacca
- **Sumatra**: Posts at Padang, Palembang (indirect control)
- **Borneo**: Coastal factories (Banjarmasin treaty relations)

**Capital**: Batavia (Jakarta)

**Classification**: TAG (VOC or NED as overlord)

**Justification**: VOC exercised sovereignty through fortified factories, treaties, and tribute extraction. Governor-General in Batavia commanded military and diplomatic authority independent of Amsterdam in practice.

**Sources**:
- Ricklefs (2008), *A History of Modern Indonesia*, Palgrave
- Niemeijer (2013), *Batavia: A Colonial Metropolis*, KITLV Press

### JAVANESE STATES (POST-1755 TREATY OF GIYANTI)

#### Surakarta Sunanate (Kasunanan Surakarta)

**Extent**: Central Java (eastern half of Mataram partition)
**Capital**: Surakarta (Solo)
**Ruler**: Susuhunan Pakubuwono III (r. 1749-1788)
**Classification**: TAG (VOC vassal)

**Justification**: Hereditary sultanate with court bureaucracy, army, and territorial control. VOC vassal but retained internal sovereignty.

**Source**: Ricklefs (1974), *Jogjakarta under Sultan Mangkubumi*, Oxford

---

#### Yogyakarta Sultanate (Kasultanan Yogyakarta)

**Extent**: Central Java (western half of Mataram partition)
**Capital**: Yogyakarta
**Ruler**: Sultan Hamengkubuwono I (r. 1755-1792)
**Classification**: TAG (VOC vassal)

**Justification**: Co-equal with Surakarta after Giyanti; full court apparatus, independent military.

**Source**: Ricklefs (1974)

---

#### Sultanate of Banten

**Extent**: Western Java tip (Banten region)
**Capital**: Banten (Serang)
**Ruler**: Sultan Syarifuddin (r. 1753-1777)
**Classification**: TAG (VOC vassal, limited autonomy)

**Justification**: Ancient sultanate reduced by VOC but still sovereign over Banten territory until 1808 annexation.

**Source**: Guillot & Nurhakim (1990), *Histoire de Banten*, Archipel

### SPANISH PHILIPPINES

**Entity**: Capitanía General de Filipinas

**Extent (1763)**:
- **Luzon**: Manila (capital), Cavite, Ilocos, Pampanga, Laguna — Spanish control
- **Visayas**: Cebu, Panay, Samar, Leyte — Christian lowlands under Spanish authority
- **Mindanao**: Northern coast (Spanish forts), but interior + south controlled by Muslim sultanates

**Capital**: Manila

**Classification**: TAG (SPA or separate PHI tag as Spanish colony)

**Justification**: Direct Spanish colonial administration under a Governor-General, audiencia, Catholic church hierarchy. Note: British occupation Sept 1762–May 1764, returned by Treaty of Paris (Feb 1763 but evacuation delayed).

**Sources**:
- Cushner (1971), *Spain in the Philippines*, Institute of Philippine Culture
- Tracy (1995), *The Rise of Merchant Empires*, Cambridge

### MUSLIM SULTANATES

#### Sultanate of Aceh

**Endonym**: كسلطانن أچيه (Kasultanan Acèh)

**Extent**: Northern Sumatra (Aceh province + northern coast)
**Capital**: Banda Aceh (Kutaraja)
**Ruler**: Sultan Alauddin Johan Syah (r. 1760-1781)
**Classification**: TAG (independent)

**Justification**: Powerful sultanate with navy, trade monopolies (pepper), and Islamic scholarly tradition. Fought Dutch for centuries; never fully subjugated until 20th century.

**Source**: Reid (2005), *An Indonesian Frontier: Acehnese and Other Histories*, KITLV

---

#### Sultanate of Sulu

**Endonym**: Kasultanan Sug (Tausug)

**Extent**: Sulu Archipelago (southern Philippines), northeastern Borneo coast
**Capital**: Jolo
**Ruler**: Sultan Azim ud-Din I (r. 1735-1748, 1763-1774, intermittent)
**Classification**: TAG (independent, resisted Spanish/Dutch)

**Justification**: Maritime sultanate controlling Sulu Sea trade, slave-raiding fleets, diplomatic relations with China and European powers.

**Source**: Warren (1981), *The Sulu Zone*, NUS Press

---

#### Sultanate of Maguindanao

**Endonym**: Kasultanan sa Magindanaw

**Extent**: Southwestern Mindanao (Cotabato basin)
**Capital**: Lamitan / Sultan Kudarat
**Ruler**: Sultan Mahamad Khairuddin (Fakir ud-Din) (r. 1755-1780)
**Classification**: TAG (independent, resisted Spanish)

**Justification**: Riverine sultanate with fort-towns, standing army, and tributary network over non-Muslim upland groups.

**Source**: Majul (1973), *Muslims in the Philippines*, UP Press

---

#### Sultanate of Brunei

**Endonym**: كسلطانن بروني (Kesultanan Brunei)

**Extent**: Northern Borneo coast (Brunei Bay to Sabah), formerly much larger
**Capital**: Kota Batu (Brunei Town)
**Ruler**: Sultan Muhyiddin (r. 1762-1804)
**Classification**: TAG (independent but declining)

**Justification**: Ancient thalassocracy, tributary to China (Ming/Qing); by 1763 in decline but retained sovereignty over northern Borneo.

**Source**: Saunders (1994), *A History of Brunei*, Oxford

---

#### Johor-Riau Sultanate

**Endonym**: كسلطانن جوهر-رياو (Kesultanan Johor-Riau)

**Extent**: Southern Malay Peninsula (Johor), Riau Islands, eastern Sumatra coast
**Capital**: Daik (Lingga Island) after 1722
**Ruler**: Sultan Sulaiman Badrul Alam Shah (r. 1722-1760), then succession disputes; Bugis *Yamtuan Muda* (vice-regent) holds real power
**Classification**: TAG (independent, Bugis-dominated)

**Justification**: Successor to Malacca Sultanate; by 1763 Bugis *Yamtuan Muda* effectively rules, but sultan retains legitimacy.

**Source**: Andaya & Andaya (2001), *A History of Malaysia*, Palgrave

## C. NORTH AFRICA / MAGHREB + EGYPT

### INDEPENDENT MOROCCO

**Entity**: Sultanate of Morocco (Alaouite Dynasty)

**Endonym**: السلطنة الشريفة (al-Sultana al-Sharīfa), المملكة المغربية (al-Mamlaka al-Maghribiyya)

**Extent**: Morocco (Atlantic + Mediterranean coasts, Atlas Mountains, northern Sahara oases)
**Capitals**: Marrakesh (imperial capital), Fes (traditional capital), Meknes (Alaouite seat)
**Ruler**: Sultan Mohammed III (r. 1757-1790)
**Classification**: TAG (fully independent)

**Justification**: Only North African state never conquered by Ottomans; recognized sovereignty, diplomatic relations with Europe, control of trans-Saharan trade.

**Sources**:
- Pennell (2003), *Morocco Since 1830*, NYU Press
- El Mansour (1990), *Morocco in the Reign of Mawlay Sulayman*, MENAS Press

---

### OTTOMAN REGENCY OF ALGIERS

**Entity**: Regency of Algiers (Deylik of Algiers)

**Endonym**: إيالة الجزائر (Eyālet al-Jazāʾir)

**Extent**: Central Maghreb (modern Algeria coast + Tell Atlas)
**Capital**: Algiers
**Ruler**: Dey (elected from Janissary corps) — in 1763: Dey Ali III (r. 1754-1766)
**Classification**: TAG (autonomous Ottoman vassal)

**Justification**: Formally Ottoman eyalet, but Dey elected locally, conducted independent diplomacy (corsair state), remitted nominal tribute to Porte.

**Sources**:
- Ruedy (2005), *Modern Algeria*, Indiana University Press
- Çelıktepe (2021), *The Regency of Algiers*, Ottoman History Podcast

---

### BEYLIK OF TUNIS (HUSAINID DYNASTY)

**Entity**: Regency of Tunis

**Endonym**: إيالة تونس (Eyālet Tūnis)

**Extent**: Eastern Maghreb (modern Tunisia)
**Capital**: Tunis
**Ruler**: Bey (hereditary from Husainid dynasty) — in 1763: Ali Bey II (r. 1759-1782)
**Classification**: TAG (autonomous Ottoman vassal)

**Justification**: Husainid beys ruled hereditarily from 1705; Ottoman suzerainty nominal, independent foreign policy, tribute irregular.

**Sources**:
- Perkins (2014), *A History of Modern Tunisia*, Cambridge
- Brown (1974), *The Tunisia of Ahmad Bey*, Princeton

---

### REGENCY OF TRIPOLI (KARAMANLI DYNASTY)

**Entity**: Regency of Tripoli

**Endonym**: إيالة طرابلس الغرب (Eyālet Ṭarābulus al-Gharb)

**Extent**: Western Libya (Tripolitania + Fezzan oases)
**Capital**: Tripoli
**Ruler**: Pasha (Karamanli dynasty hereditary from 1711) — in 1763: Ali Pasha Karamanli (r. 1754-1793)
**Classification**: TAG (autonomous Ottoman vassal)

**Justification**: Karamanli dynasty ruled as hereditary pashas; corsair economy, trans-Saharan trade, de facto independent.

**Sources**:
- Folayan (1979), *Tripoli during the Reign of Yusuf Pasha Qaramanli*, Ife University Press
- Dearden (1976), *A Nest of Corsairs*, John Murray

---

### OTTOMAN EGYPT (MAMLUK PERIOD)

**Entity**: Egypt Eyalet (Mamluk-controlled)

**Endonym**: إيالة مصر (Eyālet Mıṣr)

**Extent**: Nile Valley + Delta, Sinai, Red Sea coast
**Capital**: Cairo (al-Qāhira)
**Ruler**: Nominally Ottoman governor, de facto Ali Bey al-Kabir (Mamluk, r. 1760-1772 as Sheikh al-Balad)
**Classification**: TAG (Ottoman de jure, Mamluk de facto)

**Justification**: Ottoman conquest 1517, but by 1760s Mamluk beys (especially Ali Bey al-Kabir) ruled autonomously, declared independence 1769, struck coins in own name.

**Sources**:
- Holt (1966), *Egypt and the Fertile Crescent*, Cornell
- Crecelius (1981), *The Roots of Modern Egypt*, Bibliotheca Islamica

### SAHARAN/SAHEL MARGINS — CULTURE-ONLY

**Status**: Largely stateless nomadic confederations and oasis towns

**Major groups**:
- **Tuareg confederations**: Kel Ahaggar (Hoggar Mountains), Kel Ajjer (Tassili), Kel Aïr (Niger), Kel Gres — pastoralist confederations with *amenokal* (chiefs) but no state sovereignty
- **Fezzan oases** (Murzuq, Sabha, Ghat): Under nominal Tripoli control, but Tuareg/Arab tribes autonomous
- **Teda/Toubou** (Tibesti region): Stateless pastoralists
- **Sahrawi/Moorish tribes** (Western Sahara): Hassaniya-speaking, stateless, raiding economy

**Classification**: CULTURE-ONLY (no tags)

**Justification**: Segmentary lineage systems with no centralized authority; *amenokal* and *shaykhs* have prestige but no coercive power or defined territory. Oases may pay tribute to Tripoli/Morocco but are not integrated into state structures.

**Sources**:
- Keenan (2002), *The Lesser Gods of the Sahara*, Frank Cass
- McDougall (2003), "Controlling the Sahara", *Journal of African History*
